# Developer Lies and Whines: A Collection of Humorous Truths

The author takes a tongue-in-cheek approach to discussing common developer excuses, dubbed "lIES" or lies we all keep telling ourselves. The main points of the text highlight four key examples frequently used by developers to excuse themselves from responsibility.

### 1. Underestimating Time Commitment
This often occurs due to underestimation of time needs in task descriptions. The author shares an anecdote about overestimating the completion time for a piece of code, only to realize it would have been better to leave refactoring tasks until the end of a sprint.

### 2. Postponing Refactoring
The tendency to postpone "later" often leads to missed deadlines and ignored issues. The author argues that creating a refactor task even if left deep in the backlog can increase chances of it happening someday.

### 3. Overestimating Estimation Capabilities
Developers frequently underestimate their estimation abilities, only to require more time as work piles up. In most projects, it's better to prioritize and include non-critical tasks during sprint time to avoid "poor planning" concerns.

### 4. Disregarding Test Deliverables
Testing is often put off until the end of project development, even if bugs are likely to appear throughout. The author notes that testing should always be a top priority.

### 5. Assuming Technical Complexity
 Developers may assume their role has limited influence on issues due to technical complexities. This "lie" was evident when mentioning frontend/backend distinctions, with many users finding that the bug occurs frequently in either direction.

### 6. Underestimating Non-Technical Factors
The author attributes project completion speed to non-existent dependencies and assumptions made by teammates and external parties.

### 7. Overreliance on Assumptions
Many developers underestimate reliance on existing code or technology tools, often leading to frustration when they fail to deliver as promised.

The text serves as a lighthearted yet informative expose of common developer excuses, acknowledging that developers are not inherently broken and can benefit from self-reflection and accountability. The humorous tone throughout provides an engaging way to discuss these important topics.
