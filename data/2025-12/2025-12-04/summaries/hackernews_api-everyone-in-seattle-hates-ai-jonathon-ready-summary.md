# Everyone in Seattle Hates AI

January 2025

Seattle is notorious for being a city with a peculiar attitude towards Artificial Intelligence (AI). While other major tech hubs like Silicon Valley or New York City have welcomed the advancements and innovative potential of AI, Seattle's approach has been vastly different.

Recently, I had the opportunity to join Microsoft as an engineer, where Satya Nadella continued his push for "growth mindset" and breaking down silos. This culture fostered experimentation and risk-taking. However, when faced with a sensitive topic like AI, this cultural shift didn't translate into engagement or understanding from most employees.

One former Microsoft colleague shared her concerns about the company's approach towards AI. She mentioned how every time anyone in Seattle discussed AI, there was an instant negative reaction from engineers who felt caught between being part of the solution and those who did not want to be associated with it.

Unfortunately, this perception wasn't limited to me. Every time I presented my AI-powered map, Wanderfugl, to a Seattle engineer, I received similar responses: criticism, hostility, and frustration. The only place where people seemed genuinely interested and curious about AI was abroad – in Tokyo, Paris, or San Francisco.

This contrast raises questions about the culture within Microsoft's operations in Seattle. Is this perception a result of leadership's emphasis on empowerment rather than innovation, or is it indicative of some fundamental issue that needs to be addressed?

Ultimately, I've come to realize that my experiences in Seattle suggest that many people in big tech companies are more concerned with maintaining their reputation and avoiding controversy around AI than truly leveraging its potential.

### The Need for Change

The sudden shift from AI enthusiasm to negativity can be attributed to the recent push into Copilot 365. This has led to a broader conversation about Microsoft's approach towards AI, where many feel it is still underutilized given its capabilities.

It's time for big tech companies, including Seattle-based ones like Microsoft, to reevaluate their priorities and approaches when addressing AI advancements. The enthusiasm and innovation fostered by leaders such as Satya Nadella must be balanced with transparency, understanding, and empathy towards developers who see the potential beyond surface-level concerns about safety or financial viability.

AI itself is not a silver bullet for any company; it requires careful consideration of its implications and benefits to truly succeed. Microsoft may have missed an opportunity to harness Wanderfugl's growth potential in Seattle by letting fear and uncertainty rule the day.
