# AI Drafting My Stories? Over My Dead Body | WIRED

**The Rise of AI-Generated Writing in Journalism**

Journalists have long grappled with the challenges of writing engaging and effective articles. The reliance on large language models (LLMs) for content creation has raised concerns among writers and editors who value the unique touch and nuance that human writing brings.

**The Evolution of AI-Assisted Writing**

Several reports have highlighted writers who use AI to generate substantial portions of their work, including:

*   Alex Heath, a tech reporter at The Wall Street Journal, who uses AI to write drafts based on his notes, interview transcripts, and emails.
*   Nick Lichtenberg, Fortune reporter, who has written 600 stories since July using AI-assisted writing.

These developments have sparked debate about the potential impact on journalism as a whole.

**The Rise of Human-Centric Writing**

Heath's own approach emphasizes that he sees AI as a tool to augment his writing abilities, rather than replace human content creation. This perspective is echoed by other writers who prefer to focus on crafting engaging articles through their own efforts.

He comments, "I see AI as a tool; I don't see it as replacing anything — the only thing that's replaced is drudgery that I didn't want to do anyway."

**Balancing Human and Machine Contributions**

While AI-assisted writing offers convenience and cost savings, it raises important questions about the role of humans in journalism. Efforts like these highlight the need for writers to develop skills that complement human content creation.

Some observers caution against overly reliant on AI tools without acknowledging their potential benefits:

*   "We can't just get rid of all the hard work because of a few people being lazy and using AI."
*   Writers who take ownership of their writing process have more control over how it is perceived by readers.
