# 3 ways to bounce back from sudden team cuts

## Key takeaways
- Sudden cuts remove critical knowledge; capture it before engineers leave.  
- Re‑evaluate capacity versus responsibilities, keep “lights‑on” work, and align with stakeholders on what to cut, delay, or re‑scope.  
- Plan to operate with a smaller team for a period; consider internal staffing options, but remember that hiring and re‑staffing are costly and time‑consuming.

## 1. Take advantage of off‑boarding time
- Use the notice period to document tacit knowledge: project context, niche code areas, tech‑debt items, and maintenance plans.  
- Encourage pair programming or collaborative hand‑offs to spread ownership.  
- Create artifacts such as architecture diagrams, recorded learning sessions, and written notes on refactoring or framework upgrades.

## 2. Rethink deadlines and deliverables
- First assess the new team size and the responsibilities that remain.  
- Prioritize “keep the lights on” work (e.g., platform stability, ops) and be prepared to de‑prioritize or cut feature work.  
- Communicate openly with product partners and peers; use collaborative conversations to weigh trade‑offs and set realistic expectations.  
- Adjust processes and standards as needed (e.g., renegotiate bug‑fix SLA) to match the reduced bandwidth.  
- Aim for a priority list that feels “ambitious but achievable” for the smaller team.

## 3. Don’t forget staffing
- If budget allows, initiate back‑fill requests, but plan for the long hiring and ramp‑up timeline; operate with the reduced team until the role is filled.  
- When back‑filling isn’t possible, explore internal moves: request help from other teams or consider temporary re‑allocation of engineers.  
- Evaluate trade‑offs between projects, choose the highest‑impact initiative, and staff accordingly.  
- Remember that organizational changes are disruptive and may not speed delivery, as new engineers need time to gain context.  

## Closing note
Navigating unexpected layoffs or attrition requires swift knowledge capture, realistic re‑prioritization, and thoughtful staffing decisions to keep the team functional while minimizing disruption.
