# A day in the life of Asia’s fuel crisis

## New Zealand – Kaipara farm (James Brady)

- Dairy farm with 200 cows; diesel powers tractors and machinery, petrol powers quad‑bikes.  
- Uses ~900 L diesel and 200‑300 L petrol per month; diesel price rose to NZ$1.85/L (+$1.03/L).  
- Fuel cost increase adds about NZ$1,252 per month (NZ$15,024 per year).  
- Fertiliser prices up 40 %; overall farm expenses surge.  
- No way to pass costs to customers, forcing delays in investment and maintenance while essential activities continue.

## Vanuatu – Port Vila (Daniel Thomas, bus driver)

- Works 6 am–9 pm, earning about A$120/day.  
- Fuel price rise could cut take‑home pay to ~A$70/day, threatening loan repayments.  
- Buses need air‑conditioning in 30 °C heat, further increasing fuel use.  
- Drivers may have to raise fares, but higher fares risk losing passengers.

## South Korea – Sejong (Kim Hooin, public‑service worker)

- Manages government EV fleet, follows strict fuel‑saving rules, and adopts household measures (shorter showers, daytime phone charging, weekend laundry).  
- Views the 12‑point energy‑saving campaign as a collective effort rooted in Korean frugality and past crisis resilience.

## Thailand – Surin province (Teerayut Ruenrerng, mobile grocery truck owner)

- Daily routine includes multiple trips to scarce diesel stations; sometimes receives only limited fuel vouchers.  
- Diesel shortage and price hikes cut profits by up to 20 %; market prices for produce and packaging also rise.  
- Occasionally receives reduced quantities of ordered goods (e.g., 5 kg chicken instead of 10 kg).  
- Forced to raise prices despite reluctance; stresses compounded by regional conflict (Cambodia‑Thailand) and lack of support.

## Japan – Tokyo (Koichi Matsumoto, sento owner)

- Runs third‑generation sento “Takara‑yu,” historically heated by oil, switched to gas five years ago.
