# Why have there been no arrests from the Epstein files?

## Overview
- More than 3 million pages of DOJ‑released documents detail accusations against Jeffrey Epstein, Ghislaine Maxwell, and numerous high‑profile individuals.
- Since the 2025‑2026 releases, no new U.S. arrests have been made, though some resignations and reputational damage have occurred.
- The U.K. has pursued arrests on corruption‑related grounds, highlighting a contrast with the U.S. approach.

## Legal reasons cited by the DOJ
- Justice Department spokesperson Katie Kenlein stated there is “no credible evidence” that Epstein’s network extended criminal activity beyond Epstein and Maxwell.
- Prosecutors require “rock‑solid evidence” to meet the burden of proof; without it, charges cannot be filed.
- Redaction errors in the released files have further complicated the evidentiary picture.

## Expert explanations for the lack of prosecutions
- **Burden of proof** – Barbara McQuade (U. Michigan Law) emphasizes the need to prove guilt “beyond a reasonable doubt,” which is especially hard in sex‑trafficking cases.
- **Witness credibility** – Paul Butler (Georgetown Law) notes problems with victim credibility and forensic gaps can prevent a case from moving forward.
- **Victim reluctance** – Diane Goldstein (Law Enforcement Action Partnership) explains victims may fear retaliation, distrust police, or avoid the trauma of testifying, limiting essential testimony.
- **Investigative hurdles** – Goldstein adds that without a legitimate police investigation, criminal filings cannot proceed.
- **Conspiracy charges** – Jessica Roth (Cardozo Law) points out that FBI identifiers of “co‑conspirators” are not formal accusations, making conspiracy prosecutions difficult.
- **Legal framework differences** – McQuade observes the U.S. lacks a single federal statute equivalent to the U.K.’s “misconduct in public office” charge, limiting avenues for corruption‑related prosecution.

## Comparison with the United Kingdom
- British authorities arrested former Prince Andrew (now Andrew Mountbatten‑Windsor) and ex‑ambassador Peter Mandelson on suspicion of misconduct in public office, focusing on alleged sharing of confidential government information.
- The U.K. investigations rely on statutes such as the Official Secrets Act and public‑corruption laws, which have no direct federal counterpart in the United States.

## Additional challenges specific to sex‑crime cases
- Victim statements are crucial for establishing timelines and elements of sexual assault, yet intimidation and threats can silence witnesses.
- Defense attorneys often aggressively attack victim credibility, further discouraging testimony.
- Prosecutors must follow strict policies and procedures; any lapse can derail a potential case.

## Summary of key points
- The primary reason for no new U.S. arrests is the lack of sufficient, admissible evidence to meet the high criminal standard of proof.
- Legal, procedural, and evidentiary obstacles—especially in sex‑trafficking cases—make prosecution of Epstein’s associates extremely challenging.
- Differences in legal statutes and investigative focus explain why the U.K. has pursued corruption‑related arrests while the U.S. has remained largely silent on additional criminal charges.
