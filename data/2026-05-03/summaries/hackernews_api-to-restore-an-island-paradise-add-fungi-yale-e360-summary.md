# To Restore an Island Paradise, Add Fungi

## Overview
- Conservation on Palmyra Atoll has focused on removing invasive coconut palms and eradicating black rats, but native fungi are now seen as essential for full ecosystem recovery.  
- Researchers found rare mycorrhizal fungi beneath native Pisonia trees, some endemic to the atoll, and identified sites where these fungi could be transplanted to aid seedling growth.  

## Restoration Milestones
- 19th‑century deforestation replaced native forest with coconut plantations.  
- 2011: black rats eliminated by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service.  
- By 2022: 1.5 million invasive palms removed.  

## Role of Fungi
- Mycorrhizal fungi form symbiotic relationships with Pisonia roots, supplying nutrients needed for tree establishment.  
- Co‑author Toby Kiers emphasizes that successful restoration now integrates native plants with native fungi.  

## Ecological Connections
- Native broadleaf forests support seabird colonies; seabird guano enriches surrounding waters, promoting coral reef growth.  
- Healthy reefs generate sediment that helps islands remain above rising seas.  
- Charlie Cornwallis describes the chain: fungi → Pisonia trees → seabirds → coral reefs; breaking any link threatens the whole system.  

## Publication
- Findings reported in *Current Biology* by a team led by Charlie Cornwallis (Lund University).
