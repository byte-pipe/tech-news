# Clipping is a shoo‑in media trend of the year – MediaCat UK  

## Overview  
- Clipping = breaking long‑form videos into 30‑ to 60‑second snippets for social platforms.  
- The practice has risen to prominence this month due to two high‑profile examples.  

## Notable Cases  
- **Geese / Chaotic Good** – Marketing agency founders described on a podcast how they fabricated thousands of accounts to flood social feeds with Geese clips, branding the tactic “trend simulation.”  
- **OpenAI / TBPN** – OpenAI bought the video‑podcast TBPN for $200 million. Although TBPN only draws ~7 000 views per episode, its clips generate hundreds of thousands of views and are monetised with “brought to you by…” tags.  

## Economics of Clipping  
- **Clipper networks** – Max Peterson (founder of Clip) organized 16 000 mostly teenage clippers via Discord; paid $500 000 to 1 300 clippers between Jan‑Sept.  
- **High‑spending streamers** – In Feb, streamer N3on reported spending close to $1 million per month on clipping, with his top clipper earning ~ $170 000 monthly.  
- AI firms are now promising to replace these large‑scale clipper armies.  

## Shifting Role of Clips  
- Initially, clips were a sampling tool to drive traffic to longer content (71 % of Gen Z discover TV/film via short‑form videos).  
- Clips are increasingly an end in themselves, creating inflated perceptions of influence (e.g., Prof G Media’s Clavicular livestream).  
- Paid clippers masquerade as genuine fans, blurring the line between authentic engagement and manufactured hype.  

## Implications for Platforms  
- Recommendation algorithms are being gamed by coordinated early‑engagement signals.  
- Platforms may attempt to tighten detection of artificial clip activity, though historically they act faster against third‑party value extraction than against harmful content.  

## Outlook  
- Clipping’s impact on the media ecosystem is real and likely to persist beyond the dubious practices that exploit it.
