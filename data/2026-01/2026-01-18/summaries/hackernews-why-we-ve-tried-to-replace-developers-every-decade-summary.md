# Why We've Tried to Replace Developers Every Decade Since 1969

## The Pattern That Frustrates Everyone

### The Dream Was Born in the Shadow of Apollo

In 1969, Neil Armstrong took his first steps on the moon as a symbol of humanity's greatest achievement. Behind this historic event stood Margaret Hamilton and her team, who developed crucial software for the Apollo guidance system by hand, despite its mission-critical nature.

The success of the Apollo program revealed that writing complex software required specialized knowledge, focus, and significant resources. Yet, this achievement fueled frustration among business leaders: why prioritize the expensive requirement when a simple alternative seemed so obvious? This cycle would repeat in subsequent decades.

### The Rise of COBOL

In the late 1960s and 1970s, Common Business-Oriented Language (COBOL) emerged as a response to the perceived need for simplified software development. Despite its claim of readability, COBOL proved complex, requiring advanced programming skills and sophisticated logic.

Many business analysts attempted to write COBOL, but found that it failed to meet their needs: readability alone did not eliminate complexity. A new category of COBOL programmers emerged, further entrenching the notion that specialized developers were required for critical systems.

### The Rise of CASE Tools

The 1980s brought Computer-Aided Software Engineering (CASE) tools as a way to simplify software development and increase productivity. These tools promised visual design solutions, generating working code from data flowcharts and relationships diagrams.

Most CASE tool implementations failed or underperformed, largely due to their complexity, high costs, and the difficulty of modeling business processes. Organizations invested heavily in these initiatives, expecting significant gains but often not achieving them.

## Understanding the Pattern

Despite being promised as a solution to software development's problems, each decade reinforced a common pattern: we've tried to replace developers every 40 years since 1969. This cycle reveals that:

* Specialized knowledge and focus are essential for complex software development.
* Business leaders prioritize expensive solutions over simpler alternatives.
* The persistence of this pattern underscores the need for a critical review of our relationship with technology.

## Key Takeaways

* Simple solutions often fail to deliver because they assume ideal conditions not available in reality.
* Repeating success stories with each new decade only highlights how far we are from achieving true simplification and democratization of software development.
* The underlying patterns of this cycle emphasize that creating effective software requires understanding the value, challenges, and complexities of the problem domain.

### Future Research Directions

Further research should investigate:

* Why we've persisted in focusing on centralized labor solutions despite decades of promises for simplified or more productive alternatives.
* Alternative forms of software development that enable collaboration among diverse disciplines without requiring specialized expertise.
