# IPv6 just turned 30 and still hasn’t taken over the world • The Register

#### Network Evolution

# IPv6 Still Holding Up Despite Lack of Maturity (Hear from IPv6 Survivors)

## Key Points:

* The world has largely moved past the need for IPv4 addresses due to increasing demand.
* The first version of IPv6, RFC 1883, was released in December 1995 and added a much larger address space.
* The increased address space made future-proofing the internet possible, but only after making modest changes to network protocols.
* According to data from sources like Google's APNIC and Cloudflare, less than half of worldwide internet users now use IPv6.
* The decision to use either IPv4 or IPv6 didn't necessarily lead to improved networking or security. In fact, some features were added in the older protocol.

#### Network Evolution Timeline:

* 1995: RFC 1883, the first edition of IPv6, was released.
* Early adoption likely began around 1998-2000 due to slow network operator adoption and high costs associated with deploying IPv6 infrastructure.
* The mid-to-late 2000s saw a small surge in IPv6 adoption as net neutrality regulations and increased funding for research on IPv6 led to improved understanding of its potential.

#### Surprising Takeaways:

* IPv6's modest changes were often overlooked, leading to slow uptake despite the new address space available.
* The protocol was not fundamentally different from IPv4; only minor improvements were implemented in the latter.
* Lack of maturity was a significant hurdle in IPv6 adoption.
