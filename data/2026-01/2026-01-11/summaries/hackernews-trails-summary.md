**The Psychology of Self-Deception**

*   **Lies and Deception**: A self-deceiver is someone who intentionally deceives themselves, often to avoid admitting their weakness or vulnerability.
*   **Blue Lies**: This type of lie is a white lie, which involves telling a small dishonesty to maintain social harmony. Examples include giving false information about your intentions or behavior in front of others.

**Evolutionary Psychology**

Research has shown that humans tend to conform to norms and beliefs because they are evolutionary advantages. One key principle of this process is the idea of "self-deception." In this context, it means believing one's own biases, flaws, or even lies about oneself can be advantageous for social cohesion.

**Examples in Real Life**

*   Self-revelation: When asked, people who have benefited from self-deception might reveal a flaw they don't want others to know. This demonstrates how lying to themselves or others is a normal and effective method of maintaining comfort in uncertain situations.
*   Manipulation of Social Norms: People may use self-deception when trying to manipulate social norms. By making small falsehoods, individuals can influence what others believe about them.

**Evolutionary Factors**

Self-deception has played significant roles in evolution across different species and populations. In some contexts, it is beneficial for an individual to deceive themselves to avoid negative outcomes or gain acceptance from others. However, self-deception can also be detrimental when used excessively or unconsciously without awareness of its effects on one's life.

The psychological concept we discussed today highlights the importance of considering human behavior, motives and perceptions as a complex interplay influenced by various factors including societal norms, evolutionary pressures, personal values, and more. These complex dynamics illustrate the immense power and importance attached to deception in both everyday interactions and broader cultural contexts.
