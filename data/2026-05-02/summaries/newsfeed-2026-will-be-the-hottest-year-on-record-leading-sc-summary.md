# 2026 predicted to be the hottest year on record

## Main prediction
- James Hansen and colleagues argue that 2026 will surpass 2024 as the warmest year on record, despite 2024 holding the current record of 1.5 °C above pre‑industrial levels.  
- They note that sea‑surface temperatures already indicate the planet is 0.17 °C warmer than in 2023, a larger increase than the 0.11 °C rise observed between 2023 and 2024.

## Role of El Niño
- A strong El Niño is expected to develop in the second half of 2026, potentially a “super El Niño” that could push global temperatures higher.  
- The first three months of 2026 were about 0.1 °C cooler than the same period in 2024, so the latter half must be significantly hotter for the annual average to exceed 2024.

## Model projections and estimates
- Zeke Hausfather (Berkeley Earth) projects 2026 to be 1.47 °C above pre‑industrial levels, making it the second‑warmest year, based on the early‑year temperature trend.  
- The UK Met Office forecast for 2026 ranges from 1.34 °C to 1.58 °C above pre‑industrial, with a central estimate of 1.46 °C, leaving uncertainty about beating the 1.55 °C recorded in 2024.  
- Hausfather’s later analysis gives a 26 % chance of 2026 being the hottest year and a 56 % chance of it being the second hottest.

## Scientific uncertainty
- Adam Scaife (Met Office) emphasizes that short‑term forecasts carry significant uncertainty and that probabilities, not certainties, are appropriate.  
- John Kennedy (WMO) notes that Hansen’s forecast is one of many methods, each yielding a range of possible outcomes.  
- The possibility that climate sensitivity is higher than current models assume could mean faster warming than projected.

## Expected impacts
- Regardless of the exact ranking, the combination of unprecedented global warming and a strong El Niño is likely to produce extreme weather: heatwaves, droughts, and wildfires across Australia, Southeast Asia, Africa, India, and the Amazon.  
- Scientists agree that the El Niño will amplify the already high baseline of global temperatures, leading to unprecedented events later in the year.

## Context
- 2024 remains the current record year, the first time global temperatures exceeded 1.5 °C above pre‑industrial levels.  
- Recent wildfires in Patagonia illustrate the kind of extreme events already linked to extreme weather patterns.
