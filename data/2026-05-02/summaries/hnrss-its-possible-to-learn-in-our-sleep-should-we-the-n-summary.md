# It’s Possible to Learn in Our Sleep. Should We?

## Historical background
- In 1932 Alois Benjamin Saliger patented the **Psycho‑phone**, a timed phonograph that played affirmations while users slept; advertisements claimed it could achieve months‑long results overnight and cost the equivalent of about $4,000 today.  
- Early testimonials spoke of weight loss, sudden wealth, and even a “Psycho‑phone baby.”  
- The idea of effortless nocturnal learning appears in literature (e.g., Huxley’s *Brave New World*) and in anecdotes of breakthroughs such as Mendeleev’s periodic table and Mary Shelley’s plot for *Frankenstein*.

## Early scientific attempts and their flaws
- **1916**: Navy soldiers seemed to learn Morse code better when it was played overnight.  
- **1942**: Repeated playback of “My fingernails taste terribly bitter” reportedly stopped nail‑biting in 40 % of boys at a summer camp.  
- **1952**: Participants exposed to Chinese vocabulary during sleep appeared to memorize more words.  
- These studies lacked objective verification that subjects were truly asleep; sleep‑stage monitoring (e.g., REM detection) was unavailable, so many participants may have been awake.

## Critical reassessment
- A 1954 paper by Charles W. Simon and William H. Emmons concluded that most “sleep‑learning” subjects were actually awake, rendering the findings essentially meaningless.  
- Sleep‑learning was relegated to “science fiction and quackery” for decades, as noted by sleep researcher Ken Paller.

## Modern revival: targeted memory reactivation (TMR)
- **2007 (Björn Rasch)**: Participants learned spatial locations while smelling rose; re‑exposure to the scent during non‑REM sleep improved later recall, even though they did not remember the odor.  
- **2009 (Ken Paller)**: Sounds linked to object locations were replayed during verified sleep; participants later recalled the spatial positions better, establishing the TMR paradigm.  

## Behavioral effects from sleep cues
- **2014 (Anat Arzi)**: Smokers exposed to a mixture of cigarette and rotting‑fish odors during sleep reduced their cigarette consumption by more than 30 % compared with those exposed while awake.

## Lucid‑dream interventions
- **Konkoly & Paller** gave puzzles to lucid dreamers; one participant solved a geometry problem after dreaming of a related scene.  
- **Emma Peters (University of Bern)** trained motor skills (finger tapping, coin tossing, dart throwing) within REM lucid dreams; post‑sleep performance improved relative to control groups, though dream environments remain uncontrolled.

## Conversational learning during REM
- Multi‑site studies (U.S., France, Germany, Netherlands) asked lucid dreamers yes‑or‑no questions and simple math problems while confirming sleep via EEG/EMG.  
- Participants signaled answers (e.g., left‑right eye movements) and some later recalled the questions, indicating a form of complex information transfer.  
- Example: Christopher Mazurek heard the problem “8 − 6” in a dream, responded with eye movements, and remembered the question after waking.

## Open questions
- Ethical implications of influencing the unconscious mind.  
- Limits of information that can be encoded without disrupting sleep architecture.  
- Potential applications (education, habit change) versus risks of manipulation.
