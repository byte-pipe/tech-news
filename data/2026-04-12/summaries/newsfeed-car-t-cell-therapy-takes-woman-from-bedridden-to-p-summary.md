# CAR T‑cell therapy takes woman from bedridden to ‘perfectly fine’

## Background
- Autoimmune diseases arise when self‑targeting immune cells escape normal screening and persist.
- Traditional treatments rely on immune‑suppressing drugs, which can be ineffective and have side‑effects.
- CAR T‑cell therapy, originally developed for cancer, involves extracting a patient’s T‑cells, genetically engineering them to attack a specific target, and reinfusing them.

## Patient case
- A woman developed three autoimmune conditions after pregnancy:
  1. Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia (antibodies destroy red blood cells).
  2. Immune thrombocytopenia (antibodies destroy platelets).
  3. Antiphospholipid syndrome (antibodies promote dangerous clot formation).
- Prior treatments with various immunosuppressants failed; she required regular blood transfusions and anticoagulants.
- She was bedridden when first seen by Dr Fabian Müller at the University Hospital of Erlangen, Germany.

## CAR T‑cell treatment
- Müller’s team engineered CAR T‑cells to target the patient’s antibody‑producing B‑cells.
- The modified cells were infused, killing the pathogenic B‑cells while leaving older, non‑pathogenic immune cells intact.
- The patient’s immune system recognized the CAR T‑cells as foreign and eliminated them within months, allowing new, normal B‑cells to develop.

## Outcomes
- Within seven days the patient could get out of bed; within months she appeared fully recovered.
- At 11 months post‑treatment she reported being “perfectly fine,” with only minor lingering effects attributed to earlier drug therapies.
- Most patients treated with CAR T‑cells for autoimmune diseases remain disease‑free, though occasional relapses have required repeat treatment.

## Broader implications
- CAR T‑cell therapy shows promise for a range of autoimmune disorders, including lupus, multiple sclerosis, colitis, and severe asthma.
- Side‑effects appear milder for autoimmune applications than for cancer, likely because fewer cells need to be eliminated.
- The therapy is costly (US $200‑600 k per treatment) but may reduce long‑term healthcare expenses and restore patients to work.
- Longer follow‑up studies are needed to confirm durability of cures and to assess the approach in larger patient cohorts.
