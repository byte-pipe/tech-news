# Back to Earth: What happens to the Artemis II astronauts now?

## Immediate medical evaluation
- Upon splashdown, the crew are examined by doctors on the recovery warship.  
- They are then flown by helicopter to an aircraft that transports them to NASA’s Johnson Space Center in Houston for further health monitoring.

## Physical effects of the two‑week flight
- Microgravity causes muscle and bone loss, especially in posture‑supporting muscles (back, neck, calves).  
- Exercise mitigates but does not eliminate loss; muscle mass can drop up to 20 % after two weeks.  
- Compared with longer stays on the ISS (5–6 months) and shuttle missions (2–3 weeks), the health impact on Artemis II astronauts is expected to be relatively modest.

## Psychological adjustment
- Astronauts are trained to remain level‑headed; “space come‑downs” are not anticipated.  
- Crew members have expressed deep appreciation for teamwork, purpose, and the view of Earth from space.  
- Many astronauts describe a reluctance to leave space because of its excitement and the sense of shared humanity it reinforces.

## Reunion with families
- The crew will be welcomed by their spouses, children, and other relatives.  
- Personal anecdotes: Commander Reid Wiseman discussed contingency plans with his daughters before launch; Dr Catherine Hansen highlighted her husband Jeremy’s children’s excitement.  
- Families plan private celebrations followed by public events.

## Post‑flight engagements
- NASA has not disclosed specific duties, but the astronauts remain active members of the agency’s astronaut corps.  
- Future Artemis missions (Artemis III in 2027, Artemis IV in 2028) will draw from the same pool of astronauts.  
- President Donald Trump called the crew while they were in orbit and invited them to the White House for an Oval Office reception; it is unclear whether Canadian astronaut Jeremy Hansen will attend.  
- The crew’s public profile is expected to rise sharply, bringing fame and the need for adjustment.

## Outlook and broader mission context
- Artemis II marked the deepest human spaceflight since Apollo 13, extending the record by over 4,000 miles.  
- The mission’s success paves the way for lunar landings and, eventually, crewed missions to Mars.  
- Personal items taken to the Moon and the mission’s scientific goals underscore NASA’s long‑term objectives.
