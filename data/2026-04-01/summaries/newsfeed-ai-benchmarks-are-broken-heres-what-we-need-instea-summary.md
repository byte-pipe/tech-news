# AI benchmarks are broken. Here’s what we need instead.

## The problem with current AI benchmarking
- AI performance is usually measured by comparing machines to individual humans on isolated tasks with clear right‑or‑wrong answers.  
- Such tests are easy to standardize, rank, and publicize, but they do **not reflect how AI is actually used**.  
- Real‑world AI operates within messy, collaborative environments, interacting with multiple people over extended periods.  
- This mismatch leads to misunderstandings of AI capabilities, hidden systemic risks, and mis‑judged economic and social impacts.  

## Consequences when benchmarks fail
- Organizations adopt models that score highly on technical benchmarks, investing significant resources.  
- In practice, these models often cause delays, extra work, or outright failure (e.g., FDA‑approved radiology AIs that slowed reporting due to integration with local standards).  
- High‑scoring models are abandoned to an “AI graveyard,” wasting time, money, and eroding trust in AI, especially in critical sectors like health.  
- Regulatory oversight based on incomplete metrics creates blind spots, leaving governments and firms to shoulder unanticipated risks.  

## Proposed solution: HAIC benchmarks
HAIC = **Human–AI, Context‑Specific Evaluation**. It reframes benchmarking along four dimensions:

1. **Unit of analysis** – shift from individual, single‑task performance to team and workflow performance.  
2. **Time horizon** – move from one‑off tests with right/wrong answers to long‑term impact assessment.  
3. **Outcome measures** – expand beyond correctness and speed to include organizational outcomes, coordination quality, and error detectability.  
4. **System effects** – consider upstream and downstream consequences rather than isolated outputs.  

## How HAIC is applied
- **Team‑level focus**: A UK hospital examined how AI affected multidisciplinary team coordination, deliberation, and risk practices, not just diagnostic accuracy.  
- **Longitudinal assessment**: In humanitarian projects, AI was evaluated over 18 months, tracking how easily human teams could detect and correct errors.  
- **Economic relevance**: By measuring system‑level effects, HAIC helps temper inflated expectations of productivity gains that stem from task‑level benchmarks.  

## Why HAIC matters
- Aligns evaluation with the real contexts where AI is deployed, providing a more reliable signal of readiness.  
- Reduces wasted investments and protects public trust by exposing hidden failures before large‑scale rollout.  
- Offers regulators metrics that reflect actual risk, enabling better oversight.
