# A responsible authorship culture is needed — it is a collective responsibility

## Main argument
- A responsible authorship culture is a key aspect of scientific integrity.  
- Authorship decisions should be anchored in three interconnected principles: **credit**, **accountability**, and **transparency** (Kiermer et al., Proc. Natl Acad. Sci. USA 123, e2531268, 2026).

## Supporting evidence
- The conclusion comes from a working group convened by the US National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, in which the article’s authors participated as journal editors.  
- The article is published in *Nature* (doi: https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-026-00997-9) and also appears in *JAMA*, *PLoS Biology*, and *PLoS Medicine*.

## Implications for the research community
- Emphasises that fostering a responsible authorship culture requires collective effort from researchers, institutions, and publishers.  
- Suggests systematic adoption of tools such as the Contributor Role Taxonomy (CRediT) to transparently record the extent of each author’s contribution.
