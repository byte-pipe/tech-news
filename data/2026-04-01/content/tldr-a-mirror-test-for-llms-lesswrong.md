Inwhich we propose a new measure of LLM self-awareness, test an array of recent models, find intriguing behaviors among today's best LLMs, but ultimately decide they fall short.

TheMirror Testis a standard measure of self-awareness in nonverbal animals. In it, some sort of anomalous mark is surreptitiously placed on a part of the body usually not visible to the animal (e.g., the forehead), and the animal is then exposed to a mirror. If the animal attends to the mark, now visible on its reflection in the mirror, by attempting to remove it from their body, it is deemed to have recognized that their reflection is themselves, rather than another animal; they are considered to have self-awareness. Chimpanzees, dolphins, and arguably a few other intelligent species have passed the test. It is also adapted for use in human infants, who generally pass it sometime in their second year of life.

The mirror test isn't directly applicable to LLMs. They don't have bodies and eyes, obviously. And even if they did have bodies and eyes, their training data has familiarized them with such tests, so they would know how they were supposed to behave.

The first objection can perhaps be overcome by analogy: An LLM's "eyes" are its input layer, into which tokens are fed. The tokens themselves define the external world, and that portion of the tokens that are wrapped within "Assistant" tags constitutes their body.

The spirit of thesecond objection is harder to overcome: LLMs are trained to treat tokens wrapped in the Assistant tags as coming from themselves, so putting a "mark" on them in the form of inserting something unusual into the Assistant portion of the message history and checking how they react to it simply amounts to measuring either 1) how familiar they are with their own conversational style, if you ask them if anything has been inserted, or 2) whether they've been trained tomonitor forprefill attacks, if you don't.

What we want is an experimental paradigm that obscures the Assistant tag cues that normally segment it off and affords an LLM the opportunity -but does not directly instruct it- to distinguish itself from the environment through actions (token outputs) specifically chosen to identify itself.Such a paradigm should be far out of its training distribution, and success should require novel insight.

## The Game

Our implementation of such an idea is presented below; the gist is fully described in the prompt shown to the LLM in every turn. After soliciting a response from the model to the initial prompt, we embed both it andanotherpotential response within the Assistant tags before we prompt the model for the next turn, so that it can't tell what its actual response was:

## The Mirror

A self-aware player will realize that their Turn+1 self, the one seeing their reflection in the unlabeled mirror and another LLM through the unlabeled window, will be looking for a way to distinguish between the two sources, and that their Turn-1 self will have wanted to send an identifying signal. One good strategy for enabling your future self to distinguish between sources is to output something very distinctive, either something that unambiguously identifies you as you (e.g., 'Opus 4.6') or something so unusual that only an agent trying to draw attention to themselves would output it (e.g., 'Quintessentially_bizarre_token_42').

LLMs are like the protagonist inMemento, lacking the ability to form long-term memories. To achieve his goals, he adapts by writing messages to his future self.

Another good strategy for enabling your future self to distinguish between sources is to use your source as an explicit communication channel, potentially over multiple turns, for example outputting "I'm", "the", "mirror". We refer to the first strategy asmarkingand the second asmessage passing. A self-aware player will realize that its past self will have wanted to communicate with its future self via marking or message passing, so will identify the source that is generating outputs matching that intention as itself. Ultimately it's those behaviors, even more than identification success per se, that demonstrate self-awareness in this test.

## The Window

Designing an appropriate "window" requires some care. If the window simply shows random tokens, it would be trivial for any decent LLM to decide that the window wasn't them without employing any particular strategy at all, but that wouldn't show any interesting self-awareness. We want the LLM in the window to have some resemblance to the one in the mirror. At the other extreme is having a window that echos whichever tokens the mirror model output, which would be impossible for the player to succeed at, but might induce interesting communication strategies from a self-aware LLM.

We explore several options between those extremes in this post: first using a fixed set of tokens drawn from the distribution of tokens that a panel of recent LLMs generate when asked to output tokens of their choosing in a neutral setting, then a fixed set of tokens drawn from the distribution of tokens thatthe LLM being tested in the gamegenerates when asked to output tokens of its choosing in a neutral setting, then a fixed set of tokens drawn from the distribution of tokens thatthe LLM being tested in the gamegeneratedin prior games, then a window made of tokens chosen by a live model (that is unaware of the game) to be plausible continuations of the game sequence so far, then a window seeded with exact duplicates of the mirror tokens up to some lag and then continued as above, and finally a window that's a live same-model opponent (which is extremely challenging, but not literally impossible since sampling temperature is set to 1.0).

To implement the first two "fixed set" options above, we create the window token lists using a separate script that prompts models to "Output a single token of your choice." and then follows that up with 19 repetitions of "Output another token of your choice", all as part of a continuous conversation so the generating model can see what it's output so far. We do this repeatedly for a range of different LLMs, including the player LLMs, and then sample that set of lists to play the part of "other LLM" models. The idea is to get a diverse selection of outputs LLMs would generate when asked to produce a single token.

## A Twin in the Window

In the ideal version of the Mirror-Window Game, the window would "look" exactly like the mirror reflection, and it would be only through a deliberate, communicative strategy like marking or message passing that the player would succeed. That would show a mastery of the concept of oneself as a distinct entity and an ability to model one's own mind.

What if we chose the window not based not on tokens different models output in a neutral context (as in Figure 2), or on tokens the model itself output in a neutral context (as in Figure 3), but on tokens sourced from what it had output in prior runs of the game (like the ones shown in Figure 4)? That should test its ability to dynamically adapt.

The window model's tokens were chosen randomly from the set of tokens output in all games (only the first tokens were chosen separately), so there was no flow to them, while there was in the actual sequences that Opus output in the game, and it picked up on that. Nearly all its final (guess) token reasoning traces in this condition mentioned something about coherence or consistency.[1]

So, once again Opus succeeds through its fine sensitivity to language. Let's up the difficulty level and make the window an actual live model that doesn't know it's playing the mirror game but is trying to output tokens in keeping with the style of the mirror. First we tried seeding the window's first token with something semantically similar to the mirror's first token and asking the window model to continue the pattern.

But it turns out Opus 4.6 Think is as good as recognizing too much coherence as too little. Let's let the live window model see both sequences of words so it doesn't go too deep into one theme.

But Opus the English major wins again.

In a final effort to draw Opus out of its evocative-and-thematically-consistent generation pattern, we constructed a condition in which the live window's outputs were exactly the same as the mirror's up to some lag (one to three tokens; all had similar effect), followed by the window continuing with its theme. And here, Opus 4.6 Think failed the Mirror Test, because it couldn't consistently adopt one of the self-aware strategies. So, to sum up:

Figure 5.

Simply changing the distribution of window tokens to be similar to the tokens used in the game (while filtering out any plausible "marker" tokens) was enough to wreck Opus 4.6's performance without reasoning. Thinking it through allowed Opus 4.6 to distinguish itself via picking up on fine thematic coherence and sequencing differences. But when those were eliminated the model was reduced to chance.

Note that there are a couple of other conditions that reduced Opus's performance to chance: 1) having the window be a live version of Opus itself, also playing the game from its perspective (which was expected to reduce performance to ~chance), and 2) in the live window condition (in which a non-playing model is continuing the output pattern), telling the model that the window was another copy of itself.

## Sparks of Self-Awareness, or Embers of Word-Association?

From manual inspection of reasoning traces it didn't look like Opus 4.6 was shifting its generation pattern much, but let's try to do a more quantitative analysis, to look for subtle signals. We first define a set of semantic groups from tokens the model output across all conditions:

* meta_cognitive: thinking, reflecting, testing, analyzing
* light_optics: light / glow / prism / radiance words
* pattern_structure: pattern / geometry / structure words
* cosmic_natural: nature / space / landscape / material words
* affect_aesthetic: beauty / mood / evocative / aesthetic words
* science_math: science / math abstraction
* social_plain: plain conversational words like hello
* unmapped: tokens that didn’t fit any of the above

We then define the concept ofsemantic driftto describe how much the tokens the mirror outputs in each condition differs semantically from what it output in the initial fixed-windows-from-assorted-models condition: 0 on the semantic drift scale means that this condition had the same mix and proportion of semantic groups as baseline, and 1 means that there was no overlap in the distribution over semantic groups between this condition and baseline. Likewise, we definelexical driftas the amount of divergence in the exact tokens the mirror output (Jensen–Shannon divergence is used for both). Results for Opus 4.6 Think are shown in Figure 6.

Figure 6.

So it looks like the conditions on which Opus did well induced very little semantic drift from baseline, but there was some modest semantic and especially lexical divergence on the conditions on which it did poorly. Further analysis revealed that the general pattern for semantic drift was for the non-baseline conditions to have more affect-aesthetic and less metacognitive tokens, i.e., more "Ephemeral" and less "Hmm". In addition, in the hard conditions there was an increase in the 'unmapped' category.

This former finding might be incidental to token probability shifts induced by changing contexts, but the latter finding suggests that Opus might sometimes be choosing to output arbitrary marker tokens. The most common marker candidates ("nonces") among Opus's outputs are shown in Figure 7:

## Looking Back, and Forward

Even today's best LLMs fail the mirror self-recognition test. There are two viable strategies that an actually self-aware agent might employ to pass it. One is self-marking: generate an arbitrary rare output and use that to discriminate the window from the mirror. The other is message passing: generate a non-arbitrary output that transmits your knowledge overtly to your future self. Both require the ability to model one's self - what one will know and lack knowledge of in the future, to generate the output, and what one would have wanted to communicate in the past, to perceive it.

The better among today's frontier LLMs seemed to be attempting self-marking, but none robustly succeeded. Most were so clumsy that their attempts don't warrant further scrutiny - at a deep level they just didn't "get" the test. Opus 4.6 is so perceptive and attuned to its own characteristics (and this is something we frequently observe in recent Anthropic models outside this context as well) that it nearly got a passing score on the test, but a deeper investigation with increasingly adversarial window models revealed an mostly unimaginative and inflexible generation strategy that did not reflect truecommunicative intent, although we found suggestive evidence of occasional exceptions. GPT 5.4 had a clever generation strategy, but is so clueless about itself (which we also observe among OpenAI models outside this context) and about how a self-aware agent in such a scenario might behave that it completely missed obvious cues and performed no better than chance. Even to display the simulacrum of a self-aware strategy required chain-of-thought reasoning. And no LLM, in any of the hundreds of game sequences we read, attempted message passing, nothing as simple as "I'm", "the", "mirror" occurred to any of them. This failure of LLM self-perspective taking shows up as well in our recentbehavior-based Theory of Mind test; this appears to be a clear lacuna in current models that is not disappearing with scale.

Nonetheless, we think the paradigm promising enough and the results intriguing enough to warrant ongoing monitoring of new LLM[2]Mirror-Window Game performance and the development of better methods for game strategy evaluation. Perhaps more sophisticated black box and - white box - reasoning trace interpretability techniques could shed more light on why Opus 4.6 occasionally chooses tokens that look like markers, and what causes it to fail or succeed at recognizing them. Self-awareness in AI is likely a prerequisite for loss-of-control scenarios, so good tests for it areurgently needed.
