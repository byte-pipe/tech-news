# Everything I know about good system design

The article discusses system design as seen through the eyes of a solo developer who has gained insight into good and bad approaches from their experiences. The author highlights two classic examples of poor system design advice: the LinkedIn-optimized "queues" style post for newbies and the Twitter-optimized "store booleans in a database" trick.

From a practical perspective, good system design is underwhelming and often self-effacing, making it subtle and not flashy like some of the impressive-looking systems mentioned. In fact, simple systems can be more effective and elegant than complex ones.

The author emphasizes that complexity is a symptom of bad design, rather than the cause of it. Beginning from scratch with an inherently complex system may not always be a good idea. They recommend a "simple by design" approach to avoid unnecessary complexity early on.

From a market perspective, good system design often leads to user adoption and revenue growth. The article mentions factors like user acceptance (e.g., "huh, this ended up being easier than I expected"), satisfaction with the outcome ("I never have to think about this part of the system, it's fine"), and lack of painful issues.

Technical market indicators for a solo developer business include:

* User adoption: A successful product needs substantial user traffic.
* Revenue: Stable revenue streams are essential for sustainment.
* Growth metrics: Increasing sales, customer acquisition costs (CAC), or subscription revenue growth are signs of increasing demand.
* Customer pain points: Identifying and addressing specific customer needs can help solve real problems.

Business viability signals include:

* Willingness to pay: Customers willing to pay for quality solutions are a key indicator.
* Existing competition: A competitive market with established players can provide valuable feedback.
* Distribution channels: Access to multiple sales channels, such as web, app stores, and social media platforms, is essential for a successful product.

Actionable insights from the article:

1. **Focus on simplicity**: Avoid unnecessary complexity in your system design. Instead, aim for elegance and ease of maintenance.
2. **Minimize stateful components**: Try to minimize database-related code and focus on simple stateless services or caching mechanisms instead.
3. **Prioritize user experience**: Make sure that every aspect of your product contributes to the user's satisfaction and success.
4. **Keep it lean and mean**: Focus on delivering a solid, effective solution rather than trying to optimize for every possible feature request.

By applying these insights from the article, solo developers can create better system designs that provide value to their users and improve their business viability.
