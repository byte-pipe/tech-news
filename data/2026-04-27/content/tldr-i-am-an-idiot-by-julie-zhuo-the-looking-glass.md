# I am an idiot

### Notes on idiocy, in the age of something smarter.

Julie Zhuo
Apr 21, 2026
99
7
15
Share

### i.

The title says it all: I am an idiot.

It used to be hard to say this. It used to be hard to eventhinkit.

It’s like there’s a trip-wire in my mind. The moment a thought breachesI'm an idiotterritory, or even brushes against it withUgh I'm so bad at thisorThe only explanation is my stupidity, another thought snaps to attention like a drill sergeant:That's loser-talk, soldier! Drop and give me twenty reframes!

Sometimes a third thought trundles in like an indulgent grandmother:Don’t be ridiculous. You’re wonderful just the way you are. Did you know everyone fails sometimes?Other times, it arrives with the eye-roll of an older sibling:I’m not going to entertain that kind of nonsense. Go for a run. Or eat some Flamin’ Hot Cheetos. Get over yourself.

As all these thoughts engage in a ferocious power struggle, I inhale a bag of Flamin' Hot Cheetos.

Eventually theI am an idiotthought slinks back into the murky depths of my mind, sulking, where it lies in wait, never fully banished.

### ii.

I am an idiot about many, many things:

I cannot explain to you the fundamentals of physics, or chemistry, or biology.

My memory for names, dates, and details is so bad I’d be the worst witness at a trial.

Following mathematical proofs exhausts me.

My wit is plodding; I can’t think on my feet.

I use 100 lines when one would do, because the right words elude me.

My mind jumps around like a cricket, unable to enjoy the perfectly good leaf it’s on.

I rarely have conviction. When I do, it's only a matter of time before I'm proven wrong.

Multi-tasking feels like an arcane skill.

I have every intent to follow up on all my texts and messages but rarely do.

With kids, I lecture too much and listen too little.

With adults, I listen too much and contribute too little.

My prediction algorithm is eroding.

My taste is out of date.

My judgment is continually upended.

And yet the world keeps spinning, glittering in its marvels.

### iii.

We swim in a world where knowledge is power and competence is currency, especially among achievement-oriented, terminally online, hustler types.

The layman’s dream is to besogoodat something that you will make boatloads of money and get oceans of affection. People will admire and want tobeyou, or curse you because they aren’t. The pulsing heart of this dream iswinning. And it’s the smart, hardworking, hungry folks who seem to win. Not the idiots.

I’ve interviewed hundreds of people and not once has anyone said, “You know what, I’m really an idiot.” We’ve learned to launder weakness into strength.I’m slow because I’m thoughtful. I’m abrasive because I’m truth-seeking.Idiocy doesn’t get the same treatment.

But let us look at it, let us reallysitwith this question:

What’s wrong with being an idiot?

When I examined it, two fears jumped out: that nobody wants to be around an idiot, and that nothing good happens to one.

Strip those away (imagine a world where people sought out idiots and good fortune followed them) and the trait itself would be neutral.

So idiocy is not some inherently negative trait, it’s only so because of its association to rejection and misfortune.

Which brings us to the next question: are those fears true?

### iv.

Exhibit A: famous people who have said they were ding-dongs:

Albert Einstein: “I know quite certainly that I myself have no special talent.”

Warren Buffet: “I’ve been an idiot for not buying Amazon.”

Shakespeare: “The fool doth think he is wise, but the wise man knows himself to be a fool.”

Elon Musk: “Starting a car company is idiotic and an electric car company is idiocy squared.”

Confucius: "Real knowledge is to know the extent of one's ignorance.”

Ray Bradbury: “A conglomerate heap of trash, that’s what I am.”

Mark Twain: "I must have a prodigious quantity of mind; it takes me as much as a week sometimes to make it up."

Niels Bohr: "An expert is a person who has made all the mistakes that can be made in a very narrow field."

Charles Darwin: “My power to follow a long and purely abstract train of thought is very limited; and therefore I could never have succeeded with metaphysics or mathematics.”

Socrates: “I know that I know nothing.”

### v.

Surely there is a difference between an accomplished personsayingthey are an idiot, and actually being an idiot. After all, if you are accomplished, you must not be an idiot.

But what is idiocy, really?

Merriam-Webster gives usa foolish or stupid person.Foolish and stupid relative to what, though? To other humans? To the smartest humans? To some imagined ideal?

If Einstein, Shakespeare, and Socrates claim to be idiots, is your judgment so sharp that you are certain these people did not mean what they said? Do you charge them either with false modesty or error in judgment? (If it is an error in judgment, then maybe they really are idiots!)

Recently, Anthropic announced that its latest model, named Mythos, outperforms previous models across the board, but the headline finding is that itmanaged to find security vulnerabilitiesin widely-used open-source code that 27 years of human review had not caught.

We see no reason to think that Mythos Preview is where language models’ cybersecurity capabilities will plateau. The trajectory is clear. Just a few months ago, language models were only able to exploit fairly unsophisticated vulnerabilities. Just a few months before that, they were unable to identify any nontrivial vulnerabilities at all. Over the coming months and years, we expect that language models (those trained by us and by others) will continue to improve along all axes…

The machines are getting smarter with every model.

At first, they overpowered us with their calculation speed.

Then, their fluency with the world’s accumulated knowledge passed ours.

Soon, their prediction will sharpen. Their taste will catch up. Their judgment will improve.

I am an idiot, yes. But as time goes on, we all may well be.

### vi.

The fear of a thing is often worse than the thing. Most of the suffering lives in the wait.

When I was a kid, an inexperienced ER nurse tried to draw my blood and missed a good dozen times. After that, the sight of a needle would send me into a mess of tears.

At eight, people understood. I got sympathetic looks and lollipops. At eighteen, waiting for travel vaccines before a trip abroad, the sobs that erupted when the nurse walked in with the syringe were more alarming than charming.

The agony was in all the moments before the needle. The whimpering countdown, the anxiety that imagined the incoming poke as a pain of extreme proportions, my body stiffening into a board as the nurse swabbed my arm.

It all bubbled into a hot, bright point of panic before she emptied the syringe and stuck a Band-Aid over it. And then I’d slump over in relief. The worst part, the imagined part, was over.

This was my relationship to needles until the day I learned from my doctor that I was pregnant. Matter-of-factly, she put me on a regular blood-draw routine, unaware of my extreme fears.

I thought to myself,Pregnancy is going to be a bitch if I panic-attack every time a needle comes near.At that moment, something switched in me. I decided to stop being afraid.

At the next appointment, I made myself watch. The needle went in. It was a small pinch, the same one it had always been. It was so anticlimactic.

The thing I’d been afraid of had only ever been a story.

### vii.

It’s time I stopped with the fear of being an idiot.

Let me sink into the notion, cozy into it like I would a plush set of slippers.

I’ve been an idiot too many times to count. I’ve blurted out the wrong things, chased the wrong trains, and assumed I actually knew exactly what I was doing.

I suspect I’ll grow even more idiotic, as we ricochet faster and faster into a future none of us can predict.

So call me an idiot. I’ll agree.

Interview me, and I’ll tell you what I really am.

Maybe we’ll find some common ground. Maybe it won’t be so terrible.

There are marvels yet to be discovered in the world. Creative acts that still need pursuing. Pieces of truth still to be assembled. After all,our souls need proof of work.

Whoever said that idiots couldn't be happy? That they couldn't grow content?

It’s only ever a story in our minds.
