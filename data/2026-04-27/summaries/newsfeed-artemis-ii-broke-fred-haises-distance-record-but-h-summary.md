# Artemis II Breaks Apollo 13 Distance Record – Insights from Fred Haise  

## Overview  
- Artemis II’s crew of four traveled 252,756 mi (406,771 km) from Earth, surpassing the Apollo 13 record of 248,655 mi set in 1970.  
- The mission used a free‑return trajectory that took the Orion capsule farther from Earth than any previous human flight.  
- The record stood for almost 56 years; future Artemis missions may not exceed it unless orbital dynamics or a Mars mission align.  

## Why the Record May Not Be Broken Soon  
- Lunar landing missions will orbit closer to the Moon than Artemis II’s high‑altitude free‑return path.  
- The Moon’s elliptical orbit varies between ~225,800 mi and ~252,000 mi from Earth; occasional apogees approach the Artemis II distance but occur rarely.  
- Planned Gateway orbits (near‑rectilinear halo) could allow greater Earth‑Moon distances, but NASA has canceled Gateway to focus on a lunar surface base.  
- Orion’s service module cannot reach low lunar orbit; future mission profiles will likely keep the spacecraft nearer the Moon.  

## Fred Haise’s Perspective (Interview Highlights)  
- **Record Significance:** Haise sees the distance record as a “consolation prize” after Apollo 13’s aborted landing; it was never a major personal achievement.  
- **Emotional Impact:** His biggest disappointment was not walking on the Moon, not the lack of a distance record.  
- **Context of the Record:** The Apollo 13 crew happened to be farther from Earth because the Moon was near apogee; the extra distance was incidental.  
- **Funding Reality:** Haise notes that sustained, fully funded programs like Apollo are rare; most modern efforts (e.g., Orion, Artemis I) faced long development times and budget constraints.  
- **Current Observation:** He watched Artemis II’s return from a distance, noting the superior imaging technology compared with Apollo.  

## Technical Details  
- **Artemis II Flight Path:** Free‑return trajectory, higher altitude than Apollo lunar orbits, no lunar landing.  
- **Moon’s Orbital Range:** 225,800–252,000 mi average; apogee can reach 252,727 mi, occurring roughly every 5,000 years at the extreme, but within 100 km of that distance three times before 2040.  
- **Future Opportunities:** A mission to Mars or a deep‑space orbit could definitively surpass the Artemis II distance.  

## Bottom Line  
- Artemis II set a new human‑distance‑from‑Earth record, but most upcoming lunar missions will likely stay below that threshold.  
- The record’s longevity reflects both the specific geometry of Apollo 13 and the challenges of funding and designing deep‑space crewed missions.  
- Fred Haise, the last living Apollo 13 astronaut, views the record as a minor footnote compared with his original goal of walking on the Moon.
