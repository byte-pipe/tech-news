# Humble raises $24M to build a cabless EV truck

## Company overview
- Humble is a San Francisco‑based autonomous freight startup that emerged from stealth.
- Founded by Eyal Cohen, an ex‑engineer from Uber ATG, Waabi, Apple and Spark AI (acquired by John Deere in 2023).
- The company focuses on a fully electric, cabless freight vehicle called the **Humble Hauler**.

## Vehicle design
- The Hauler deliberately omits a driver’s cab, allowing 360° sensor coverage (cameras, LiDAR, radar) and freeing payload capacity.
- Geometry is optimized for 40‑foot and 53‑foot intermodal containers.
- Operates dock‑to‑dock: picks up a container at a dock, delivers directly to the destination, and unloads without intermediate handoffs.

## Business model differentiation
- Contrasts with Aurora’s hub‑to‑hub model (human driver handoffs near city limits) and Kodiak’s fixed launch‑and‑landing zones.
- Humble bets on delivering “all the way to the customer,” arguing that logistics operators need end‑to‑end autonomy.
- Autonomy stack relies on vision‑language‑action models rather than traditional rule‑based systems.

## Funding and investors
- Closed a $24 million seed round led by Eclipse, a venture firm focused on physical AI.
- Participation from Energy Impact Partners.
- Key investor Jiten Behl (Eclipse partner, former Rivian chief strategy/growth officer) emphasizes a capital requirement “order of magnitude less” than the multi‑billion dollars spent in the sector.

## Market context
- U.S. truck freight market: $906 billion annually.
- Autonomous freight segment projected at $575.7 million in 2026, rising to $3.25 billion by 2035.
- Federal support emerging via the Self‑Drive Act of 2026 and ongoing engagement with NHTSA.

## Outlook and challenges
- Seed funding will support Hauler development and a pilot programme; commercial timeline, pilot partners, and initial geographies remain undisclosed.
- Humble must demonstrate operational proof before competitors like Aurora, which already reports >250,000 driverless miles and plans 200 autonomous trucks by year‑end.
