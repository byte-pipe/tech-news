# Renting is for Suckers - Andrew Kelley

This article discusses the problem of the growing trend of "renting" computing resources, particularly in the context of cloud computing, from the perspective of a solo developer. Here's a 3-4 paragraph analysis:

Problem/Opportunity:
The article highlights the problem of the "reverse Robin Hood effect" where those without capital are forced to rent computing resources and pay interest to large tech companies, rather than owning their own hardware. This is presented as a significant opportunity for solo developers to leverage their own personal computers and avoid the high costs and potential risks of cloud-based solutions.

Market Indicators:
The author cites specific pricing examples to demonstrate the significant cost differences between owning hardware (e.g., a $3,000 Mac mini) versus renting cloud resources (e.g., $42,000 per year for a single macOS runner). This indicates a clear pain point for users, especially solo developers, who are being pressured to move to the cloud despite the financial implications. The author also mentions the prevalence of cloud-focused talks and conferences, suggesting a concerted effort by cloud providers to drive market adoption.

Technical Feasibility:
For a solo developer, the technical feasibility of leveraging their own personal computer is extremely high. The author notes that modern personal computers are powerful enough to run top websites, and the act of programming itself is "effectively free" in terms of electricity usage. The only potential drawback mentioned is the need to occasionally download documentation or fetch dependencies, but being offline does not compromise one's ability to code.

Business Viability:
The article strongly suggests that the business viability of a solo developer-focused model is high. The author highlights the significant cost savings of using one's own hardware versus renting cloud resources, which can range from $576 per month to $90,000 per month depending on the cloud service and usage. Additionally, the author emphasizes the benefits of privacy, security, and independence that come with using one's own computer, which could be compelling value propositions for solo developers.

Overall, this article presents a compelling case for solo developers to consider building their businesses around leveraging their own personal computing resources, rather than succumbing to the pressure to move to the cloud. The clear cost savings, technical feasibility, and potential business viability make this a promising opportunity for solo developers to explore.
