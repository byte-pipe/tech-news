# Renting is for Suckers - Andrew Kelley

This article discusses the problem of the growing trend of "renting" computing resources, such as cloud computing, rather than owning and using one's own hardware. The author makes several key points:

1. Problem/Opportunity:
   - The author argues that the shift towards cloud computing and "renting" computing resources, rather than owning them, is a concerning trend that goes against basic financial planning principles. He sees this as a problem that needs to be addressed.
   - The opportunity lies in providing a compelling alternative to the cloud computing model, where solo developers and small businesses can leverage their own hardware and avoid the high costs and potential risks of cloud-based solutions.

2. Market Indicators:
   - The author cites specific pricing examples, such as the cost of using a single macOS runner on Microsoft's platform ($42,000 per year) versus the cost of purchasing a beefy Mac mini ($3,000 for two years).
   - He also mentions the prevalence of cloud-related talks and conferences, suggesting that there is a concerted effort by cloud providers to promote their services and influence the industry.

3. Technical Feasibility for a Solo Developer:
   - The author argues that programming can be done effectively on one's own hardware, without relying on cloud-based solutions. He highlights the power of modern personal computers and the low cost of programming as a hobby.
   - However, he acknowledges the potential complexity and risks associated with the emerging "agentic coding" trend, where developers may need to trust third-party platforms and models, which could introduce security and privacy concerns.

4. Business Viability Signals:
   - The author suggests that the shift towards cloud computing is driven by the financial incentives of cloud providers, who have "enormous budgets" to spend on marketing and influencing the industry.
   - He questions the transparency and trustworthiness of these cloud providers, highlighting their history of untrustworthiness and the potential for hidden agendas in their pricing and service models.

Overall, the article presents a compelling case for solo developers and small businesses to consider alternatives to cloud computing and to leverage their own hardware and resources. The author sees this as an opportunity to provide a more cost-effective and secure solution, while also challenging the dominant narrative around the benefits of cloud computing.
