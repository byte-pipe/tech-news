# Analysis: Bennett‑Lapid alliance attempt to unseat Israel’s Netanyahu

## Background
- Former prime ministers Naftali Bennett (far‑right) and Yair Lapid (centrist) announced the revival of their 2021 coalition, creating the “Together” party under Bennett’s leadership.  
- The 2021 alliance had formed a broad government that included right‑wing, centre, left‑wing parties and, for the first time, a party representing Palestinian citizens of Israel.  
- That government rotated the premiership (Bennett first, then Lapid) and achieved a stable budget and reduced influence of religious parties, but collapsed in 2022 after internal disputes and defections.

## Poll projections
- A Jerusalem Post poll predicts the new bloc will win four seats fewer than the combined totals of Bennett’s and Lapid’s former parties if run separately.  
- The alliance is also projected to have one seat fewer than Netanyahu’s Likud party.

## Policy towards Palestinians
- Both leaders have supported Israel’s military actions in Gaza; Lapid has spoken of a two‑state solution, while Bennett repeatedly opposes a Palestinian state.  
- Bennett has advocated a “shoot‑to‑kill” policy against Palestinians crossing from Gaza and excludes “Arab parties” from any future coalition.  
- Palestinian rights group Adalah warns that this stance delegitimises the Arab vote and reinforces racism.

## Political context
- Netanyahu’s appeal rests on nationalist rhetoric supporting the war in Gaza, the West Bank occupation, and conflicts with Lebanon and Iran.  
- His vulnerabilities include multiple corruption charges, a trial, and criticism for deflecting responsibility for the Oct 7, 2023 attacks and for weakening judicial independence.  
- Analyst Nimrod Flashenberg notes Netanyahu’s resilience but expects his popularity to remain below pre‑Oct 7 levels.

## Outlook
- The Bennett‑Lapid bloc is seen as a semi‑final step in forming an anti‑Netanyahu front; further alliances (e.g., with former chief of staff Gadi Eisenkot) may still shape the final electoral landscape.  
- The alliance’s hard‑line nationalist positioning aims to attract voters supportive of security policies, while its exclusion of Arab parties may limit broader appeal.
