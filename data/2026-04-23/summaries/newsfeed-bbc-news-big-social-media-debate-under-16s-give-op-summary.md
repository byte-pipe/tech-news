# BBC News Big Social Media Debate: Under-16s give opinions on ban

# Summary of “I can’t stop using it” – under‑16s have their say on possible social media ban  

## Background  
- BBC News organised a live debate with 33 pupils (aged 12‑18) from north‑west England to discuss whether social media should be restricted or banned for under‑16s in the UK.  
- The debate took place at Bolton Lads & Girls Club and was part of a wider government consultation on protecting children from harmful online content.  

## Views of the participants  

### Isaac, 13  
- Watches about four hours of TikTok daily, more on weekends.  
- Enjoys football clips, superheroes and gaming.  
- Wants no outright ban; would find it “annoying” but not life‑changing.  

### Iga, 14  
- Exposed to videos about suicide and mental‑health issues; worries younger children may imitate harmful behaviour.  
- Favors age‑based limits (e.g., ban for under‑13s) rather than a total ban for under‑16s.  
- Skeptical that a ban would work; cites Australian survey showing 61 % of 12‑15‑year‑olds still access accounts.  

### Maisie, 14  
- Uses Instagram for 2‑3 hours a day, watches short‑form clips.  
- Supports disabling autoplay and infinite scrolling to reduce “addictive” use.  
- Would even ban short‑form video altogether, believing it shortens attention spans.  

### Peace, 15  
- Values social media for following role models and inspirational content.  
- Opposes a total ban, fearing loss of connection with friends and mentors.  
- Supports stricter age verification and overnight curfews, but finds feature‑blocking less effective.  

## Government stance and consultation  
- MPs have rejected a blanket under‑16 ban three times.  
- Education Minister Olivia Bailey says the issue is “how” to act, not “if”.  
- Shadow Education Secretary Laura Trott urges faster protection from extreme content.  
- A statutory ban on phones in schools has been announced.  
- The consultation will consider academic evidence, especially from Australia’s experience, and will explore less severe restrictions.  

## International example: Australia  
- Six months ago Australia banned new accounts for under‑16s on major platforms and deactivated existing ones.  
- Despite the ban, many youths still retain access, highlighting enforcement challenges.  

## Proposed measures under review  

- **Feature restrictions:** disabling video autoplay, infinite scrolling, or short‑form video to curb addictive patterns.  
- **Age verification:** stronger checks to prevent under‑age account creation.  
- **Overnight curfews:** limiting usage during night hours to improve sleep.  
- **Broader digital tools:** limits on AI chatbots after concerns that they may encourage self‑harm.  

## Key concerns highlighted by participants  

- Exposure to harmful or distressing content (suicide, violence, misogyny).  
- Potential for imitation of risky behaviour seen online.  
- Impact on mental health and attention spans.  
- Balancing protection with the desire for social connection, inspiration, and entertainment.  

## Outlook  
- The government will respond to the consultation in the summer and decide on the final set of online protections for children.  
- Participants like Isaac hope any measures will be limited rather than a full ban, viewing a total prohibition as “annoying” but not essential to their daily lives.
