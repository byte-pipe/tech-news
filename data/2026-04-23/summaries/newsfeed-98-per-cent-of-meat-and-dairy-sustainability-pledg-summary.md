# 98 per cent of meat and dairy sustainability pledges are greenwashing

## Study overview
- Researchers led by Jennifer Jacquet (University of Miami) examined sustainability reports and consumer‑facing websites of 33 of the world’s largest meat and dairy corporations from 2021‑2024.  
- The aim was to distinguish genuine climate action from public‑relations (PR) claims.

## Main findings
- A total of 1,233 environmental claims were identified.  
- 98 % of these claims qualify as greenwashing: vague future promises lacking clear implementation plans.  
- More than two‑thirds of the statements had no supporting evidence; only three were backed by peer‑reviewed scientific literature.  
- 17 of the 33 companies have set net‑zero targets, but the strategies rely heavily on carbon offsets rather than actual emission reductions.  
- Concrete actions reported were minimal, e.g., a regenerative‑agriculture pilot covering just 24 farms (0.0019 % of global operations) and a 3 mm reduction in sausage‑packaging tape width.

## Expert commentary
- Marco Springmann (University of Oxford) described the industry’s claims as “window dressing.”  
- Pete Smith (University of Aberdeen) noted that the results were unsurprising given the sector’s history of greenwashing.  
- Tim Benton (University of Leeds) highlighted systemic incentives for large firms to over‑promise and maintain the status quo, drawing parallels with tobacco and fossil‑fuel industries.

## Implications
- The prevalence of greenwashing undermines public trust and may delay meaningful climate action in animal agriculture, which accounts for at least 16.5 % of global greenhouse‑gas emissions.  
- Effective mitigation will require transparent, evidence‑based measures rather than reliance on offsets or minor packaging tweaks.
