# Treetops glowing during storms captured on film for first time

## Overview
- Corona discharges are tiny electrical pulses that cause tree leaves to glow in ultraviolet (UV) during thunderstorms.
- For over 70 years the phenomenon was only hypothesized; this study provides the first direct observation in nature.
- Results were published in *Geophysical Research Letters*.

## Research Expedition
- Team: William Brune (professor), Patrick McFarland (doctoral candidate), Jena Jenkins (assistant research professor), David Miller (research lab specialist).
- Field work began in June 2024, traveling the U.S. east coast in a modified 2013 Toyota Sienna equipped with a telescopic weather device.
- Initial attempts in Florida yielded few storms; the breakthrough occurred near Interstate 95, at the University of North Carolina at Pembroke, North Carolina.
- Instruments were aimed at a 100‑ft sweetgum tree and a nearby loblolly pine during a two‑hour thunderstorm.

## Findings
- Captured 859 corona events on the sweetgum and 93 on the loblolly pine; events lasted from a brief blink to several seconds.
- Observed coronae on four additional thunderstorms and four other tree species.
- UV camera, coupled with a Newtonian “Corona Observing Telescope System,” isolated corona emissions by blocking solar UV wavelengths.
- Laboratory work had previously linked corona UV emissions to the production of hydroxyl radicals (OH), a major atmospheric oxidizer.

## Implications
- Hydroxyl radicals break down water vapor, volatile organic compounds, and methane, contributing to atmospheric cleansing.
- Corona discharges may affect leaf health (visible damage at emission points) and have broader effects on forest and atmospheric chemistry.
- The phenomenon is nearly invisible to the naked eye but becomes evident with specialized instrumentation.

## Future Work
- Open questions: Do corona discharges harm or benefit trees? Have trees adapted to this process? What is the net impact on forest health and atmospheric cleaning?
- Planned collaborations with tree ecologists and biologists to explore ecological and physiological consequences.
