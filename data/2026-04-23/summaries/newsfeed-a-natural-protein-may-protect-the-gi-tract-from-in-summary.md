# A natural protein may protect the GI tract from infection

## Key Findings
- Lectins are mucosal proteins that bind sugars on cell surfaces; intelectin‑2 is one such lectin.
- Intelectin‑2 specifically binds galactose on bacterial membranes, trapping bacteria and disrupting their cell membranes, leading to bacterial death.
- The protein also binds galactose in mucins, strengthening the mucus layer that lines the intestine.

## Dual Protective Mechanism
- **Mucus stabilization:** By attaching to mucins, intelectin‑2 reinforces the protective mucus barrier.
- **Direct antimicrobial action:** When the barrier is breached, intelectin‑2 can neutralize or restrain escaping bacteria.

## Potential Applications
- Could be developed into a novel antimicrobial agent against hard‑to‑treat pathogens such as *Staphylococcus aureus* and *Klebsiella pneumoniae*.
- Restoring appropriate levels of intelectin‑2 may benefit individuals with inflammatory bowel disease, where either deficiency (weakened barrier) or excess (loss of beneficial microbes) is problematic.

## Research Team
- Led by MIT chemistry professor Laura Kiessling.
- Collaborators include former MIT postdoc Amanda Dugan and PhD candidate Deepsing Syangtan.

## Outlook
- Leveraging human lectins like intelectin‑2 offers a new strategy to combat antimicrobial resistance by harnessing innate immune defenses already present in the body.
