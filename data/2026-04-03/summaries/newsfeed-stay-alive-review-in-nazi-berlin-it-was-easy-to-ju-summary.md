# Stay Alive review: In Nazi Berlin, it was easy to just go along

## Overview
- Historian Ian Buruma’s *Stay Alive: Berlin, 1939‑1945* examines everyday life in Nazi Berlin through diaries, memoirs and interviews, many of which are now lost.
- The narrative is anchored by the experience of Buruma’s Dutch father, Leo, who was forced to work in a Berlin factory.
- The book spans the early “normal” period after the invasion of Poland, the intensifying hardships of war, and the city’s collapse under Allied bombing and Soviet occupation.

## Main Themes
- **Moral compromise:** The review stresses that living under a totalitarian regime inevitably corrupts ordinary people, blurring their moral compass.
- **Conformity:** Most Berliners, including Buruma’s father, chose to “stay alive” by doing their jobs, seeking pleasure and avoiding the atrocities around them.
- **Resistance vs. acquiescence:** While a few, such as journalist Ruth Andreas‑Friedrich, actively resisted, the majority turned away from the horrors.

## Notable Characters Highlighted
- **Coco Schumann:** Young Jewish guitarist who risked his life to play “degenerate” jazz.
- **Lilo:** Fifteen‑year‑old who initially embraces Nazi ideals but later admires the conspirators against Hitler.
- **Helmuth von Moltke:** Conservative intelligence officer who tried to undermine the regime from within and was executed.
- **Erich Alenfeld:** Converted Jew who wrote to Hermann Göring asking to serve Germany.
- **Ruth Andreas‑Friedrich:** Journalist who led the “Uncle Emil” resistance network, helping Jews escape and supporting other anti‑Nazi groups.

## Illustrative Details from the Book
- A family trained a parrot to say “Heil, Hitler” to mislead patrols.
- Filmmakers shot a movie without film to avoid conscription.
- Jewish villas in Grunewald were seized by Nazis and later owned by Russian oligarchs.
- Some dissidents hid in the city’s underworld, including brothels, earning the nickname “U‑boats.”

## Author’s Assessment (John Powers)
- Buruma avoids re‑hashing familiar Nazi horrors, instead offering two “dark truths” relevant to contemporary authoritarian trends:
  1. **Systemic corruption:** Even well‑meaning individuals become complicit.
  2. **Ease of compliance:** Most people simply go along, a disturbing but unsurprising human tendency.
- The reviewer admires Ruth Andreas‑Friedrich for her bravery without self‑righteousness, seeing her as the book’s moral anchor.

## Conclusion
- *Stay Alive* provides a nuanced portrait of ordinary Berliners, showing how daily survival often meant silent participation in a genocidal regime.
- The work serves as a cautionary reminder that authoritarianism thrives when people choose convenience over conscience.
