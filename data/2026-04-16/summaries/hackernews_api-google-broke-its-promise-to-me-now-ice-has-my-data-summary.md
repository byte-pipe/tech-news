# Google Broke Its Promise to Me. Now ICE Has My Data. | Electronic Frontier Foundation

**Google's Broken Promise: A Student's Long-Overdue Investigation into Deceptive Data Handling Practices**

In September 2024, Amandla Thomas-Johnson, a Ph.D. student studying in the US on a student visa, had an unexpected experience with Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE). Despite having attended a pro-Palestinian protest for just five minutes, ICE sent Google an administrative subpoena requesting his data. This prompted Google to deliver Thomas-Johnson's information anonymously to ICE without giving him a chance to challenge the request.

This incident marks a stark deviation from Google's previous promise to notify users before handing over their data in response to legal processes, like administrative subpoenas. Google had previously assured users that they would provide timely and fair notice regarding requests for user data.

Today, Thomas-Johnson is seeking investigations into Google's alleged deceptive trade practices for breaking this long-standing promise. The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) has filed a complaint with the California Attorney General and New York Attorney General to press for an inquiry into the matter.

**Analysis of Breaking Its Promise**

Thomas-Johnson's experience highlights several key issues with Google's data handling practices:

*   **Lack of notice**: Thomas-Johnson was not given adequate notice before his data was handed over to ICE, violating Google's promise to provide timely notification.
*   **Unfair timing and circumstance**: The conditions under which the subpoena was issued (a short incident of pro-Palestinian activism) may have been unfair or unreasonable depending on Google's policies.
*   **Deception and lack of transparency**: Google's actions were deceptive and secretive, making it difficult for Thomas-Johnson to challenge the request in a timely manner.

**Implications and Next Steps**

This case raises important questions about data handling practices, corporate responsibility, and consumer protection.
