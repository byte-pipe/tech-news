# AI needs solid botanical data more than ever

# Summary of “AI needs solid botanical data more than ever”

## Background
- The author graduated in 2017 from one of the last stand‑alone botany departments in the United States.  
- Their undergraduate training in plant and fungal taxonomy underpins current PhD research in fungal ecology.  
- The botany department has since been merged into a generic biology programme, reducing specialized training opportunities.

## Importance of Taxonomy
- Accurate identification of organisms (e.g., underground truffles) requires years of hands‑on experience with morphology, scent, microscopic spore features, and host relationships.  
- General‑biology curricula often allocate only a single day to plant or fungal studies, insufficient for developing taxonomic expertise.  
- Reliable taxonomy is essential for monitoring and protecting biodiversity; without it, species cannot be meaningfully tracked.

## AI and Biotechnology Context
- Large language models (LLMs) are trained on scientific literature that contains formally named species.  
- Vast portions of Earth’s biodiversity, especially fungi (with <10 % formally described), lack scientific names, limiting AI’s ability to learn about them.  
- Tech companies (e.g., Anthropic’s $400 M acquisition of Coefficient Bio, OpenAI’s life‑science investments) are moving into biology for drug discovery, protein design, and agriculture.

## Risks of Incomplete Data
- AI models lacking species‑level identification may fail to distinguish harmless organisms from toxic ones.  
- Gaps in taxonomic knowledge can jeopardize public safety in drug discovery, agricultural management, and biosurveillance applications.

## Call to Action
- Preserve and expand specialist training in botany and zoology to prevent loss of taxonomic expertise.  
- Recognize the necessity of solid botanical and fungal data for the success of AI‑driven biotechnology initiatives.
