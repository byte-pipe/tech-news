# AI influencers are ‘everywhere’ at Coachella

## Overview
- Coachella has become a showcase for influencers, both human and synthetic, eager to display “festival experiences.”
- Generative‑AI tools now create avatars that are hard to distinguish from real attendees, leading to a flood of AI‑generated posts.

## Prominent AI avatars and their tactics
- **Ammarathegoat** – ~170 k Instagram followers; no AI disclosure; posts AI‑generated images with the Kardashians, Bretman Rock, James Charles, and Coachella backdrops; visual glitches hint at synthetic origin.  
- **Grannyspills** – >2 M followers; avatar built with Higgsfield AI; labeled “digital creator” but AI info hidden in a menu; videos appear unnaturally smooth, images overly polished.  
- **Lil Miquela** – one of the earliest and biggest AI influencers; “interviewed” J Balvin at Coachella 2019 and appears again this year.  
- **Miazelu** and **Anazelu** – 252 k and 312 k followers respectively; post realistic festival shots; bios state “AI influencer,” yet individual posts lack explicit disclosure.  
- Male‑presenting AI accounts tend to disclose their synthetic nature because they sell prompting guides and AI‑creation tools to followers.

## Why AI influencers attend (or pretend to attend) Coachella
- The festival is dubbed the “Influencer Olympics,” where creators chase brand deals, agency representation, and audience growth.  
- Featuring celebrities in AI‑generated posts boosts reach and taps into fan bases of real stars.  
- Some accounts link to adult‑content platforms (OnlyFans, Fanvue) and use fabricated festival images to attract subscribers.  
- Lack of clear disclosures leaves many viewers unable to tell real from fake, fueling speculation about “Dead Internet” theories and general gullibility.

## Industry and ethical implications
- Brands already spend “high six figures” to send human creators to Coachella; AI avatars could offer a cheaper, fully controllable alternative.  
- The rise of undisclosed synthetic influencers blurs the line between authentic experience and manufactured reality, potentially exploiting audiences who cannot attend in person.  
- As AI avatars achieve micro‑celebrity status, the trend is likely to continue unless platforms enforce clearer labeling or disclosure standards.
