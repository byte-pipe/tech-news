# AEO is not SEO 2.0 – The Future of SEO

## The comparison between SEO and AEO is a trap  
- Treating AEO as a simple evolution of SEO leads to optimizing for a user journey that no longer exists in an AI‑first environment.  
- LLM responses look like search results, but the underlying mechanics are fundamentally different.

## Rankings don’t exist in LLMs  
- Visibility in LLMs is not tied to keyword rankings or a “page one.”  
- The surface area of presence is infinite and disaggregated across unpredictable prompts.  
- Clicks are no longer the primary KPI; the conversion funnel built around clicks loses relevance.

## Visibility can’t be manufactured as it is in search  
- LLM visibility resembles brand marketing more than traditional SEO.  
- Short‑term hacks may work briefly, but models eventually filter out manipulation.  
- Established, frequently cited brands are favored; brand recognition becomes a prerequisite.

## AEO needs SEO, but SEO alone isn’t enough  
- Technical crawlability, structured data, and coherent site architecture remain essential for AI scrapers to ingest content.  
- Without solid SEO foundations, a site may be ignored or misrepresented by LLMs.  
- AEO is an extension of SEO, not a replacement.

## Links are not citations  
- Treating citations as new backlinks is misleading.  
- LLMs evaluate brand signals and the reputability of information, not just domain authority scores.  
- Success depends on being the trusted source the model uses to form its understanding, not merely on high DA or link volume.

## LLMs are personalized  
- Traditional search results were relatively consistent across users, enabling rank tracking as a KPI.  
- LLM responses vary with personal context, prompt phrasing, conversation history, platform, and internal weighting—making “prompt tracking” an unreliable analogue to rank tracking.

## What this means for measurement  
- Legacy SEO metrics (rankings, clicks) no longer map to the AEO reality.  
- Success should be measured by brand authority inputs: third‑party mentions, customer loyalty, and brand recognition.  
- Real marketing research—asking customers how they discovered you—becomes essential for proving impact.
