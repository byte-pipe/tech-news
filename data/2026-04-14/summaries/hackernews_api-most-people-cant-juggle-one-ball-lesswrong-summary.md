# Most people can't juggle one ball — LessWrong

## Introduction

## How to juggle
### Step 0 – Ready stance
- Feet and hands shoulder‑width apart, elbows at 90°, head slightly up, shoulders relaxed.

### Step 1 – One ball
- Throw from one hand to the other so the ball peaks just above eye level and lands between the fingers.  
- Keep the throw impulse mainly from the elbow; let the catch “self‑catch.”  
- Practice until throws are accurate without moving the catching hand.

#### Common one‑ball mistakes
- Reaching up to catch reduces available time.  
- Ignoring the ball or staring at it continuously; a brief glance at the apex is optimal.

### Step 2 – Two balls
- Start with one ball in each hand.  
- Throw the first ball, wait as long as possible, then throw the second ball on an inner arc (closer to the first hand) rather than a simple pass.  
- After the second throw, move the hand outward to catch the first ball.  
- Practice starting with both right‑hand and left‑hand leads.

#### Common two‑ball mistakes
- Throwing the second ball too early; it should be triggered by the first ball’s landing.  
- Using different heights for the two throws.  
- Throwing the second ball behind the first instead of inside the arc.

### Step 3 – Three balls
- Begin with two balls in one hand, one in the other.  
- Throw from the hand with two balls, then perform a two‑ball swap with the opposite hand.  
- Continue swapping until each hand holds one ball and the third ball is caught – a “flash.”  
- Increase the number of swaps before the final catch; six catches (2 × 3) constitute a qualified three‑ball cascade.

#### Common three‑ball mistakes
- Throwing too fast, leaving insufficient processing time; return to two‑ball practice.  
- Throwing balls forward; using a table, bed, or wall as a barrier helps keep the pattern vertical.

## Tricks
- **Outside throw**: throw on the outside of the incoming ball; combine with inside throws for patterns like “tennis.”  
- Other beginner tricks: under‑arm, behind‑the‑back, and overhead throws/catches.  
- Recommended pattern: **Mill’s Mess**, where balls follow each other in a visually appealing way.

## Numbers (advancing to more balls)
- **3 balls**: a few hours to a week of practice.  
- **4 balls**: about one month; learn a two‑ball fountain in each hand and then juggle both simultaneously (even‑ball fountain).  
- **5 balls**: took the author four years; same cascade principle but with faster timing and three crossing balls in mid‑air.  
- **6 balls**: easier than 5, but most jugglers jump from 5 to **7 balls** after mastering the cascade, as the skill set aligns better with the next odd number.  

The guide emphasizes patient, incremental practice, focusing on consistent arcs, timing, and avoiding premature or mis‑aligned throws.
