# The Economics of Software Teams: Why Most Organizations Are Flying Blind

## Overview of Team Costs
- An engineer in Western Europe costs roughly **€130,000 per year** (salary, taxes, pension, equipment, office, management overhead).  
- A team of **8 engineers** therefore incurs:
  - **Annual cost:** €1,040,000  
  - **Monthly cost:** €86,667 (≈ €87k)  
  - **Daily cost:** €4,000  
- Most engineers and their managers are unaware of these figures, and the numbers rarely enter prioritisation discussions.

## Internal Platform Team – Financial Logic
- Mission: build and maintain a developer platform for ~100 other engineers.  
- Break‑even calculation:
  - Platform must save the 100 engineers **1,340 hours per month** (≈ 13.4 h per engineer, ~3 h per week).  
  - At €65 per working hour, this equals the team’s monthly cost (€86,667).  
- Realistic viability threshold:
  - Because initiatives succeed only 50‑70 % of the time, the team must generate **3–5 ×** its cost.  
  - Required monthly value: **€260,000 – €433,000**.  
  - This means the platform must be truly transformative, not just marginally helpful.

## Customer‑Facing Product Team – Same Math, Different Levers
- Monthly cost identical to the platform team (€86,667).  
- Revenue‑per‑user assumption: **€50/month**.  
- Break‑even requires value equivalent to **1,740 users**; 3‑5 × threshold needs **5,000 – 8,700 users**.  
- Key levers:
  - **Churn reduction:** 2 % monthly churn on 50,000 users = 1,000 users lost → €50,000 at risk. Preventing this covers most of break‑even.
  - **Activation improvement:** Raising activation from 30 % to 35 % on 10,000 sign‑ups adds 500 users → €25,000 extra revenue (≈ 29 % of break‑even).
  - **Sales conversion:** Increasing free‑to‑paid conversion from 4 % to 4.5 % adds 100 paying users → €5,000 extra revenue.  
- Small gains across multiple metrics compound, but only when teams understand the financial impact of each lever.

## Why Visibility Is Missing
- Over the past two decades, organizations have treated engineering headcount as a generic asset without tying it to concrete financial outcomes.  
- Decision‑makers lack the cost context, leading to choices (feature work, platform rebuilds, operational delays) that are evaluated without measurable monetary consequences.

## Implications for Organizations (including the rise of LLMs)
- Without clear cost and value metrics, teams cannot prioritize high‑impact work, risking wasted capital.  
- The emergence of large language models (LLMs) offers new productivity tools, but their ROI must be measured against the same financial thresholds (break‑even and 3‑5 × return).  
- To move from “flying blind” to financially informed engineering, organizations should:
  - Publish and track per‑engineer cost figures.  
  - Quantify time‑saved or revenue‑protected outcomes for each initiative.  
  - Apply the 3‑5 × viability benchmark when evaluating platform or product investments.
