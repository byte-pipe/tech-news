# An Interview with OpenAI CEO Sam Altman and AWS CEO Matt Garman About Bedrock Managed Agents

## Context and Embargo Situation
- The interview was conducted on Friday under embargo; the embargo was lifted after Microsoft announced changes to its agreement with OpenAI on Monday.
- Initial question addressed how Bedrock Managed Agents fit with Microsoft’s exclusive Azure access to OpenAI models.
- Microsoft’s announcement clarified that OpenAI can now serve products on any cloud, including AWS.

## Revised Microsoft‑OpenAI Agreement (Key Points)
- Azure remains the primary cloud partner; OpenAI products ship first on Azure unless Azure cannot support required capabilities.
- OpenAI may serve its products on any cloud provider.
- Microsoft retains a non‑exclusive license to OpenAI IP for models and products through 2032.
- Microsoft will no longer pay a revenue share to OpenAI.
- OpenAI continues to pay a revenue share to Microsoft through 2030, capped at a fixed percentage.
- Microsoft stays a major shareholder and continues direct participation in OpenAI’s growth.

## Strategic Implications
- Azure’s exclusivity previously gave it a competitive edge but limited OpenAI’s reach to enterprises preferring other clouds.
- The change reduces Azure’s differentiation but improves Microsoft’s financial outlook by removing the revenue‑share expense.
- OpenAI sees AWS as a large growth opportunity, willing to forgo Azure‑related revenue for the next few years.
- The AGI clause has been removed, allowing the partnership to run through 2032 regardless of technological milestones.

## Focus on AWS: Bedrock Managed Agents
- Bedrock Managed Agents are positioned as “Codex in AWS,” leveraging local execution for security and complexity handling.
- The offering aims to simplify agent workflows for organizations that already store most data in AWS.
- It differs from Amazon’s existing AgentCore by providing a managed, cloud‑native experience.

## Interview Highlights

### AWS and Startups
- Matt Garman compares the AI wave to the early days of AWS: both lower barriers to entry, allowing small teams to build quickly with powerful tools.
- The excitement comes from enabling creators to do things previously reserved for large enterprises with massive infrastructure budgets.

### Differences Between Commodity Compute and AI
- AI training requires vertically integrated superclusters, advanced networking, and tight software‑hardware coupling, unlike the more fungible, elastic nature of traditional compute.
- Rapid adoption and scale of AI present new challenges compared to the early AWS era.

### Topics Covered in the Interview
- **AWS and Startups** – parallels between early cloud adoption and current AI democratization.
- **Bedrock Managed Agents** – definition, benefits, and distinction from AgentCore.
- **Local vs. Cloud** – trade‑offs in execution environments for AI agents.
- **Trainium** – discussion on why custom chips may matter less for most AI users.
- **Customer Demand & AI Stack** – how enterprise needs shape the AI ecosystem and the rationale for partnering versus full integration (e.g., Google’s approach).
