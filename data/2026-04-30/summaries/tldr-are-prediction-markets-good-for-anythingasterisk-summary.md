# Are Prediction Markets Good for Anything?

## Introduction
- Prediction markets are often dismissed as casinos, but their potential value lies in the data they generate.  
- Early endorsements from Nobel laureates (Arrow, Kahneman) and economists (Hayek, Hanson) argued that markets can aggregate dispersed knowledge and improve decision‑making.  

## Theoretical Foundations
- **Hayek (1945):** Markets efficiently combine local, tacit information via prices.  
- **Hanson (2000):** Proposed “futarchy,” using markets to evaluate policy outcomes.  
- **Tetlock, Mellers, Scoblic:** Promoted forecasting tournaments for intelligence and depolarizing politics.  

## Current Landscape (2026)
- Public platforms such as Polymarket and Kalshi handle billions in monthly volume.  
- Over 80‑90 % of this activity is concentrated on sports, cryptocurrency, or election betting, not on questions that generate useful societal information.  
- Concerns about gambling addiction and insider trading dominate the discourse, while the promised informational benefits remain under‑examined.  

## Demand for Market Information
- The usefulness of forecasts can be gauged by external demand: firms, media, policymakers, and the public reading the market data.  
- Vitalik Buterin’s analogy: for bettors it’s a betting site; for non‑bettors it functions as a news source.  

## Five Categories of Potential Benefits
1. **Risk monitoring** – early alerts (e.g., COVID‑19 outbreak) that can alter personal or organizational plans.  
2. **News interpretation** – quantifying how events (e.g., Strait of Hormuz closure) shift probabilities of larger outcomes (e.g., US recession).  
3. **Policy‑outcome planning** – informing decisions such as potential TikTok bans.  
4. **Accountability for leaders** – providing probabilistic checks on statements (e.g., Trump’s Iran strike comment).  
5. **Discovery of novel information** – tracking milestones like major AI breakthroughs.  

## Evaluation of the Five Benefits

### Risk‑Monitoring
- Strong alignment of supply (bettors) and demand (readers).  
- Approximately 2,821 risk‑monitoring markets with $3.8 billion total volume; median market volume ≈ $82 k.  
- Geopolitical risk dominates; 199 conflict markets resolve daily/weekly, offering near‑real‑time escalation tracking.  
- Energy traders, shipping firms, and mainstream media cite these markets, confirming demand.  

### Blind Spots and Limitations
- Prediction markets rarely originate news stories; they tend to reflect already prominent events.  
- Health and climate risk markets receive minimal participation and volume, limiting their reliability.  
- Example: Kalshi’s COVID‑19 vaccine adoption markets averaged only $8 k per market and missed key outcomes (e.g., German case surge). No institutional adopters emerged for these signals.  

## Observations on Market Dynamics
- Large spikes in volume (e.g., $400 m on Trump’s inauguration, $327 m on Romania’s election scandal) are tied to high‑profile political events, not to systematic risk tracking.  
- The “flashy” nature of certain topics attracts bettors but does not guarantee the generation of novel, actionable intelligence.  

## Conclusion
- Prediction markets deliver valuable risk‑monitoring information when there is clear external demand, especially for geopolitical events.  
- Their broader promise—enhancing news interpretation, policy planning, leader accountability, and discovery of novel insights—remains largely unrealized due to low participation in non‑spectacle domains such as health and climate.  
- To fulfill the original vision, markets need larger, more diverse bettor bases and institutional consumers willing to integrate market signals into decision‑making processes.
