# Maryland becomes first state to ban surveillance pricing in grocery stores

## Main provisions
- The law prohibits grocery stores and third‑party delivery services from using a shopper’s personal data (location, internet search history, demographics, etc.) to set higher prices for that individual.
- It was signed by Governor Wes Moore, who framed the measure as protecting consumers from predictive analytics that enable companies to charge the most each person is willing to pay.
- Exemptions include loyalty‑program discounts and promotional offers; the statute does not restrict a retailer from raising prices for everyone and then offering individualized discounts that achieve the same effect.
- Enforcement is limited to the state attorney general; private citizens cannot bring lawsuits under the law.

## Context and broader landscape
- Surveillance (dynamic) pricing has been documented by the FTC in sectors such as clothing, beauty, home goods, and hardware, but grocery pricing is seen as especially urgent because it affects food affordability.
- The FTC has opened a federal investigation but, according to its current chair, the prior administration’s report was a “rush job,” suggesting limited federal action.
- Bills with similar aims are being considered in Colorado, California, Massachusetts, Illinois, and New Jersey.

## Reactions and criticism
- **Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC)**: welcomes the ban but warns that industry‑drafted carve‑outs make the law harder for consumers to detect and enforce.
- **Consumer Reports**: praises the focus on the issue but calls the enforcement provisions “weak” and urges legislative revisions to close loopholes.
- **American Economic Liberties Project**: stresses that the lack of a private right of action undermines accountability and could set a weak model for other states.
- **Instacart**: announced it will stop using technology that enables individualized pricing, claims it never engaged in the practice, and supports the law’s core principle.

## Potential implications
- The law may serve as a template for other states; critics fear it could become a “permission slip” for continued discriminatory pricing if the loopholes are not addressed.
- Strengthening enforcement mechanisms and removing exemptions are seen as essential steps for the legislation to achieve its consumer‑protective goals.
