# Twelve centuries of cherry blossoms — peak bloom in Kyoto, 812–2026  

## Overview  
- The peak bloom dates of Kyoto’s cherry trees have been recorded for more than a millennium, covering **1,215 years** with **838 observations**.  
- This constitutes the longest continuous record of any natural phenomenon on Earth.  

## How to read the chart  
- Each pale dot represents a single recorded peak (the day a designated tree was judged to be at full bloom).  
- A 30‑year rolling mean (the rose line) traces a coherent climate signal across the whole span.  
- **Little Ice Age**: gradual shift to later peaks from the 14th to the 19th centuries.  
- **Modern era**: from around 1900 the line moves earlier; by the late 20th century it falls below any value seen during the Heian period.  
- **2026 peak**: March 29, more than two weeks earlier than the pre‑modern average.

## Source and method  
- Phenological observations compiled by Yasuyuki Aono (Osaka Prefecture University) from imperial diaries, monastery records, and modern meteorological data.  
- Data archived at NOAA Paleoclimatology and accessed via Our World in Data (CC‑BY).  
- The 30‑year rolling mean is calculated where at least five observations fall inside the window.
