# Doctors dismissed my endometriosis as anxiety

## Overview
- Endometriosis affects >1.5 million women in the UK; average diagnosis delay is 9 years 4 months.  
- 39 % of women saw their GP ≥10 times before endometriosis was considered; 46 % of hospital attendances ended with no treatment.  
- Many patients report being told their pain is IBS, anxiety, or that they should simply take the contraceptive pill.

## Amy Peckham‑Driver (31, Suffolk)
- First severe pain at 14, passed out in a toilet; doctors dismissed endometriosis because she was “too young”.  
- Treated with contraceptive pills for years; finally diagnosed at 27 with deep infiltrating endometriosis affecting all pelvic organs.  
- Underwent private surgery; surgeon said her pelvis “looked like a bomb had gone off”.  
- Condition continues to progress; now faces risk of bowel obstruction, possible kidney failure, and is on an urgent‑case NHS surgery list.  
- Egg freezing led to IVF rejection due to low egg supply after surgeries; she blames delayed diagnosis for her infertility.  
- Advocates for better GP training and more research; helps organise “Let’s Talk Women’s Health”.

## Ami Clarke (28, Bedfordshire)
- Symptoms began at 13; diagnosed a decade later.  
- Endured up to 18 days/month of unbearable pain; multiple contraceptive pills and a Mirena coil gave little relief.  
- First laparoscopy at 23 reduced pain temporarily; paid £10 000 for private robotic surgery in 2022, which also provided only short‑term improvement.  
- Runs a Facebook support group; wants endometriosis recognised as a “full‑body inflammatory disease”.

## Faye Ramsey (31, Ipswich)
- Collapsed with severe stomach pain at a wedding 11 years ago; repeatedly told it was IBS or stress because she already had two children.  
- Laproscopy in 2023 initially reported “no endometriosis”, then later scans showed adhesions on her bowels.  
- Describes feeling powerless and worries about being a good mother.  
- Calls for school education on the condition and for doctors to move beyond prescribing contraceptives; collects women’s health stories for the government.

## Mairead Morgan (41, Essex)
- Symptoms started at 11; diagnosed at 38 after a ruptured cyst required laparoscopy.  
- Uses yoga and cold‑water swimming to ease pain; still experiences flare‑ups, worsened by perimenopause.  
- Career impacted but she continues to push forward; knits during painful periods.

## NHS and Endometriosis UK response
- NHS acknowledges that many women’s experiences “aren’t good enough” and is working to roll out more convenient services.  
- Emma Cox, CEO of Endometriosis UK, states that care has been neglected for too long and urges governments to act urgently on awareness, training, and funding.
