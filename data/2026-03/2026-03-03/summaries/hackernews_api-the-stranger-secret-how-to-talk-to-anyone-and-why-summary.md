# The stranger secret: how to talk to anyone – and why you should  

## Personal encounters that sparked the idea  
- On a quiet train a woman in her 70s asked to sit together; the author listened to her for most of a 50‑minute journey.  
- Later, at a restaurant, the author chatted with a shy Korean waitress about home and food.  
- These moments raised the question of how to know the “right” boundaries when speaking to strangers.  

## Why talking to strangers has become rare  
- Public spaces (pubs, queues, transport) now feel empty of casual conversation.  
- Contributing factors: headphones, smartphones, touch‑screen ordering, remote work, pandemic habits, and a “social norm reinforcement” loop where silence begets more silence.  
- Individual reasons include neurodivergence, introversion, discomfort with eye contact, and aversion to small talk.  

## Consequences of the decline  
- Loss of a basic human skill: the ability to initiate and sustain spontaneous dialogue.  
- Experts warn of broader effects:  
  - Dr Jared Cooney Horvath notes Gen Z underperforms previous generations on cognitive measures.  
  - Dr Rangan Chatterjee links low self‑worth in children to poor conversational ability.  
  - Psychologist Esther Perel calls it a “global relational recession,” emphasizing the need for practice to strengthen social muscles.  

## Social‑media experiments and their pitfalls  
- Videos of strangers being approached (jokes, compliments, “talk to strangers” challenges) are popular.  
- These clips are often performative, individualistic, and sometimes exploitative, turning genuine interaction into content for clicks.  
- The commodification can make authentic conversation feel even more alienating and reinforce the perception that talking to anyone is a daunting, staged act.  

## The underlying message  
- Many people have stopped taking chances on others and on themselves, fearing rejection or misunderstanding.  
- Re‑engaging in spontaneous conversation restores personal enrichment and benefits society by rebuilding the “social muscles” that keep communities connected.
