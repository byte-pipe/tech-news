# Analytics Engineering’s Unfinished Work

## Introduction
- I became an analytics engineer to fill a gap in how organizations understand their data, but left when the role stopped evolving toward that promise.
- I see a new window of opportunity and want to prevent the role from closing again.

## What an Analytics Engineer Is
- Captures how a business understands itself and gives that understanding structure.
- Identifies meaningful entities, defines relationships, and formalizes measurements.
- Turns tacit knowledge scattered across people and tools into explicit, reusable, trustworthy assets.
- Tools, frameworks, and job descriptions are expressions of this core spirit.

## The Best Ones I’ve Worked With
- Possess an instinct to make data make sense before leaving it alone.
- Obsess over correct definitions and relentlessly ensure models reflect business reality.
- Treat data modeling with the same seriousness as software architects treat code architecture.
- Their practice raises the ceiling for the role without lowering the floor.

## Where It Stagnated
- Semantic layers and data‑modeling conversations matured, but the role’s identity hardened around dbt.
- “Analytics engineer” became synonymous with “writes SQL in dbt,” limiting scope.
- The broader spirit of the role was eclipsed by a single tool.

## Where Logic Lives
- **Dimensional data model (structural logic):** defines entities, relationships, and grain; captures hierarchies and shapes the business.
- **Semantic layer (measurement logic):** defines metrics, filters, and department‑specific definitions (e.g., “active customer”).
- **AI context layer (interpretive logic):** holds institutional memory, unwritten understandings, and nuanced meanings that don’t fit structured definitions.
- Deliberate scoping of each layer prevents logic from leaking where it doesn’t belong.

## Same Core Skill Across Tools
- Context engineering, semantic layers, and AI agents all require a deep care for capturing business understanding and structuring it for humans and machines.
- The best analytics engineers have always performed this work, even when tools were narrower and layers less explicit.
- Stakes are higher now: loose definitions can mislead AI agents that directly inform executive decisions.

## The Second Chance
- The role is uniquely positioned to address current problems that demand meaning, structure, and legibility across all layers.
- This is a second chance after the first stagnation; the emerging challenges align with the role’s original purpose.
- Call to action: pick a frequent stakeholder question (e.g., “how many active customers?”) and trace what an AI agent would need to answer it correctly. Identify breaks in data modeling, metric ownership, or contextual understanding—those are the work opportunities for analytics engineers.

## Where I Landed, and Where I’m Looking
- Returned to a data‑engineer title but continued modeling, metric definition, and cross‑layer knowledge structuring.
- The title is secondary; the work remains the same.
- I hope analytics engineering evolves into the role it was always meant to be, leveraging the people and potential that already exist.
