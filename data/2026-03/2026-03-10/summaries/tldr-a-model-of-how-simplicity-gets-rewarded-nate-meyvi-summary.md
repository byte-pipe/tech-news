# A model of how simplicity gets rewarded

## Why rewarding simplicity is hard
- Few engineers and even fewer managers value simplicity.  
- Those who do value it often cannot recognize it.  
- Simplicity’s benefits are invisible and the failure modes of complex systems are unpredictable, making advantages hard to communicate.  
- Building simple systems can limit future work opportunities, which are professionally valuable.  
- Simple systems rarely use trendy tools, which are often seen as valuable and fun.  
- Complex solutions are more legible to institutions.

## A simplified institutional model
1. The organization does not try to directly measure or recognize simplicity or other high‑judgment quality aspects.  
2. It cultivates a high‑trust culture of expert individual contributors.  
3. Those experts informally assess how well their peers perform in high‑judgment areas.  
4. During performance reviews, each person allocates a scalar amount of positive recognition to peers.  
5. The scalar points are translated into institutionally legible notes about high‑profile projects and measurable effects, rather than explicit scores.

## Takeaway
- The model is an abstraction, not a precise description of most workplaces.  
- It shows that simplicity and other subtle qualities can be rewarded indirectly, even when they are hard to measure directly.
