# Singing dogs may show the evolutionary roots of musicality

## Study overview
- Psychologist Aniruddh Patel and colleagues examined whether dogs can perceive pitch and modify their howls accordingly.
- The work was inspired by observations of wolf pack howling, where individuals appear to aim for different tones, creating a discordant chorus that may deter predators.

## Method
- Dog owners recorded their pets howling to a favorite track in three versions: original key, transposed three semitones higher, and transposed three semitones lower.
- The study focused on two ancient breeds thought to be closer to wolves: Samoyeds and Shiba Inus.
- Each dog had to produce at least 30 howls lasting a minimum of one second for each pitch condition to be included in the analysis.

## Findings
- All four Samoyeds demonstrated sensitivity to the pitch changes, consistently adjusting their vocalisations to the new key, though they did not match the exact pitch.
- The two Shiba Inus showed no adjustment, appearing tone‑deaf.
- Patel suggests possible genetic variation within ancient breeds that could influence predisposition to pitch modulation, while acknowledging that a larger sample might reveal more musicality.

## Implications for the evolution of music
- The results hint that the ability to coordinate pitch with others may have very ancient evolutionary roots, not necessarily dependent on speech or complex vocal learning.
- Dogs seemed engaged with the music, gazing into the distance rather than seeking a reward, indicating that music may act as a surrogate howl‑like signal that puts them in a social, joining mode.

## Expert commentary
- Buddhamas Pralle Kriengwatana (KU Leuven) finds the findings intriguing but calls for more varied samples and a comparison between ancient and modern breeds.
- She notes that the dogs’ imperfect pitch matching could reflect a desire to be heard rather than to replicate the exact tone.
