# Is legal the same as legitimate: AI reimplementation and the erosion of copyleft

## Background
- Dan Blanchard released chardet 7.0, a 48‑times faster rewrite of the Python library, changing its license from LGPL to MIT.  
- The rewrite was generated by feeding the original API and test suite to Anthropic’s Claude, which produced code with less than 1.3 % similarity to prior versions.  
- Original author Mark Pilgrim objected, arguing that the LGPL requires derivative works to remain under the same license and that the AI‑assisted rewrite is not a clean‑room effort.  
- Open‑source figures Armin Ronacher (Flask creator) and Salvatore Sanfilippo (antirez, Redis creator) defended the relicensing, each concluding that Blanchard’s actions are legitimate.

## Legal arguments presented by antirez
- Cites the GNU project’s reimplementation of the UNIX userspace as a precedent: copying code is illegal, but reimplementing functionality is lawful.  
- Argues that AI‑assisted reimplementation occupies the same legal ground as the GNU case, therefore it is permissible.

## Critique of antirez’s reasoning
- The GNU precedent is a *counterexample*: the GNU effort moved software from proprietary to free, expanding the commons.  
- In the chardet case the vector moves in the opposite direction: a copyleft‑protected library is reimplemented under a permissive license, removing the guarantee that derivatives stay open.  
- Antirez does not address this directional difference, conflating legal permissibility with a socially beneficial outcome.

## Legal arguments presented by Ronacher
- Discloses personal bias: he has long wanted chardet under a non‑GPL license.  
- Claims the GPL “runs against” the spirit of sharing because it restricts what can be done with the code.

## Critique of Ronacher’s reasoning
- Misreads the GPL: it does **not** forbid private use or keeping source private; it only imposes conditions when code is *distributed*.  
- The GPL’s “share‑in‑kind” requirement reinforces, rather than suppresses, a reciprocal sharing culture.  
- By contrast, the MIT license allows anyone to take code, improve it, and close it off as proprietary, creating a one‑way flow of benefits toward those with more resources.  
- Historical evidence shows that stronger copyleft enforcement reduced the ability of large companies to appropriate GPL code without contributing back.

## A self‑refuting example
- Vercel reimplemented GNU Bash (GPL) with AI and published it without controversy.  
- Cloudflare later reimplemented Next.js (MIT) with AI, provoking outrage from Vercel.  
- The reaction illustrates that “more share‑friendly” is interpreted as beneficial only when the flow is outward from the reimplementer, not when a competitor reapplies the same tactic to permissively licensed code.

## Legality versus social legitimacy
- Both antirez and Ronacher treat legal permissibility as a proxy for moral acceptability.  
- Law sets a floor: actions that are not illegal are *allowed*, but they are not automatically *right* or socially legitimate.  
- The debate highlights a gap between what the law permits (relicensing via AI reimplementation) and what the open‑source community values (preserving the commons through copyleft).

## Conclusion
- The chardet relicensing case demonstrates that AI‑generated rewrites can legally sidestep copyleft obligations.  
- Legal correctness does not resolve the ethical question of whether removing copyleft protections is legitimate.  
- The discussion underscores the need to distinguish between compliance with copyright law and adherence to the broader social contract that underpins open‑source collaboration.
