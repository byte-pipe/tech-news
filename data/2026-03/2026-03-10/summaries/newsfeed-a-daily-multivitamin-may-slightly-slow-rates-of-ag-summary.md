# Summary of “A daily multivitamin may slightly slow rates of ageing | New Scientist”

## Study overview
- Researchers conducted a double‑blind, placebo‑controlled randomised trial with 1,000 participants (average age ≈ 70).
- Participants took either a standard multivitamin/mineral supplement (Centrum Silver) or a placebo for two years.
- Blood samples were collected at baseline, one year, and two years to analyse DNA‑based epigenetic clocks.

## Main findings
- Five epigenetic clocks were used to estimate biological age from DNA methylation patterns.
- The multivitamin group showed a modest slowing of epigenetic ageing compared with placebo; the effect reached statistical significance for two second‑generation clocks, which are more closely linked to health outcomes.
- The reported slowdown corresponds to roughly four months of biological age over the two‑year period, though the magnitude is small relative to individual variation.

## Interpretation and limitations
- The clinical relevance of a four‑month reduction in epigenetic age is uncertain; it does not equate to a “fountain of youth.”
- Effect sizes are modest, and the findings may not generalise to non‑European populations, younger adults, or other multivitamin formulations.
- The study also tested cocoa extracts, which showed no impact on epigenetic clocks.
- Potential risks of excessive individual vitamins (e.g., vitamin A, B3, B6) were noted, but the multivitamin used did not involve mega‑dosing.

## Expert commentary
- Howard Sesso (Harvard) cautions that multivitamins cannot yet be universally recommended for ageing benefits, though no adverse effects were observed.
- Steve Horvath (UCLA) highlighted the robustness of the trial design compared with typical observational supplement studies.
- Daniel Belsky (Columbia) emphasized the variability in translating epigenetic clock changes into real‑world health outcomes.

## Practical take‑away
- Daily multivitamins may offer a slight, low‑risk benefit in slowing biological ageing markers, but current evidence is insufficient to endorse routine use for all older adults. Further research is needed to confirm health impacts and applicability across diverse groups.
