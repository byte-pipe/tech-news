# The Window Chrome of Our Discontent – Pixel Envy

## Background
- Apple’s UI redesigns (Lion, Yosemite, Big Sur, Tahoe/Liquid Glass) are repeatedly framed as efforts to “focus on content” by de‑emphasizing surrounding chrome.
- Microsoft’s Metro and Fluent designs are cited as parallel attempts to reduce UI clutter and highlight content.

## Design Evolution in Pages
- **Lion (OS X 10.7)**: Toolbar clearly separated from document by a grey background; small, low‑contrast controls.
- **Catalina (Yosemite era)**: Introduction of a right‑hand “Format” panel; toolbar still distinct but controls remain modest in size.
- **Big Sur**: Toolbar adopts the same bright white as the document; buttons appear to float over content, reducing visual separation.
- **Tahoe/Liquid Glass**: Further blending of toolbar and document; with “Reduce Transparency” enabled, contrast drops and icons become pixellated.

## Critique of Toolbar Changes
- The merging of toolbar and document creates constant distraction because the window chrome no longer recedes into the background.
- Older toolbars used muted colours and varied shapes, offering a quiet visual reminder of available tools without intruding.
- Newer toolbars employ uniform monochromatic icons on a white background, making it harder to distinguish functions (e.g., yellow “Table” vs. black‑on‑yellow “Comment”).
- Icon redundancy (e.g., two plus‑sign buttons for “Add Page” and “Insert”) adds confusion.
- The “Reduce Transparency” accessibility setting, which the author uses, further diminishes contrast, making the UI harder to read.

## Accessibility and Contrast
- Early versions (Pages ’09) often failed contrast checks despite using dark outlines and distinct shapes.
- Big Sur’s design meets contrast standards, but at the cost of visual homogeneity.
- Liquid Glass improves internal icon contrast (darker grey icons on white), yet overall toolbar still blends with the document, especially when transparency is reduced.
- System accent colour can mitigate some garish yellows, but certain UI elements (e.g., “Update” button) remain fixed.

## Conclusion
- Apple’s single strategy of blending UI chrome into the document does not achieve the promised focus on content; instead, it reduces the ability of the toolbar to fade into the background.
- By discarding varied shapes, colours, and clear separations, the newer designs increase visual noise rather than diminish it.
- The author remains skeptical that Apple’s approach truly serves user productivity, arguing that a well‑defined, contrasting toolbar better supports a distraction‑free writing experience.
