# AI just gave you superpowers — now what?

## Overview of the conversation
- Participants: Robert Hackett (moderator), Christian Catalini (MIT Crypto Economics Lab, co‑author of “Some Simple Economics of AGI”), Eddy Lazzarin (CTO, a16z crypto).  
- Focus: implications of the paper “Some Simple Economics of AGI” for labor markets, startup strategy, and the broader AI‑driven economy.

## Key themes

### Automation vs. verification
- **Automation**: AI agents can now perform many tasks that were previously done by humans, especially in software development.  
- **Verification**: The dominant new cost is checking and correcting AI output. This verification work replaces much of the “writing code line‑by‑line” effort.  
- Measurement drives automation: anything measurable tends to be automated; the remaining “unknown unknowns” (Knightian uncertainty) require human judgment.

### AI agents as coworkers
- Since December 2025, incremental improvements let agents handle long‑running, loosely specified tasks.  
- Interaction feels conversational: give high‑level instructions, let the agent work autonomously, and review results later.  
- This shift changes the engineer’s role from pure coding to guiding, iterating, and verifying AI‑generated work.

### Impact on junior roles and the “codifier’s curse”
- Junior engineers traditionally learned by writing code; now much of that code is auto‑generated.  
- The “codifier’s curse” describes the risk that reliance on AI erodes deep understanding of fundamentals among newcomers.  
- Value shifts toward skills in prompt engineering, critical evaluation, and domain expertise.

### Meaning‑makers, consensus, and status economies
- Humans will increasingly serve as “meaning‑makers” who interpret AI output, build narratives, and establish consensus.  
- Status economies emerge around the ability to curate trustworthy AI‑generated content and to signal reliability.

### Crypto as infrastructure for identity, provenance, and trust
- Decentralized identity and provenance systems can anchor AI‑generated artifacts, ensuring traceability and accountability.  
- Crypto‑based mechanisms may become essential for verifying the authenticity of AI work, especially in high‑stakes domains.

### Two possible macro‑futures
1. **Hollow economy**: Over‑automation leads to widespread verification fatigue, job displacement, and a decline in meaningful human contribution.  
2. **Augmented economy**: Humans focus on high‑value verification, meaning‑making, and creative recombination, while AI handles routine production, resulting in higher overall productivity.

## Practical takeaways for startups and professionals
- **Prioritize verification workflows**: Build tools and processes that make human review fast and reliable.  
- **Invest in “meaning‑making” talent**: Hire people who excel at interpreting, contextualizing, and communicating AI output.  
- **Leverage crypto for trust**: Incorporate decentralized identity and provenance layers to differentiate products and mitigate risk.  
- **Re‑skill junior staff**: Emphasize prompt engineering, critical thinking, and domain expertise over rote coding.  
- **Monitor the automation‑verification split**: Adjust team structures as the balance shifts toward verification‑heavy work.
