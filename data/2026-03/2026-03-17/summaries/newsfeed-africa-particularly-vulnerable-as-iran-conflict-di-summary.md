# Africa Vulnerable to Supply Chain Disruptions due to Iran Conflict

## Key Points
* Countries heavily relying on imported fertiliser through the strait of Hormuz are particularly vulnerable to supply chain disruptions.
* The conflict has disrupted trade through the strait, affecting 54% of Sudan's fertiliser imports, while Somalia faces 30%, and Kenya at 26%.
* The majority (one-third) of seaborne trade in fertiliser is transported through the strait, exploiting Gulf-produced cheap fossil gas.
* Fertiliser prices have risen significantly since the war started, with potential impacts on food costs, cost of living pressures, and vulnerable populations.

## Analysis
The conflict has disrupted Africa's critical supply chains for fertilisers, exacerbating food production challenges. The reliance on imported fertiliser for African countries makes them highly vulnerable to disruptions. Rising oil and gas prices worldwide will have a similar impact, particularly affecting informal sector workers who contribute significantly to economic activity in many African economies.

## Consequences
The consequences of these disruptions are far-reaching:
- Fertiliser shortages could lead to reduced agricultural productivity, food insecurity, and increased poverty.
- Higher prices for fertilisers may make food less affordable, especially for low-income households.
- The volatility of global energy markets can further exacerbate economic challenges in Africa.

## Factors at Play
The vulnerability of African economies is compounded by various factors:
* Reliance on foreign markets: Africa's diverse economy relies heavily on international trade, making them more susceptible to disruptions.
* Volatile commodity exports: Countries like Kenya, which exports a significant portion of their agricultural products, are also affected by price fluctuations in global energy markets.
* High debt levels and weak infrastructure: These challenges may be compounded by reduced international support and the inability to diversify revenue streams.

The outcome will depend on how leaders respond to these disruptions. While some might impose costly subsidies or absorb the costs on consumers, others could face significant social and political pressures, leading to policy decisions that exacerbate existing vulnerabilities. The impact of higher oil prices on food security is likely to be further exacerbated in countries already reliant heavily on imported fertilisers.
