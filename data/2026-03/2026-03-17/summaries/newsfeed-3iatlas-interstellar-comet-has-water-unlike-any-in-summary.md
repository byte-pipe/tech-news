# 3I/ATLAS: Interstellar comet has water unlike any in our solar system | New Scientist

**The Water-Carbon Molecule in Comets**

The interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has been detected with water and carbon molecules at levels never seen before in our solar system. This suggests that it formed around a drastically different star, one more than twice as old as the sun.

* **Composition**: The comet contains high levels of water (40 times higher than any other comet) and carbon dioxide (far exceeding what we've ever seen).
* **Age Estimate**: Its age is estimated to be 8 billion years, just half that of our sun.
* **Deuterium Levels**: High deuterium levels (10 times the normal amount), which naturally occur in oceans on Earth but are present here at over 40 times higher than usual.
* **Carbon Dioxide and Deuterium Ratio**: Extremely high ratios between carbon dioxide and deuterium, found nowhere else in our universe.

**Formation Theories**

The comet's unusual composition suggests that it formed around an alien star, radically different from our own sun. The presence of such a star could have triggered processes in its forming region that led to the creation of water and carbon molecules.

* **Precursors**: It is unlikely that water would be produced if it were formed in other regions of the solar system due to the lack of sunlight.
* **Supernovae**: Low levels of carbon-13 suggest that the comet may have formed around a star system with few or no supernova explosions, which typically occur when stars are more massive and closer together.

**Observations**

Astronomers tracking 3I/ATLAS using the James Webb Space Telescope detected its unusual composition, while NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center analyzed its water and carbon levels.
