# Anthony Leggett obituary: physicist who brought quantum theory to the macro world

## Early life and education
- Born in 1938 in London; showed talent across many subjects.  
- Studied “Greats” (philosophy, ancient languages, history) at Balliol College, Oxford.  
- Disillusioned with academic philosophy, switched to physics after the 1957 Sputnik launch spurred scientific funding.  
- Earned a second degree in physics at Oxford and completed a PhD in condensed‑matter theory under Dirk ter Haar, who gave him freedom to pursue his own interests.  
- Became fluent in several languages during this period.

## Postdoctoral work and development of a unique theoretical style
- Conducted postdoctoral research at the University of Illinois at Urbana‑Champaign (UIUC) and Kyoto University.  
- Integrated the empirically‑driven, model‑building approach of Urbana with the unified, principle‑focused perspective of the Moscow Landau school.  
- Adopted a style that favored physical insight over heavy mathematics.

## Academic career
- 1967: Appointed to his first faculty position at the University of Sussex, where he taught extensively and supervised PhD students worldwide until 1982.  
- Took an eight‑month sabbatical at Cornell University, then returned to UIUC, where he remained for the rest of his career.

## Major research contributions
- **Superfluid helium‑3 theory (1972‑1976):** Developed a simple, intuitive theory for the complex superfluid phases of liquid helium‑3, predicting a large nuclear‑magnetic‑resonance signal later confirmed experimentally.  
- **Macroscopic quantum phenomena:** Created a theory that enabled understanding and exploitation of quantum effects in macroscopic systems, laying groundwork for quantum computing technologies.  
- Awarded the 2003 Nobel Prize in Physics for his pioneering work on superfluids.

## Legacy and impact
- Recognized for making quantum theory accessible at the macroscopic scale and for influencing generations of physicists through his teaching and mentorship.  
- Died at age 87, leaving a lasting imprint on condensed‑matter physics, quantum physics, and the emerging field of quantum information science.
