# AI Is Here, But The Hard Parts Haven’t Changed

## TL;DR
- AI tools are now ubiquitous in data engineering; only 1 out of 194 survey respondents does not use them.
- The main impact is faster code writing, but the “hard parts” of data work remain unchanged.

## Adoption Landscape
- 82 % of respondents in the larger 2026 State of Data Engineering Survey (n = 1,101) use AI tools daily or more.
- Non‑adoption is typically due to industry‑specific restrictions (e.g., healthcare).

## Perceived Impact of AI
- 57 % say AI makes them write code significantly faster.
- 23 % cite learning and exploration benefits.
- 12 % note assistance without workflow change.
- 7 % report replacement of some manual tasks.
- Concern: faster code generation may lead to quality issues and “production becoming a cesspool.”

## Preferred AI Tools
- Claude: 49 %
- GitHub Copilot: 16 %
- ChatGPT / OpenAI: 15 %
- Other (Gemini, etc.): 12 %
- Cursor: 7 %
- IDE‑integrated tools (Copilot + Cursor) together account for 23 % of usage.

## What Will Matter Most in 2027
- Data modeling and semantic layers: 49 %
- AI‑native pipelines: 23 %
- Data governance and compliance: 21 %
- Cost optimization: 7 %
- Insight: Even with AI, contextual understanding of data remains a critical, non‑automatable challenge.

## Persistent Hard Parts
- Top bottlenecks (large survey):
  - Legacy systems / technical debt: 25 %
  - Lack of leadership direction: 21 %
  - Poor requirements / upstream issues: 19 %
- Data‑modeling pain points:
  - Pressure to move fast: 59 %
  - Lack of clear ownership: 51 %
- Teams using ad‑hoc modeling experience the highest firefighting rate (38 %) versus canonical/semantic models (19 %).
- Over a quarter of teams spend significant time firefighting.

## The “1 = 10” Dilemma
- Some believe a single engineer with AI can replace a team of 4‑10.
- Leadership expects higher velocity from smaller, AI‑enabled teams without sacrificing quality.
- Reality: AI‑generated code can create new technical debt; many engineers do not fully understand the code they inherit.

## Career Outlook
- 71 % of pulse‑survey respondents are optimistic about their careers.
- In the main survey, 42 % expect their data teams to grow in 2026, while only 7 % anticipate shrinkage.

## Takeaway
- AI has transformed how quickly code is produced, but core challenges—legacy systems, leadership, requirements, ownership, and contextual data modeling—remain stubbornly unchanged.
