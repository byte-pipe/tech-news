# Police used AI facial recognition to arrest a Tennessee woman for crimes committed in a state she says she’s never visited | CNN  

## Overview  
- Angela Lipps, 50, a Tennessee grandmother, was arrested on July 14 2025 after an AI facial‑recognition match linked her to bank‑fraud cases in Fargo, North Dakota, a state she says she has never visited.  
- She spent more than five months in custody (initially in a Tennessee jail, then extradited to North Dakota) before the charges were dismissed on December 23 2025.  

## How the AI system was used  
- West Fargo Police employed Clearview AI, a database that scrapes billions of internet photos, to identify a “potential suspect” with facial features similar to Lipps from a fake ID used in a fraud case.  
- The match was forwarded to Fargo Police, who assumed the accompanying surveillance photos were also sent, leading to Lipps being added to the suspect list.  
- Fargo Police did not have its own AI system; reliance on West Fargo’s system was later described as “part of the issue.”  

## Errors and departmental response  
- Police chief Dave Zibolski acknowledged “a couple of errors”:  
  - Unawareness that West Fargo had independently purchased an AI system.  
  - Failure to submit surveillance photos to the certified North Dakota State and Local Intelligence Center for proper facial‑recognition analysis.  
  - Lack of a mechanism to quickly learn that an arrested individual was already in custody elsewhere.  
- Changes announced:  
  - Prohibit use of West Fargo’s AI data by Fargo detectives.  
  - All future facial‑recognition identifications will be reviewed monthly by the Investigations Division commander.  
  - Work directly with state and federal agencies and the Intelligence Center for future cases.  

## Legal and personal impact  
- Lipps faced felony theft and unauthorized use of personal identifying information charges.  
- Bank records later showed she was in Tennessee at the time of the fraud, leading the State Attorney’s Office to consider the evidence “potentially exculpatory.”  
- Charges were dismissed without prejudice; Lipps was released on December 24 2025.  
- Her lawyers describe the detention as “trauma, loss of liberty, and reputational damage” and are exploring civil‑rights claims, though no lawsuit has been filed yet.  
- Lipps expressed relief that the ordeal is over and said she will never return to North Dakota.  

## Broader context  
- The case highlights growing concerns about police reliance on AI facial‑recognition tools, which have been linked to other misidentifications nationwide.  
- Calls for greater oversight, transparency, and accountability in the deployment of such technologies continue.
