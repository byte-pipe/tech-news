# How the Apple II created the core of personal computing

## Early context and the birth of Apple II
- In the 1970s personal computers were sold as DIY kits; the Apple I was a bare circuit board that hobbyists assembled themselves.  
- Steve Jobs, after a suggestion from the Byte Shop owner, realized a pre‑assembled machine with a built‑in keyboard could reach a broader audience.  
- The Apple II launched in 1977 as a beige plastic unit with an integrated keyboard, sold for $1,298 (about $7,000 today); a monitor was sold separately.

## Technical innovations
- The Apple II offered rudimentary color graphics and BASIC programming out of the box, eliminating the need for users to type low‑level commands to start the machine.  
- Apple partnered with a floppy‑disk maker, bought half‑assembled drives at a discount, and Wozniak designed a cheaper, more efficient controller board, delivering the world’s most affordable 5.25‑inch floppy drive by June 1978.  
- The floppy drive made saving, loading, and distributing software far easier than the slow cassette‑tape method.

## Market impact and software ecosystem
- The availability of affordable floppy drives sparked an explosion of Apple II software, driving revenue up 640 % in 1979 and 230 % in 1980.  
- VisiCalc, the first spreadsheet program (the great‑grandmother of Microsoft Excel), launched exclusively on the Apple II in 1979; its success multiplied Apple II sales tenfold.  
- Aggressive marketing to schools and the machine’s appeal as an educational toy introduced millions of children to computing, fostering a generation of early programmers and gamers.

## Cultural and legacy notes
- Steve Wozniak’s engineering ensured the Apple II was functional immediately on power‑up, a key factor in its popularity.  
- The Apple II became the most successful personal computer of its era, shaping Apple’s reputation for integrated hardware‑software products—a philosophy that still defines the company.  
- Despite attempts in the 1980s to replace the Apple II with a next‑generation model, none matched its impact, and the platform remained a cornerstone of Apple’s early success.
