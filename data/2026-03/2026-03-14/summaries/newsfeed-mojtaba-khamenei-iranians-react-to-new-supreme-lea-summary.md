# Iranians react to new supreme leader's first address

## Background
- Mojtaba Khamenei was announced as Iran’s new Supreme Leader, succeeding his father.
- Since his appointment he has not been seen, filmed, or photographed in public.
- The first official communication was a written statement read by a state‑TV presenter.

## Content of the address
- Vowed to keep the Strait of Hormuz closed to international shipping, threatening a cut‑off of about one‑fifth of the world’s oil supply.
- Promised continued retaliation for “the blood of citizens” killed since the war with the United States and Israel began, stating that past strikes were only a “limited portion” of what is to come.
- Said he learned of his appointment through state television.

## Public reaction
- **Skepticism about his condition**: many citizens doubt whether Khamenei is alive or capable of speaking; U.S. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth claimed he was “wounded and likely disfigured” in an early Tehran air strike, though no evidence was provided.
- **Quotes from Tehran residents**:
  - “I don’t even think it was his message.” – woman, 40s.
  - “Control of the country is in the hands of the IRGC.” – same interviewee.
  - “It was surprising he did not issue even a voice memo; it raises more doubts.” – man, 30s.
  - “I’m not convinced he wrote the message himself.” – man, 20s.
- **General sentiment**: the address was seen as offering no clarity, reinforcing the belief that the regime’s policies will not change regardless of the new leader.
- **Pro‑regime rally**: on Quds Day, crowds in Tehran displayed photos of Mojtaba Khamenei; officials such as President Massoud Pezeshkian and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi appeared in the footage.
- **Security incident**: BBC‑verified video showed an explosion near the rally, with chants of “Allahu Akbar” continuing.

## Context and aftermath
- Internet blackout in Iran limits communication; only brief satellite uplinks allow some contact, mainly from anti‑regime voices.
- The war has caused extensive civilian damage: Iran’s UN ambassador reported thousands of civilian sites destroyed; HRANA estimates ~1,800 deaths, two‑thirds civilians.
- The message is viewed by many as a continuation of the Islamic Republic’s hardline stance, with little expectation of internal change.
