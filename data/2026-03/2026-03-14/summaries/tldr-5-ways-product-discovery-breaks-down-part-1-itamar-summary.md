# 5 Ways Product Discovery Breaks Down (part 1) – Summary

## Overview
- Many organisations acknowledge the value of research‑driven product development, yet most fail to implement product discovery effectively.
- The article identifies five recurring problems that sabotage discovery and outlines practical remedies.
- The first two problems—*must‑have features* and *weak goals*—are explained in detail.

## Modern Product Development Cycle
- Product discovery sits between goal‑setting and building.
- It consists of two complementary activities:
  * **Opportunity / problem discovery** – research on users, market, competitors, technology, and idea gathering from customers and internal stakeholders.
  * **Solution discovery** – evaluating and validating ideas, creating a feedback loop of learning that informs re‑evaluation and decision‑making.
- Common misconception: discovery is only “problem space” work (e.g., user interviews). Marty Cagan stresses that discovery must also produce solutions that are valuable, usable, feasible, and viable.

## 1. Must‑Have Features
### Why it Happens
- Companies run a two‑track system: some ideas go through discovery, others are labeled “must‑have” and fast‑tracked to delivery.
- The “must‑have” label is often assigned using the SCORE heuristic (Sparse data, Consensus, Opinions, Rank, Ego).
- Leaders view discovery as unnecessary for core areas, fearing loss of control, and AI hype pushes a build‑first mentality.

### What You Can Do About It
- **Show business value**: Quantify waste from unvalidated “must‑haves” (e.g., dev weeks, product bloat, missed revenue) and present discovery’s impact in dollars, person‑weeks, and competitive advantage.
- **Create trust**: Keep managers and stakeholders involved by sharing interim results, pivots, A/B‑test outcomes, and using visual artefacts such as GIST boards, opportunity‑solution trees, or similar.
- **Transition gradually**: Adopt hybrid approaches (e.g., Now/Next/Later roadmaps) that still require validation of high‑priority ideas, then move toward fully discovery‑driven roadmaps.

## 2. Bad / Weak Goals
### Why it Happens
- Teams often lack clear, measurable outcomes (“problems to solve”).
- Companies provide high‑level business targets (revenue, market share) and departmental metrics, but these do not translate into actionable product goals.
- The absence of outcome‑focused goals leads to reliance on plans and roadmaps, causing teams to default to “follow the plan.”
- Output‑oriented goals incentivise shipping features over achieving outcomes, weakening discovery’s purpose.

### What You Can Do About It (outlined in the article)
- Establish explicit, outcome‑based goals that are tied to business impact and can be measured.
- Align discovery activities with these outcomes, ensuring every idea is evaluated against the defined success criteria.
- Shift performance metrics from output (features shipped) to outcome (value delivered, user impact).
