# Firings, funding cuts, and a shooting leave CDC demoralized

## Overview
- The article examines the cumulative impact of mass firings, budget reductions, and an August 2025 shooting on the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) workforce and the surrounding Atlanta community.
- It is based on reporting by NPR in partnership with KFF Health News.

## Timeline of Events
- **February 13‑15 2025:** The Trump administration initiates the “Valentine’s Day Massacre,” terminating roughly 1,000 CDC employees in the first wave of layoffs.
- **Spring 2025:** Additional staff reductions and early retirements bring total CDC workforce loss to about 25 % (≈3,000 workers).
- **Summer 2025:** A shooter opens fire on CDC buildings on August 8, killing DeKalb County Police Officer David Rose and later committing suicide; two deaths are reported.
- **Fall 2025:** A federal government shutdown further strains remaining staff and local businesses.
- **March 2026:** Dr. Jay Bhattacharya is appointed interim CDC director while also leading the NIH; the agency still lacks a permanent director after six months.

## Personal Impact
- **Sarah Boim:** Former communications specialist in the National Center for Environmental Health. She was dismissed despite an “outstanding” performance rating and a recent raise. Boim now works as a health contractor and freelance worker, expressing grief over lost expertise and community harm.
- **Ben McKenzie:** Current CDC researcher who participates in protests and runs a mutual‑aid group that has distributed over $200,000 to assist former staff with rent and other needs. He describes the workplace as feeling like a target after the shooting.

## Community and Economic Effects
- **Local Businesses:** Sri Thai restaurant, located across from the CDC campus, reports a noticeable dip in catering orders and overall revenue after the layoffs and the shutdown.
- **Protests:** Weekly Tuesday demonstrations have continued for a year, with crowds growing to about 75 participants on the anniversary of the mass firings. Protesters display signs such as “We love CDC workers” and “Save Public Health,” and leave flowers at a memorial for Officer Rose.
- **Economic Ripple:** The loss of CDC salaries reduces spending in the Atlanta area, affecting neighbors, friends, and local commerce.

## Government Response
- **HHS Statement (Biden administration):** Claims the department had become “bloated” with a 38 % budget increase and 17 % workforce growth, and asserts that cuts are aimed at eliminating wasteful, duplicative entities aligned with the “Make America Healthy Again” agenda.
- **Recovery Outlook:** The article questions whether the demoralized CDC staff can rebuild capacity and restore public‑health functions amid ongoing leadership vacancies, funding constraints, and lingering physical damage from the shooting.

## Key Takeaways
- The CDC has lost roughly a quarter of its workforce, creating gaps in expertise that could cause “generational harm” to public‑health outcomes.
- Economic fallout extends beyond the agency, impacting nearby restaurants and other local businesses.
- Emotional trauma from the shooting and continuous protests adds to staff burnout and morale challenges.
- Mutual‑aid initiatives and community solidarity are emerging as short‑term support mechanisms, but long‑term recovery depends on stable leadership and restored funding.
