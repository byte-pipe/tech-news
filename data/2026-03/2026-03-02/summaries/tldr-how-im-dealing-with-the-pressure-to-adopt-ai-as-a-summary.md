# How I’m dealing with the pressure to adopt AI as a designer

## The current climate
- The design world is saturated with loud, often brag‑filled AI chatter.
- Six months ago I felt anxious, fearing automation could erase my career.
- I sensed pressure to take a stance on AI while experts debate the details.
- After a month of trying to catch up, I concluded that uncertainty is the most realistic position for now.

## You control how much AI enters your work
- AI is a collection of tools; you remain the gatekeeper.
- Adopt a tool only if it improves your work or at least does not harm user outcomes.
- If a tool fails to meet that bar, you simply do not use it.
- This decision‑making mirrors the evaluation skills you’ve used throughout your career.

## Wait six months
- Patience can counter hype: ignore a new AI tool until it has been tested for six months.
- After six months, early adopters’ lessons and stable tutorials become available.
- Example: Claude Code was ignored at launch, revisited six months later when reliable resources existed, avoiding time spent on fleeting tools.

## Protect the work
- Center your thinking on the actual design work, not on hype cycles.
- LinkedIn and similar platforms amplify marketing, anxiety, and status‑signalling.
- “Everyone’s using it” is not a design justification; speed does not equal improvement.
- Opt out of noisy spaces if needed; valuable insights will surface through trusted colleagues and projects.

## Experiment widely. Deploy narrowly.
- Play with AI in low‑risk experiments to build vocabulary and instinct.
- Use image generators, throwaway code prototypes, or AI‑driven content stress tests for learning.
- Treat production use as a responsibility; apply the same scrutiny as any critical tool.

## AI as a working assistant
- If you haven’t used AI, start by treating it as a personal assistant.
- Ask it to check emails, locate unmet commitments, or list typical approaches—tasks that support, not replace, your judgment.
- Feed it your own notes and half‑formed ideas; let it help organize and surface themes.
- This low‑stakes entry frees mental energy for higher‑level decisions.

## Protect the middle layer – that’s where your skill lives
- Design work consists of three layers: inputs (research, data), outputs (UI, code), and the middle layer (interpretation, synthesis, judgment).
- AI excels at the edges (summarising research, generating UI patterns, drafting copy) but should not dominate the middle layer.
- Over‑reliance on AI can cause “regression to the mean,” producing average, unremarkable solutions.
- A 2026 Anthropic study showed developers using AI scored 17 % lower on comprehension tests, indicating skill erosion when AI handles sense‑making.
- Guard the middle layer to preserve innovation and deep understanding.

## Bring AI in when it benefits them, not you
- Peer pressure to use AI is not a design argument.
- When deciding on AI for client‑facing work, shift the question from “Is this interesting to me?” to “Does this benefit the client/project?”.
