- Received a motivational letter from his father at age 15, urging steadfastness against detractors and framing intellectual defiance as a religious duty.

## The mathematical problem of infinity
- Classical mathematics treated infinity as a convenient fiction; Zeno’s paradoxes and theological concerns made it controversial.
- By the 19th century, mathematicians sought to formalize foundational concepts, including the nature of numbers and the continuum.

## Parallel work of Dedekind and Cantor
- Richard Dedekind (1858) rigorously defined the real numbers but kept his results private until he could verify them.
- In 1870, Cantor, unaware of Dedekind’s definition, developed his own construction of the real numbers while studying equation behavior.
- Both published independent papers in early 1872, presenting a complete, gap‑free number line, overturning the earlier belief that the continuum contained inevitable gaps.

## Significance of the plagiarism claim
- The newly uncovered 1873 letter suggests Cantor may have borrowed key ideas from Dedekind without proper attribution.
- If confirmed, this would reshape Cantor’s legacy, turning the narrative of a solitary genius into one of intellectual appropriation.

## Broader impact on mathematics
- Cantor’s 1874 paper introduced the concept of different cardinalities of infinity, sparking a revolution that eventually rewrote large parts of mathematics.
- The controversy highlights the collaborative and contested nature of mathematical discovery in the late 19th century.
