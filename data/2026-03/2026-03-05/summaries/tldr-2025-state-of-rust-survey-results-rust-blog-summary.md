# 2025 State of Rust Survey Summary

## Survey Overview
- Conducted from 17 Nov 2025 to 17 Dec 2025, gathering **7 156** completed responses (completion rate 76.2%).  
- Slight decline compared with 2024 (7 310 responses, 77.4%).  
- Results closely mirror the previous year; most differences are under 1 percentage point.  
- Multiple concurrent surveys (e.g., Compiler Performance, Variadic Generics) may have reduced participation.  
- Full report is available for download.

## Screenshotting Rust Use
- Majority develop on the **stable compiler** and keep up with releases, trusting stability guarantees.  
- Nightly usage is lower than in 2024, likely reflecting a shift after recent stabilizations (let‑chains, async closures).  
- Users who have stopped using Rust often indicate a “see you again in the future” attitude rather than a permanent goodbye.  
- Interest in crate dependencies pinned to Git repositories and in the usefulness of compiler error‑code explanations; the latter is perceived as more helpful than previously assumed.

## Challenges and Wishes
- Newly stabilized features (let‑chains, async closures) are popular and widely adopted.  
- **Most‑wanted upcoming features**: generic const expressions and improved trait methods.  
- Persistent pain points: long compile times and high storage usage.  
- Debugging difficulty dropped from 2nd to 4th place (≈ 2 pp decline); a dedicated debugging survey has been launched.

## Learning about Rust
- Slight (~3 pp) shift from traditional meetups and forums toward **LLM‑based tooling** for asking questions.  
- Primary learning resources remain the **online documentation** and reading Rust code directly.  

## Industry and Community
- Hiring demand for Rust developers continues to grow, indicating a solidifying market presence.  
- Respondents overwhelmingly want **more Rust**, but express concern about increasing language complexity.  
- Slight increase in “developer and maintainer support” needs; funding initiatives (RustNL, Rust Foundation, OpenCollective, Thanks.dev, GitHub Sponsors, Liberapay) are encouraged.  
- Trust in the Rust Foundation is improving.  
- Tooling trends: **Zed** jumps to the top editor preference, followed by **Helix**; agentic editors are rising, while VS Code and IntelliJ show signs of decline.  
- Small but notable groups still favor classic editors (Atom, Emacs, Vim).

## Conclusions
- No major surprises; trends largely confirm previous year’s observations.  
- Key takeaways: stable‑compiler dominance, enthusiasm for recent feature stabilizations, ongoing concerns about compile performance and language complexity, growing industry demand, and evolving editor preferences.
