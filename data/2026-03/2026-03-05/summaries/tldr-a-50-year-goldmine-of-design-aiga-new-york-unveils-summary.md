# A 50‑year goldmine of design: AIGA New York unveils its poster archive to the public

## Overview
- AIGA New York has opened its previously private poster archive, a 50‑year collection documenting the city’s visual culture, events, exhibitions, and talks.  
- The archive was assembled over decades by members and volunteers and informed the organization’s new visual identity last year.  
- Each poster is described by executive director Stacey Panousopoulous as “a snapshot in time,” offering a tactile, human counterpoint to today’s fleeting digital visuals.

## Archive Highlights
- The collection showcases evolving typography, image‑making, conceptual forms, printing, and digital techniques.  
- Early posters focus on design craft; later works reflect technological shifts, activism, and challenges to industry norms.  
- Notable pieces mentioned include:
  - Sagmeister’s “fresh dialogue” poster (highlighted by former president Chelsea Goldwell).  
  - Late‑1990s posters exploring the impact of computers on aesthetics (cited by former president Juliette Cezzar).  

## Insights from Executive Director Stacey Panousopoulous
- **Tactile appeal:** The archive satisfies a growing desire for physical, human‑scale design objects.  
- **Design evolution:** Over five decades, the posters reveal experimentation in type, image, and conceptual expression, mirroring broader conversations about technology and leadership.  
- **Cultural record:** Posters trace cultural and political shifts, moving from pure craft focus to urgent calls for change.  
- **Collection process:** Designers naturally saved their work; board members later recognized its archival value and kept it stored safely.  
- **Motivation for release:** The archive supports AIGA’s mission to provide open resources, aligns with the recent brand refresh, and marks a milestone after the chapter’s 40th anniversary.

## Perspectives from Former Presidents
- **Chelsea Goldwell:** Values the hand‑made, human quality of posters; sees them as opportunities for designers to express freely and have fun.  
- **Juliette Cezzar:** Highlights late‑1990s optimism about computers, notes the disappearance of regional visual styles with the rise of platforms like Instagram and TikTok, and references the “Making the City” initiative that connected design to neighborhood impact.  
- Both emphasize New York’s unique design spirit—driven by ambition, diversity of industries, and a belief that design is a necessity, not a luxury.

## Future Plans
- AIGA NY aims to make the archive physically accessible, potentially through exhibitions and a dedicated book that delves deeper into the posters’ stories.  
- The organization sees the archive as a living resource that will continue to inspire and inform the city’s creative community.

## Significance for the Design Community
- The archive provides a rare, tangible record of New York’s design history, activism, and cultural dialogue.  
- It reinforces the city’s reputation as a hub where design is integral to everyday life and a catalyst for social change.  
- By sharing the collection, AIGA NY invites designers, scholars, and the public to engage with the past while shaping the future of visual culture.
