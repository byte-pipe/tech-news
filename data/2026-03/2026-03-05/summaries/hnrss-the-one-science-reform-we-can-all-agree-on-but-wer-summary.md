# The one science reform we can all agree on, but we're too cowardly to do

## Overview
- The current academic publishing system is illogical and wasteful, especially for STEM researchers who rely on federal funding.
- Universities reward research output, not teaching, forcing scholars to seek external grants that ultimately fund for‑profit publishers.
- The author argues that eliminating for‑profit scientific publishers is the single reform that could gain universal agreement.

## How the Existing System Works
- **Funding flow**: Federal government → university research grants → researchers → private publishers (via article processing charges and subscription fees).
- **Career incentives**: Promotion and recognition depend on publishing in high‑impact journals, not on teaching quality.
- **Peer review**: Conducted by unpaid academics; editors receive minimal compensation, reviewers get only professional goodwill.
- **Publisher profits**: Journals own copyright, charge authors APCs, and place articles behind paywalls that universities must subscribe to, often using “indirect costs” from grants.

## Why For‑Profit Publishers Are the Core Problem
- Originated to handle the logistical burden of printing physical journals after WWII.
- The internet removed the need for costly physical production, yet publishers have increased prices and locked in the market.
- High‑impact journals became essential for career advancement, creating a captive audience that universities must fund.
- Estimated annual spending by U.S. universities on subscriptions and APCs is around **$2.5 billion**, with profit margins near **40 %**.

## Financial Implications
- Government subsidies to publishers could be redirected to actual research; cutting publisher costs might save more than recent federal science‑budget cuts.
- Example figures: $1 billion (ARL subscription survey, 2017) + $380 million (federal APC estimate, 2023) suggest massive, largely taxpayer‑funded outflows.

## Historical Context
- Post‑WWII surge in research output led private companies to take over journal production.
- Over time, publishers shifted from providing essential services to extracting profit from a locked‑in market.

## Resistance and Persistence
- Stakeholders benefit from the status quo, creating political and institutional inertia.
- Politicians often resort to indiscriminate budget cuts (“policy by bloodletting”), worsening the system’s dysfunction.

## Call to Action
- Eliminate for‑profit scientific publishers to stop the double‑paying loop (taxpayers → publishers → researchers).
- This reform aligns the interests of scientists, universities, and policymakers, offering a clear, achievable target for systemic change.
