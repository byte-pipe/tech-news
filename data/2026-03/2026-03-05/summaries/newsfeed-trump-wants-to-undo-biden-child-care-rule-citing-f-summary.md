# Trump wants to undo Biden child care rule, citing fraud concerns

## Background of the Biden rule
- In 2024 the Biden administration finalized a rule requiring states to pay child‑care subsidies **in advance** and based on enrollment rather than attendance.
- The change was meant to give providers like Michelle Wright of Illinois more predictable cash flow.
- Some states adopted the rule immediately; others, including Illinois, received waivers to delay implementation.

## Trump administration’s proposal
- The Trump administration has announced a plan to repeal the Biden‑era rule.
- Officials argue that paying subsidies up front creates opportunities for fraud and weakens accountability.
- The proposal was presented in a Department of Health and Human Services video featuring former deputy secretary Jim O’Neill.

## Fraud allegations that sparked the move
- Allegations of child‑care fraud surfaced in Minnesota after a YouTuber’s claims, prompting an attempted freeze of subsidies in five Democratic‑led states (later blocked by a lawsuit).
- The administration cites these allegations as evidence that the advance‑payment system is vulnerable.
- However, the Administration for Children and Families reported a payment error rate of **less than 4 %** in 2023, and fraud prosecutions remain rare.

## State oversight and existing safeguards
- Many states have increased oversight, including unannounced inspections and daily attendance logs submitted to state agencies.
- Despite the fraud narrative, oversight mechanisms are already in place to detect irregularities.

## Reactions and broader implications
- Child‑care advocates worry that the focus on fraud could stall progress toward a more stable financing system.
- Bipartisan support exists for advance‑payment models; Republican‑led Missouri enacted a pilot program to pay subsidies based on enrollment.
- Missouri officials note the approach aligns with private‑market practices but adds costs that strain state budgets.
- Providers and advocacy groups emphasize the need for reliable funding to maintain high‑quality child‑care, especially in low‑income communities.
