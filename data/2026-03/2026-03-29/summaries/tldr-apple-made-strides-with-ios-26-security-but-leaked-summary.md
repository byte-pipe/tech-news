# Summary of TechCrunch article: Apple made strides with iOS 26 security, but leaked hacking tools still leave millions exposed to spyware attacks  

## Background  
- iPhone security was long thought to require extensive resources, making zero‑day exploits rare and limited to targeted attacks.  
- Apple has promoted the idea that iPhones are “hard to hack.”  

## New Threats (Coruna & DarkSword)  
- Researchers at Google, iVerify, and Lookout identified large‑scale campaigns using the tools **Coruna** and **DarkSword**.  
- Campaigns target users worldwide who have not updated to the latest iOS version, delivering exploits via compromised websites or fake pages.  
- Leaked source code allows anyone to reuse the tools against outdated iOS devices.  
- Actors include Russian state‑linked spies and Chinese cybercriminal groups.  

## Apple’s Recent Security Improvements  
- Introduction of memory‑safe code and the **Lockdown Mode** to mitigate spyware.  
- iOS 26 on iPhone 17 (2025) adds **Memory Integrity Enforcement**, blocking memory‑corruption bugs that DarkSword relies on.  

## Impact on Different User Groups  
- **Modern users**: iPhone 17 with iOS 26 enjoy stronger protection against memory‑based attacks.  
- **Legacy users**: Devices running iOS 18 or older remain vulnerable to memory corruption exploits and other zero‑days.  

## Expert Opinions  
- **Matthias Frielingsdorf (iVerify)**: Mobile attacks are now “widespread,” but zero‑day exploits against up‑to‑date software remain expensive and limited.  
- **Patrick Wardle (Apple security)**: The rarity of documented attacks does not mean they are rare in practice; they are simply under‑reported.  
- **Justin Albrecht (Lookout)**: A secondary market for exploits incentivizes developers to resell patched tools, creating recurring threats.  

## Implications & Future Outlook  
- The discovery of Coruna and DarkSword suggests memory‑based attacks will continue to affect users who lag behind Apple’s latest security updates.  
- The “second‑hand” exploit market indicates that similar leaks may recur, pressuring users to adopt newer iOS versions promptly.  
- Overall, while Apple’s iOS 26 marks a significant security leap, millions of older iPhone and iPad users remain exposed to sophisticated spyware campaigns.
