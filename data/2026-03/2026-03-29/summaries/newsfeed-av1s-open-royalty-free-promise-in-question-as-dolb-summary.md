# AV1’s open, royalty‑free promise in question as Dolby sues Snapchat over codec

## Background
- AV1 was created by the Alliance for Open Media (AOMedia) as an open, royalty‑free alternative to HEVC/H.265.  
- Dolby Laboratories filed a lawsuit in the U.S. District Court for the District of Delaware alleging that Snap Inc. infringes Dolby patents by using AV1.

## Dolby’s claims against Snap
- Accuses Snap of infringing four Dolby patents:  
  - U.S. Patent No. 10,855,99 – “Inter‑plane prediction”  
  - U.S. Patent No. 9,924,193 – “Picture coding supporting block merging and skip mode”  
  - U.S. Patent No. 9,596,469 – “Sample array coding for low‑delay”  
  - U.S. Patent No. 10,404,272 – “Entropy encoding and decoding scheme”
- States that Snap’s app accepts AV1‑compliant videos, decodes and re‑encodes them, and tracks device support to stream AV1 when possible.  
- Argues AV1 reuses concepts from HEVC—block‑based coding flow, coding‑unit division—making it subject to existing patent licenses.  
- Seeks a jury trial, a declaration that the patents are not bound by FRAND obligations, and an injunction preventing further infringement.

## AV1’s royalty‑free status under scrutiny
- AOMedia maintains that AV1 is covered by its royalty‑free patent policy (Alliance for Open Media Patent License 1.0) and a permissive BSD‑3‑Clause Clear License.  
- Patent pool administrators Access and The Sisvel Group are licensing AV1‑related patents despite AOMedia’s objections.  
- InterDigital has also sued over AV1, targeting Amazon Fire streaming devices for alleged infringement.  
- The EU antitrust regulator investigated AOMedia’s licensing policy in 2022 and closed the probe in 2023 without a compliance finding.

## Potential impact on AV1 adoption
- The lawsuits could shape the future of AV1, which remains less widely adopted than HEVC eight years after its release.  
- IP commentator Florian Mueller notes that labeling a codec “royalty‑free” does not eliminate underlying patent rights and that non‑FRAND patent holders could demand high royalties, potentially discouraging implementation.  
- Unlike HEVC, which largely operates under FRAND‑pledged patents, AV1 may face many essential patents without FRAND obligations, raising the risk of prohibitive licensing demands.

## Responses
- Dolby and Snap declined to comment on the lawsuit.  
- An AOMedia spokesperson acknowledged receipt of interview requests but provided no response before publication.
