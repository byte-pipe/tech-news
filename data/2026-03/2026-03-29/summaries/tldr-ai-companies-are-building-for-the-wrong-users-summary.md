# AI Companies Are Building for the Wrong Users

## The anecdote: resistance even in a top‑tech firm
- An engineer at a leading tech company tried an internal AI coding agent.
- Many colleagues resisted: they preferred hand‑coding, couldn’t fit the tool into their workflow, or found the UX confusing.
- Despite the company’s expertise in foundation models, AI coding tools remain largely unused.

## The bubble is smaller than you think
- Readers of the blog are likely deep in the AI “bubble,” experimenting with tools like Claude Code or Cursor.
- The most enthusiastic users represent only a tiny fraction of the eventual market.
- Early believers will tolerate rough onboarding and clunky UX, but broader adoption requires addressing the majority.

## Why the gap is a product problem, not a people problem
- Assuming reluctant users will eventually come around without product changes is a mistake.
- AI adoption differs from past tech shifts because it challenges users’ professional identity.

### User identity
- AI agents can feel like an affront to engineers who take pride in the quality of their own code.
- Successful AI products should reinforce, not replace, a user’s self‑image—making them feel more forward‑thinking or productive.
- Trust is built gradually: engineers first accept small suggestions (tab completion) before trusting full autonomous agents.

### Editorial judgment
- Rapid feature rollout can overwhelm users who are still learning core functionality.
- Presenting a sprawling set of capabilities too early reinforces the belief that the product isn’t for them.
- Incremental, reliable improvements targeted to the right user at the right time earn trust and enable later expansion.

## The maturation moment
- Early cloud computing (e.g., AWS 2008) served startups and tech‑forward users who tolerated ambiguity.
- As cloud moved to large enterprises, providers had to adapt to stricter expectations and risk tolerances.
- AI is at a similar inflection point: after the early adopters, the next growth phase involves the “middle 50 %” who are curious but skeptical and require a different product approach.
