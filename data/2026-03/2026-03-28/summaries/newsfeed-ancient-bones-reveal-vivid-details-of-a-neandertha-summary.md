# Ancient bones reveal vivid details of a Neanderthal elephant hunt

## Background discovery
- In 1948, a 125,000‑year‑old straight‑tusked elephant (Palaeoloxodon antiquus) was uncovered in the lakebed at Lehringen, Germany.  
- A 2.3 m yew spear was found lodged between the animal’s ribs, representing the oldest complete spear known at the time.  
- At that period Neanderthals were the sole humans in Europe, so the find suggested they could hunt large game rather than merely scavenge.

## Initial issues and obscurity
- The excavation was led by Alexander Rosenbrock, a school principal and amateur archaeologist, who lacked proper documentation tools (no camera, no sketches).  
- Half of the bones were removed by a mining operation before Rosenbrock arrived; some bones had already been stolen.  
- A seven‑year legal battle allowed Rosenbrock to retain the material in Verden, after which he died in the 1950s without publishing his results, leaving the find largely forgotten.

## Modern re‑examination
- In 2025 Ivo Verheijen, bones specialist at the Schöningen Research Museum, retrieved dozens of boxes of Lehringen material stored in the museum attic.  
- The boxes contained elephant and other animal bones, flint tools, and Rosenbrock’s field notes preserved by his daughter.  
- Detailed inspection revealed clear cut marks on the elephant bones, confirming butchery.

## Findings about the hunt
- The elephant was a prime‑aged male (~30 years, >3.5 m tall) that died shortly after being speared, indicating a fresh kill.  
- Cut marks on ribs and internal bones show that hunters removed organs and meat, then left the remainder for scavengers.  
- Additional butchered bones of bears, beavers and aurochs suggest regular hunting and processing of multiple species at the lakeside site.  
- The spear likely became embedded when the wounded animal moved to water; a second spear may have been crushed beneath the animal as it collapsed.

## Significance and future work
- The Lehringen site now provides one of the most detailed reconstructions of a Neanderthal big‑game hunt.  
- Preservation efforts are underway to display the bones and spear, giving the site the recognition it merits.  
- The find, together with other Palaeolithic spears (e.g., Schöningen, Clacton), strengthens evidence that Neanderthals manufactured and used spears for active hunting.  
- Researchers plan further analysis of the spear itself to deepen understanding of Neanderthal weapon technology.
