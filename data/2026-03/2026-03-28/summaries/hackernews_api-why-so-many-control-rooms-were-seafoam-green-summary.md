# Why So Many Control Rooms Were Seafoam Green

## Overview
- The post explores why mid‑20th‑century industrial control rooms, especially those of the Manhattan Project, were painted a seafoam‑green hue.  
- It combines personal travel anecdotes, historical background, and color‑theory research by Faber Birren.

## Historical Context
- Oak Ridge, Tennessee, grew from a small farming community to a secret WWII site (“Site X”) with 75,000 people by 1945.  
- The X‑10 Graphite Reactor was the world’s second full‑scale nuclear reactor; its control room featured the distinctive seafoam green.

## Color Theory and Faber Birren
- Birren (1919‑1963) was a self‑taught color theorist who consulted corporations on how color influences perception and safety.  
- He demonstrated that a blue‑green background made meat appear redder, boosting sales, and later applied his ideas to wartime industry.

## Industrial Color Coding (adopted 1944‑1948)
- **Fire Red:** fire protection and emergency stops.  
- **Solar Yellow:** caution and physical hazards.  
- **Alert Orange:** hazardous machine parts.  
- **Safety Green:** first‑aid equipment, exits, eyewash stations.  
- **Caution Blue:** non‑safety notices.  
- **Light Green:** wall color to reduce visual fatigue.

## Application in Manhattan Project Sites
- DuPont, working with Birren, used the “Light Green” (seafoam) on walls of Oak Ridge and the Hanford B‑Reactor control rooms.  
- Birren’s 1963 book *Color for Interiors* argued that soft green tones create a non‑distracting, restful environment, improving efficiency and morale.  
- Specific recommendations observed at Hanford:
  - Medium Green dado (waist‑height wall).  
  - Medium Gray for machinery.  
  - Fire Red only for fire devices.  
  - Beige where natural light is lacking.  
  - Light‑colored flooring.
