# Apple’s long, bitter App Store antitrust war

## How it started
- In 1998 Microsoft was sued for antitrust violations that included targeting Apple’s QuickTime player, forcing Apple to drop the Windows version.
- Apple never overtook Microsoft in PCs but now dominates mobile computing, earning revenue from hardware, accessories, services, and App Store commissions.
- Developers criticize Apple for “Sherlocking” (copying their tools) and for the “Apple Tax” commission, as well as for blocking third‑party app stores and sideloading.
- Epic Games has led high‑profile challenges, seeking its own payment system and a third‑party iOS store; many governments (US, EU, Brazil, Korea, Japan) have joined antitrust actions.
- The core antitrust claim: Apple is the gatekeeper of billions of primary devices, locking out competition while charging high fees.

## How it’s going
- Courts and regulators have ordered changes, but Apple has often delayed implementation.
- Epic’s 2020 US lawsuit resulted in a 2021 ruling that largely upheld Apple’s “walled‑garden” safety argument; Apple still resists allowing external payment links, adding prohibitive fees and facing fines (e.g., Dutch regulator 2022).
- In the US v. Google case, Apple avoided liability by testifying that breaking its search deal would harm its business.
- The EU’s Digital Markets Act forced Apple to permit third‑party app stores in 2024, but with restrictive terms and extra fees; Apple later faced fines for “overly strict” compliance.
- Some tangible shifts: Amazon’s Kindle app added “Get Book” links in 2025; AltStore launched in the EU and Japan; Epic Games Store opened in Europe; Apple reduced developer fees in China.
- Nevertheless, iOS remains a tightly controlled platform; many third‑party stores (e.g., Setapp) have shut down due to complex terms and ongoing disputes with the EU.

## What happens next
- Apple will continue facing regulatory pressure worldwide; Australia, the US DOJ (2024 iOS antitrust suit), and Chinese authorities are all pursuing changes.
- The EU and Apple are still negotiating DMA compliance; Apple’s planned 2026 fee restructure was stalled by what it calls EU “political delay tactics.”
- Immediate non‑regulatory threats (e.g., market shifts, developer migration) could also impact Apple’s dominance, but legal battles are expected to dominate the near‑future landscape.
