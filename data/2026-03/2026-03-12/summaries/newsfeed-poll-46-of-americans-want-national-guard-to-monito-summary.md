# Poll: 46% of Americans want National Guard to monitor voting

## Key Findings
- 46% of Americans support deploying the National Guard to polling places for the November midterm elections; 54% oppose.
- Party split is stark: roughly three‑quarters of Republicans favor the idea, while a similar proportion of Democrats oppose it.
- About two‑thirds of respondents say they trust state or local officials to run fair and accurate elections, though confidence has slipped since the 2024 cycle.
- Concern sources differ by affiliation: Democrats cite voter suppression and misinformation; Republicans point to voter fraud; independents are roughly evenly split among the three concerns.
- Overall trust in the election process is declining across the political spectrum, and rhetoric questioning results has become more common among politicians of both parties.

## Legal Context
- Federal law bars the federal government from sending soldiers to polling places; however, state governors may legally use their National Guard for election‑related duties such as cybersecurity or security support.
- Experts suggest recent international tensions (e.g., the Iran conflict) and domestic terror threats may make the public more receptive to Guard presence for security reasons.

## Related Poll Results
- More than 80% of Americans worry that artificial intelligence will spread misinformation about voting in the upcoming midterms.
- If the congressional midterms were held today, 53% of registered voters would support the Democratic candidate in their district, compared with 44% for the Republican candidate.
- 38% of respondents approve of Donald Trump’s performance as president, a figure unchanged from the prior month’s poll.

## Methodology
- The poll was conducted by NPR/PBS News/Marist from March 2‑4, 2026, surveying 1,591 adults.
- Margin of error: ±2.8 percentage points.
- Respondents were contacted via live callers, text, and online surveys, in both English and Spanish.
