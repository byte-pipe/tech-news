# One vaccine may provide broad protection against many respiratory infections and allergens

## Overview
- Stanford Medicine researchers created an intranasal vaccine that protects mice from a wide range of respiratory viruses, bacteria, and allergens.
- The vaccine, delivered as a nasal spray, provides lung protection for several months and could, if translated to humans, replace multiple seasonal shots and aid pandemic preparedness.

## Vaccine design and immune strategy
- Traditional vaccines rely on antigen specificity, targeting a single pathogen component, which fails when pathogens mutate.
- This new vaccine does not mimic any pathogen part; instead, it mimics the communication signals between immune cells during infection.
- It integrates innate immunity (rapid, broad‑acting) with adaptive immunity (specific, long‑lasting) by sustaining innate activation through T‑cell‑derived cytokine signals.
- The formulation, named GLA‑3M‑052‑LS + OVA, combines toll‑like receptor stimuli with a harmless egg protein (ovalbumin) that recruits T cells to the lungs, maintaining the innate response for weeks to months.

## Experimental findings in mice
- Mice received one or more nasal drops of the vaccine, spaced a week apart.
- After three doses, mice were protected for at least three months against:
  - SARS‑CoV‑2 and other coronaviruses
  - Bacterial pathogens *Staphylococcus aureus* and *Acinetobacter baumannii*
  - House dust mite allergen
- Vaccinated mice showed minimal weight loss, survived infection, and had lungs largely cleared of virus or bacteria, whereas unvaccinated controls suffered severe illness and high mortality.
- The vaccine reduced viral load in the lungs by roughly 700‑fold and enabled a rapid adaptive response to any pathogen that bypassed the innate barrier.

## Significance and future outlook
- By harnessing sustained innate immunity, the vaccine offers a “double whammy” defense: a strong early barrier plus a ready adaptive response.
- If effective in humans, it could eliminate the need for multiple annual vaccines and provide immediate protection against emerging respiratory threats.
- The approach builds on observations from the Bacillus Calmette‑Guerin (BCG) vaccine’s cross‑protective effects, now mechanistically linked to prolonged innate activation driven by lung‑resident T cells.
- Further research will focus on safety, dosing, and translation to human trials, with the potential to create a universal respiratory vaccine delivered via a simple nasal spray.
