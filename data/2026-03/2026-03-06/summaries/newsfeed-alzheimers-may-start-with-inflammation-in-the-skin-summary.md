# Alzheimer’s may start with inflammation in the skin, lungs or gut

## Main findings
- Genomic analysis of >85,000 Alzheimer’s patients and 485,000 controls shows that risk‑gene expression is low in the brain but high in peripheral barrier tissues (skin, lungs, gut, spleen) and circulating immune cells.  
- Many risk genes regulate immune responses, suggesting that peripheral inflammation could trigger the disease decades before cognitive decline.  
- Highest expression of these genes occurs around ages 55‑60, a period when mid‑life inflammation appears most likely to lead to later Alzheimer’s.  

## Supporting evidence
- A Hawaiian cohort linked elevated inflammatory markers in men’s late 50s to higher Alzheimer’s incidence in their 70s‑80s.  
- Other studies find clustering of Alzheimer’s‑associated variants in skin and lung tissue, and epidemiological links between diverse inflammatory conditions (eczema, pneumonia, gum disease, diabetes, hypertension, gut infections) and later Alzheimer’s, especially when inflammation occurs in midlife.  
- Research shows cytokines and immune cells can cross the blood‑brain barrier, which becomes more permeable with age, allowing peripheral inflammation to affect brain connectivity.  

## Implications for treatment
- Current drugs targeting brain amyloid‑tau aggregates have limited success because they address a downstream effect rather than the initiating cause.  
- If peripheral inflammation is causal, therapeutic strategies should focus on:
  - Reducing systemic inflammation in midlife.
  - Enhancing barrier integrity of the blood‑brain barrier.
  - Vaccination strategies that modulate immune responses (e.g., shingles vaccine reduces Alzheimer’s risk by ~50 %; BCG vaccine lowers risk by ~20 %).  

## Expert commentary
- Neuroscientists stress the need to view Alzheimer’s as a whole‑body disease rather than solely brain‑centric.  
- While associations are strong, causation has not yet been definitively proven; further functional studies are required.  

## Outlook
- A shift toward early, systemic anti‑inflammatory interventions could reshape Alzheimer’s prevention and treatment, mirroring how understanding obesity genetics redirected drug development toward brain‑based appetite regulation.
