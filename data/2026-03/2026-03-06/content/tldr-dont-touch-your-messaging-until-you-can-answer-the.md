description: 'Don’t touch your messaging until you can answer these uncomfortable questions. Before we build a homepage, founders and marketing leaders have to decide what they actually stand for. I make sure that happens, and these are the questions that force it. 1. Who are we willing to lose? Not “who can use this.” Who are we deliberately not building for, even if they would pay? 2. What broken status quo are we attacking? If you can’t name the enemy or challenge an established assumption, you’re not messaging. 3. What are we willing to make obsolete? This complements 2. Solid products kill behaviors. Does your solution remove the need for a certain role or manual process? If nothing dies when you win, you’re incremental. And that doesn’t command conviction. 4. What belief must shift for someone to buy this? Every meaningful purchase requires a belief change. “We can’t scale this manually anymore.” “This is costing more than I thought.” “This isn’t a people issue, it’s a system issue.”
  (quoting client words from recent workshops). 5. Which single job are we best in the world at? If your homepage lists 12 things you can help your target buyers with, you haven’t decided. 6. If we had to cut 70% of our homepage, what would survive? Not what’s “important” but “what’s essential”? What is the single idea that must land for someone to feel understood and believe that what you sell will help them. 7. What are we willing to say that others won’t? If your message doesn’t risk disagreement, it won’t earn strong agreement either. Sure, safe messaging doesn’t get criticized. But it also doesn’t get chosen. If these answers are fuzzy, messaging becomes expensive therapy. Endless debates. Reopened decisions. “Can we soften this?” v9. The companies that get disproportionate returns from messaging work aren’t more creative. They’re more decided. Because one thing I can tell you after running 60+ engagements, is this: messaging doesn’t create clarity. It exposes whether clarity already
  exists. If these questions feel uncomfortable, good. That’s where the real work starts.'
