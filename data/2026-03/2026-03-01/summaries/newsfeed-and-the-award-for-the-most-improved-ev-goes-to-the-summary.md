# And the award for the most improved EV goes to… the 2026 Toyota bZ  

## Background  
- Toyota, the world’s largest automaker, has historically favored hybrids over pure‑electric vehicles.  
- Its first BEV, the bZ4x, suffered a recall and received mixed reviews, highlighting the need for a better EV offering.  

## 2026 bZ Refresh  
- Platform: Updated e‑TNGA (shared with Lexus and Subaru).  
- Naming: Simplified to “bZ”.  
- Battery: New 74.7 kWh pack, with silicon‑carbide power electronics.  
- Charging port (North America): Tesla‑style NACS socket replaces CCS1.  

## Test Model – XLE FWD Plus  
- Price: $37,900.  
- EPA range: 314 mi (505 km), up from 252 mi on the pre‑facelift version.  
- Power: 221 hp (165 kW) front‑wheel drive, 0‑60 mph in ~8 s.  
- Dimensions: 184.6 in (L) × 73.2 in (W) × 65 in (H).  

## Driving Experience  
- Acceleration is modest; the car feels “mellow” rather than sporty.  
- Four regenerative‑braking levels; no true one‑pedal mode (brakes engage below ~3–4 mph).  
- Comfortable city driving; Eco mode is deliberately slower, which suits low‑speed streets.  

## Efficiency & Charging  
- Real‑world efficiency reached 5.7 mi/kWh (≈10.9 kWh/100 km) on mixed driving.  
- Fast‑charging: 150 kW peak, 10 %→80 % in ~30 min (not tested due to sufficient range).  
- Comes with adapters for CCS1 and J1772 chargers; charging port placement favors close parking to V3 Tesla Superchargers.  

## Interior & Practicality  
- Standard 14‑inch infotainment screen, dual wireless phone chargers, and four USB‑C ports.  
- Ample storage: 27.7 cu ft (783 L) cargo volume with seats up, generous rear door openings, under‑floor cargo AC power.  
- Seating: Quick‑warming seat heaters, adequate legroom (spec sheet lists 35.3 in rear, feels more spacious).  

## Verdict  
- The 2026 bZ transforms from an “also‑ran” to a recommendable EV, thanks to significant range gain, efficiency, and a sub‑$40 k starting price.  
- Competes well against other sub‑$40 k EVs, offering a solid daily driver without the dealer hassles of some rivals.  
- A cheaper FWD variant saves $3 k but loses ~78 mi (125 km) of range; a “Woodland” version adds rugged styling.
