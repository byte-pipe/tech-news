# AI Ate the Homework: What Communities Are Actually For Now

## Why Community Meant So Much Early On  
- Learning to code was driven by the sense of belonging found on Twitter and online coding groups.  
- Asking questions gave both information and validation; it showed you weren’t alone.  
- Helping others created personal growth and tangible satisfaction.  
- Virtual Coffee grew because it offered a safe space for “I don’t know” moments and peer guidance.  

## The 2024 Turning Point  
- AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) can answer typical coding questions in seconds.  
- The speed and availability of AI introduced tension: people feel guilty asking questions that AI could solve and worry about “doing enough homework.”  
- The implicit expectation shifted: try AI first, then turn to the community.  

## The Numbers Tell the Story  
- Stack Overflow traffic fell 14 % month‑over‑month from March to April 2023, right after GPT‑4 launch.  
- By December 2024, new questions were down 60 % YoY.  
- Overall question volume is 75 % lower than its 2017 peak and 76 % lower since ChatGPT’s November 2022 release.  

## How the Unspoken Contract Changed  

### 2020‑2021  
- Basic questions were welcomed; community was the primary learning resource.  
- Horizontal mentorship: anyone could ask, anyone could answer.  

### 2025‑2026  
- Expectation to try AI before posting to avoid “wasting” others’ time.  
- Communities are now seen as places for questions AI can’t answer.  
- Implicit frustration with repeat basic queries.  

## What Communities Are Actually For Now  
- Human judgment, experience, and connection:  
  - Career decisions (“Take this job or stay?”)  
  - Production‑level implementation details  
  - Company culture insights  
  - Burnout and personal well‑being advice  
  - Peer review of projects  
  - Balancing personal crises with work demands  

## The Sustainability Crisis  
- **For builders:** deciding whether to answer AI‑solvable questions (risking learned helplessness) or redirect (risking unwelcoming perception).  
- **For members:** uncertainty about what is appropriate to ask, guilt over not trying AI, and fear of being redirected.  
- Psychological safety that once underpinned communities is eroding.  

## The Uncomfortable Questions We Need to Ask  

### About AI Expectations  
- How to acknowledge AI’s role without alienating newcomers?  
- How to handle unequal AI access among members?  
- How to transition from “Q&A community” to “judgment & experience community”?  
- Which questions truly require human input?  
- Is “try ChatGPT first” gatekeeping or a reasonable boundary?  

### About Community Purpose  
- Should we aim to be everything or focus on a specific niche?  
- Can quick‑answer users and deep‑connection seekers coexist?  
- What happens when the majority only wants fast answers?  
- How to support basic‑help seekers without burning out volunteers?  

### About Sustainability  
- Can volunteer‑run communities survive when easy questions disappear?  
- How to make helping rewarding again with only hard questions left?  
- What is the minimal viable community when AI handles the basics?  

## What Actually Works in 2026  
- Acceptance of the new purpose: not trying to be a Stack Overflow replacement, but a space for nuanced discussion and human judgment.  
- Welcoming tone with clear boundaries: suggesting AI for simple issues while maintaining psychological safety.  
- Separation of transaction (quick help) from connection (deep belonging).  
- Recognition that drive‑by participants are fine; the myth of constant engagement is obsolete.  
- Building for the people who truly need the community’s human expertise.
