# Jury finds Meta liable in case over child sexual exploitation on its platforms

## Background
- New Mexico Attorney General Raúl Torrez sued Meta in 2023, alleging the company created a “breeding ground” for child predators on Facebook and Instagram.  
- The lawsuit claimed Meta failed to warn users about platform dangers and knowingly designed features that harmed children.  
- The trial in Santa Fe began with a six‑week schedule, featuring testimony from current and former Meta employees, whistleblowers, and details from an undercover investigation that led to three arrests.

## Verdict and damages
- The jury found Meta liable on all counts, including:
  - Willful false or misleading statements about platform safety.  
  - Unfair, deceptive, and “unconscionable” trade practices.  
- Meta was ordered to pay **$375 million** in damages.  
- The award is lower than the billions sought by the state, but additional penalties and platform‑change orders may be imposed by the judge later.

## Key testimonies
- **Arturo Bejar**, former engineering director, warned executives after his 14‑year‑old daughter received sexual solicitations on Instagram and said personalized algorithms helped predators locate children.  
- **Brian Boland**, former Vice President of Partnerships, testified that safety was not a priority for CEO Mark Zuckerberg and COO Sheryl Sandberg.  
- **Adam Mosseri**, Instagram head, testified that Meta introduced safety features such as “Teen Accounts” despite negative effects on growth and engagement.  
- **Ravi Sinha**, Head of Child Safety Policy, described Meta’s technology and cooperation with law enforcement to combat child exploitation.  

## Corporate response
- Meta’s spokesperson said the company “respectfully disagrees” with the verdict and will appeal.  
- The company emphasized its investment of 40,000 staff members in safety, its “longstanding commitment” to young people, and the difficulty of fully eliminating bad actors.  
- Meta announced it will discontinue end‑to‑end encrypted direct messages on Instagram, citing low user uptake, while retaining the feature on WhatsApp.

## Reactions from the Attorney General
- Torrez called the decision “a historic victory for every child and family” and accused Meta of putting profits over safety.  
- He highlighted that Meta executives were aware of harms, ignored internal warnings, and misled the public.  

## Wider legal landscape
- The case is part of a wave of litigation targeting social‑media platforms for harms to youth, including:
  - A separate trial in Los Angeles against Meta and YouTube over addictive features affecting a young woman’s mental health.  
  - Hundreds of additional lawsuits from individuals, school districts, and other state attorneys general slated for trial later in 2026.  

## Next steps
- Meta plans to appeal the verdict and continue defending its safety record.  
- The judge may later issue orders requiring platform changes and additional penalties beyond the $375 million award.  
- Ongoing investigations and upcoming trials will further shape regulatory and legal pressures on Meta and other tech giants.
