# Meta told to pay $375m for misleading users over child safety

## Verdict and Penalty
- A New Mexico jury ordered Meta to pay $375 million (£279 million) for violating the state’s Unfair Practices Act by misleading the public about the safety of its platforms for children.
- The total penalty reflects thousands of individual violations, each carrying a maximum fine of $5,000.

## Findings of the Trial
- Meta was found liable for endangering children and exposing them to sexually explicit material and sexual predators on Facebook, Instagram and WhatsApp.
- Internal Meta documents and testimony from former employees showed the company was aware of these risks.
- Former engineering leader Arturo Béjar testified that experiments on Instagram delivered sexualized content to under‑age users and recounted that his own daughter was propositioned for sex by a stranger on the platform.
- State prosecutors presented research indicating that, in a single week, 16 % of Instagram users reported being shown unwanted nudity or sexual activity.

## Meta’s Response
- Meta disagreed with the verdict and announced its intention to appeal.
- A company spokesperson said Meta works hard to keep people safe, acknowledges the challenges of identifying bad actors, and remains confident in its record of protecting teens online.
- Recent safety initiatives cited by Meta include the 2024 launch of “Teen Accounts” on Instagram and a new feature that alerts parents if their children search for self‑harm content.

## Legal Context
- New Mexico sued Meta in 2023, alleging that its recommendation algorithms steered young users toward sexually explicit content, child sexual abuse material, and solicitation or sex‑trafficking.
- Meta is also facing a separate Los Angeles trial where a plaintiff claims Instagram and YouTube were intentionally designed to create addiction in children.
- Thousands of similar child‑safety lawsuits are currently moving through U.S. courts.

## Statements from Officials
- New Mexico Attorney General Raul Torrez called the verdict “historic,” noting that Meta executives knew their products harmed children, ignored internal warnings, and lied to the public.
- Torrez said the jury joined families, educators, and child‑safety experts in saying “enough is enough.”
