# Asteroid defense mission shifted the orbit of more than its target

## Background
- On 26 September 2022 NASA’s DART spacecraft impacted Dimorphos, the 160 m moonlet of the binary asteroid Didymos.
- The kinetic impact shortened Dimorphos’s orbital period around Didymos by about 33 minutes.
- Subsequent observations show the impact also altered the heliocentric orbit of the entire Didymos system.

## Observation methods
- Researchers led by Rahil Makadia combined:
  - 22 stellar occultations of Didymos captured between Oct 2022 and Mar 2025.
  - ~6 000 ground‑based astrometric measurements spanning 29 years from the Minor Planet Data Center.
  - Optical navigation data from DART’s approach.
  - Ground‑based radar observations.
- Stellar occultations, where an asteroid blocks a distant star, provided precise position data to detect the tiny trajectory change.

## Impact findings
- The impact reduced the along‑track velocity of the Didymos system by roughly 11.7 µm s⁻¹.
- Although minute, an early impulse can accumulate over years, producing a measurable shift in the system’s solar orbit.

## Ejecta momentum (“ejecta engine”)
- The impact expelled rock and dust, creating an additional thrust beyond the spacecraft’s momentum.
- This effect is expressed by the momentum enhancement factor β; a value of β ≈ 2 was derived for DART.
- About half of the ejecta escaped the binary system, delivering a push comparable to the spacecraft’s direct impact.

## Asteroid densities
- By linking the heliocentric deflection to the local orbital change, bulk densities were inferred:
  - Didymos (primary): ~2.6 t m⁻³, typical for silicate asteroids.
  - Dimorphos (moonlet): ~1.51 t m⁻³, indicating a porous “rubble‑pile” structure.
- The density contrast suggests Dimorphos formed from material shed by Didymos via the YORP spin‑up mechanism.

## Planetary‑defense implications
- The altered heliocentric orbit does not increase Earth impact risk; closest approach remains ~15 lunar distances for the next ≈ 100 years.
- DART demonstrated that striking a secondary body can effectively deflect an entire binary system, validating kinetic‑impact models.
- Larger spacecraft could produce proportionally larger deflections for future threats.

## Future verification
- ESA’s Hera mission, arriving at Didymos in late 2026, will perform in‑situ measurements of densities, gravitation, and physical properties.
- Hera’s data will refine the current calculations and may reveal additional insights about the system.
