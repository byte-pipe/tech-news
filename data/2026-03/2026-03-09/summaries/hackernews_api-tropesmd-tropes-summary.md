# AI Writing Tropes to Avoid

## Word Choice
- **Overused adverbs** – “quietly”, “deeply”, “fundamentally”, etc., are used to make ordinary statements sound important.  
- **“Delve” family** – verbs like “delve”, “utilize”, “leverage”, “harness”, “robust”, and “streamline” appear far too often.  
- **Grandiose nouns** – words such as “tapestry”, “landscape”, “paradigm”, “synergy”, “ecosystem”, and “framework” replace simpler terms.  
- **“Serves as” dodge** – fancy replacements for the copula (“is/are”) like “serves as”, “stands as”, “marks”, or “represents”.

## Sentence Structure
- **Negative parallelism** – the “It’s not X — it’s Y” pattern (or “not because X, but because Y”) creates false profundity.  
- **Countdown negation** – “Not X. Not Y. Just Z.” builds tension by denying multiple options before the point.  
- **Rhetorical‑question answer** – “The X? A Y.” asks an unnecessary question and answers it immediately.  
- **Anaphora abuse** – repeating the same sentence starter in rapid succession.  
- **Tricolon abuse** – overusing the rule‑of‑three, often chaining several tricolons together.  
- **“It’s worth noting” filler** – transitional phrases that add no real connection (e.g., “Importantly”, “Interestingly”).  
- **Superficial analysis** – attaching vague “‑ing” clauses that claim significance without substance.  
- **False ranges** – “from X to Y” constructions that lack a meaningful spectrum.  
- **Gerund fragment litany** – series of verbless gerund fragments (“Fixing bugs. Writing features.”) that add nothing.

## Paragraph Structure
- **Short punchy fragments** – excessive one‑sentence paragraphs that break flow and mimic a readability‑first training bias.  
- **Listicle in a trench coat** – numbered or labeled points disguised as narrative, leading to a disjointed, list‑like feel.  

These patterns are identified as common AI‑generated writing habits; avoiding them leads to clearer, more human‑like prose.
