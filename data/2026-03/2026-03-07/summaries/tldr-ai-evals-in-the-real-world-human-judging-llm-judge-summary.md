# AI Evals in the Real World: Human Judging, LLM Judges, and the Gaps Between

## Context: what we built and what “success” meant
- I led conversational optimization for an AI‑powered customer service chatbot handling high‑volume inbound traffic across travel, healthcare, education, legal, and other verticals.
- Success meant not only answering questions but also sustaining engagement and capturing contact information for downstream human sales follow‑up.

## Why traditional NLP metrics didn’t work
- Standard metrics failed to capture multi‑turn interaction, intent shifts, and progressive uncovering of user needs.
- We needed a task‑specific framework benchmarked against high‑performing human sales and support interactions.

## Our rubric: three dimensions of quality
1. **Resolution capability** – correct intent inference and accurate, relevant answers.
2. **Commercial effectiveness** – ability to maintain engagement, probe deeper needs, and guide users toward leaving contact information when appropriate.
3. **Conversational naturalness** – pragmatic human feel, discourse coherence, logical structure, appropriate follow‑ups, and smooth flow.

## Our workflow: human evaluation + LLM‑as‑a‑judge
- Subjective evaluation remained primarily human‑led; objective evaluation was automated with LLMs.
- Used two LLM approaches: prompt‑based general‑purpose models and task‑specific fine‑tuned judges.
- When criteria were clear, prompts yielded reliable large‑scale assessments; ambiguous criteria required human‑annotated data to train specialized judges.
- Evaluation loop:
  1. Run automated evaluations at scale.
  2. Spot‑check with humans for quality assurance.
  3. Manually review low‑scoring dialogues for error analysis.
  4. Feed failure patterns back into conversation design.

## What broke in practice: three bottlenecks
- **Annotation capacity and calibration** – building a gold dataset required intensive annotator training and repeated calibration; vertical product managers vetted samples, making the process expensive and slow.
- **Gold data scarcity and synthetic data mismatch** – the gold set remained too small; synthetic conversations generated by LLMs lacked real‑world ambiguity and emotional/topical shifts, leading to over‑optimistic performance estimates.
- **Judge‑model instability on pragmatics** – LLM judges struggled with implicit meanings, user silence, and pragmatic subtleties, producing high score variance; scaling human evaluation to production volume was infeasible, creating tension between scale and reliability.

## The hardest question: what does “human‑like” mean?
- Defining “human‑likeness” required addressing context sensitivity, turn‑taking dynamics, empathy, discourse‑level coherence, and more.
- It raised product‑positioning questions about the expected level of human equivalence and highlighted the need for stakeholder alignment on ambiguous quality criteria.

## What I learned: the structural constraints underneath it all
- Three underlying constraints: model capability, human resources, and scope.
  - Automated evaluation demands LLMs that are robust in pragmatic reasoning.
  - A professionally trained annotation workforce is essential for consistent quality.
  - Evaluation must be a cross‑functional effort to reduce bias and agree on what “good” means.
