- The article introduces Michaeleen Doucleff’s new book *Dopamine Kids*, which offers a science‑based plan for parents struggling with children’s screen time and cravings for ultra‑processed foods.

## Core Scientific Insight  
- Modern neuroscience shows dopamine is not a “pleasure” chemical; it drives **wanting**, not feeling satisfied.  
- Technology and ultra‑processed foods exploit this “wanting” system, creating a feedback loop where the more a child consumes, the stronger the craving becomes.  
- A separate brain system provides genuine satisfaction, but current digital platforms and engineered foods keep the “wanting” system activated while bypassing the satisfaction system.  

## How Tech Hijacks the Brain  
- Tech companies borrow tactics from the gambling industry to maximize engagement (e.g., infinite scroll, progress cues).  
- Social media promises fulfillment of belonging needs but never actually delivers lasting satisfaction, leading to continual dopamine spikes.  
- The result is a mismatch: high desire to keep using the app versus low actual pleasure from the activity.  

## Role of Ultra‑Processed Foods  
- These foods are engineered to be nutritionally sparse yet highly cravable, encouraging overeating.  
- They similarly hijack the dopamine system, reducing appetite for whole, minimally processed foods and reinforcing the craving cycle.  

## Parenting Strategies from the Book  
- **Replace, don’t just remove:** When taking away screens or junk food, offer an alternative activity that satisfies the child’s underlying needs (e.g., autonomy, adventure, physical exercise).  
- Example: Doucleff swapped evening screen time with bike rides, which led Rosy to seek out outdoor activities independently.  
- **Address the “boredom mistake”:** Simply sending a child outside without an engaging purpose can increase frustration because the dopamine drive remains unmet.  
- **Leverage flexibility of the dopamine system:** Parents can “rewire” the reward pocket by linking desirable, healthy activities to the child’s intrinsic motivations.  

## Author’s Goals and Perspective  
- Doucleff aims to give parents practical tools that work with, rather than against, children’s neurobiology, reducing daily conflict and exhaustion.  
- She critiques older parenting advice that relies on outdated psychology and neuroscience, emphasizing the need for up‑to‑date, evidence‑based guidance.  

## Key Takeaways for Parents  
- Recognize that children’s screen use and snack cravings are driven by a wanting loop, not by genuine pleasure.  
- Counteract this loop by providing engaging, autonomy‑supporting alternatives that satisfy the brain’s reward system.  
- Understand that both technology and ultra‑processed foods are designed to keep the dopamine “wanting” system active, so intentional replacement is essential for long‑term well‑being.
