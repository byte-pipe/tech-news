# Summary of “Workers who love ‘synergizing paradigms’ might be bad at their jobs”

## Study Overview
- Cognitive psychologist Shane Littrell (Cornell) introduced the Corporate Bullshit Receptivity (CBSR) Scale to measure how susceptible employees are to vague, impressive‑sounding corporate language.
- The research was published in *Personality and Individual Differences* and involved more than 1,000 office workers across four separate studies.

## Methodology
- Created a “corporate bullshit generator” that produced meaningless but grandiose sentences (e.g., “We will actualize a renewed level of cradle‑to‑grave credentialing”).
- Participants rated the “business savvy” of these generated statements alongside real quotes from Fortune 500 leaders.
- CBSR scores were validated for reliability and then correlated with established cognitive tests of analytic thinking, cognitive reflection, fluid intelligence, and workplace decision‑making.

## Key Findings
- **Higher CBSR scores** were associated with:
  - Perceiving supervisors as more charismatic and “visionary.”
  - Lower performance on analytic‑thinking and fluid‑intelligence tests.
  - Worse scores on a workplace decision‑making assessment.
  - Greater job satisfaction and stronger inspiration from company mission statements.
  - A higher likelihood of spreading corporate BS to others.
- The paradox: employees most enthused by buzzword‑laden rhetoric tend to be the least capable of making effective, practical decisions.

## Implications
- A feedback loop may develop where BS‑susceptible employees elevate leaders who rely on such language, reinforcing dysfunctional communication.
- Excessive corporate BS can act as an “informational blindfold,” increasing reputational and financial risk for organizations.
- The CBSR Scale could eventually help assess job candidates’ analytic and decision‑making abilities.
