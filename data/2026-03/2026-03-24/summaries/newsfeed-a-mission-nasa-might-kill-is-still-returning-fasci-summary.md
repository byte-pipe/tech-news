# Summary of “A mission NASA might kill is still returning fascinating science from Jupiter”

## Recent scientific findings
- Data from NASA’s Juno spacecraft (2021‑2022) show Jupiter’s lightning flashes are at least 100 times more powerful than Earth’s.
- The results were published on March 20 in *AGU Advances*.

## Juno’s operational status and funding uncertainty
- Juno remains healthy after its five‑year primary mission; an extension was granted, but a further extension has not yet been approved.
- The future of Juno and more than a dozen other robotic missions became uncertain after the Trump administration asked for “closeout” plans and proposed cutting NASA’s science budget by nearly half.
- Congress rejected most of those cuts; the FY 2026 budget provides $2.54 billion for NASA’s planetary‑science division, about $220 million less than the previous year.

## Budget pressures and NASA’s decision‑making process
- Louise Prockter, director of NASA’s planetary‑science division, stated that NASA cannot afford to support every existing mission and must make “tough decisions.”
- Five missions are under review: Juno, Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, Curiosity rover, Mars Odyssey (fuel‑limited), and a lost Mars probe.
- The agency will announce its choices in the annual operating plan submitted to Congress; the plan is currently under internal review.
- Operating these missions consumes roughly 10 % of the planetary‑science budget—about $260 million in 2025.

## Trade‑offs between existing and new missions
- Eliminating long‑lived missions could free roughly $260 million per year, equivalent to funding about two Discovery‑class missions over a decade.
- Discovery missions have a development cost cap of <$500 million; New Frontiers missions are similarly cost‑capped and occur less frequently.
- NASA’s launch cadence has slowed since the late‑1990s peak of 11 Discovery missions in 15 years; only three Discovery missions have launched in the past 15 years, with the next not before 2030.

## Scientific value of continuing Juno
- Juno continues to deliver unique observations of Jupiter’s atmospheric dynamics, including cyclones, anticyclones, and the long‑lasting Great Red Spot.
- Earlier missions (Voyager 1 in 1979) first detected Jovian lightning, but cloud cover limited power measurements; Juno’s microwave instrument can directly assess lightning intensity.
- Ongoing discoveries underscore the mission’s relevance and argue for its continued operation.

## Broader context of NASA’s budget choices
- Similar funding debates affect other long‑standing assets, such as the Chandra X‑ray Observatory, which was nearly shut down but saved by congressional direction.
- NASA must balance investment in new flagship missions (e.g., Europa Clipper, Psyche) with the cost of extending existing, scientifically productive spacecraft.
