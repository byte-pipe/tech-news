# A committed pharmacist and a homesick lifestyle blogger – Iranian civilian casualties in the war  

## Overview of the air‑strike campaign  
- For more than three weeks Tehran and other Iranian cities have been hit by US and Israeli airstrikes, targeting thousands of sites.  
- The strikes have caused a rapidly rising civilian death toll, including many children, while the Iranian government imposes an internet blackout and arrests those trying to communicate abroad.  

## Personal stories that illustrate the civilian cost  

### Parastesh Dahaghin – pharmacist  
- Young pharmacist working in Tehran’s Apadana neighbourhood.  
- Killed when an adjacent IT‑company building, linked to the internet shutdown, was hit.  
- Family statements emphasize her dedication to serving wounded patients despite warnings that Tehran was unsafe.  

### Eilmah Bilki – child victim  
- A 3‑year‑old girl from Sardasht severely wounded in early‑March airstrikes; died a day later.  
- Photo supplied by Kurdish human‑rights group Hengaw.  

### Berivan Molani – lifestyle blogger  
- 26‑year‑old online clothes‑shop owner, only child, returned to Tehran from northern Iran the day before her death.  
- Killed in her bed when a missile struck the neighbourhood of Zafaraniyeh, where Iran’s minister of intelligence lives opposite her apartment.  
- Neighbours and family recount the suddenness of the attack and the fatal crush injuries despite rescue efforts.  

## Casualty figures and documentation  
- Human Rights Activists News Agency (HRANA) records >1,400 civilian deaths, 15 % of them children.  
- At least 1,167 Iranian military personnel reported killed (Iran does not publish its own military losses).  
- Notable incident: missile strike on a primary school in Minabat killed 48 children and 10 adults, identified by Hengaw.  

## Impact on health‑care facilities  
- International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC) describes civilian suffering as “alarming.”  
- Red Crescent worker Hamidreza Jahanbakhsh among the dead; several Red Crescent facilities damaged.  
- World Health Organization (WHO) verifies >20 attacks on health‑care sites, at least nine health workers killed, and classifies collateral damage as an attack.  
- Verified footage shows severe damage to Gandhi Hospital in Tehran, a Red Crescent hospital in Mahabad, and a hospital in Bushehr (infants evacuated).  

## Restrictions on information and civilian life  
- Iranian border guards ordered to shoot people using Iraqi phone or internet networks, hindering casualty reporting.  
- Internet blackout and arrests of users trying to contact families abroad create a “heart‑breaking” environment, according to Hengaw’s Awyar Shekhi.  
- No civilian bomb shelters in Tehran; government buildings are interspersed with residential areas, increasing risk.  

## International and legal context  
- International humanitarian law obliges parties to spare civilians and medical personnel; violations constitute attacks on health care.  
- US statements claim it does not target civilians and takes obligations seriously, though investigations into specific strikes (e.g., the Minabat school) are ongoing.  
- Iranian surgeon Dr. Hashim Moazenzadeh, now in France, urges bombers to prioritize protection of hospitals, noting the large bombs and high civilian casualties.
