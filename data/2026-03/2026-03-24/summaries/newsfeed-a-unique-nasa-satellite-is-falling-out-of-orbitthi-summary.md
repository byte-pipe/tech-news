# A unique NASA satellite is falling out of orbit—this team is trying to rescue it

## Background
- The Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory, launched in 2004, is a 21‑year‑old NASA gamma‑ray burst detector still vital for multi‑wavelength astronomy.
- Swift operates in low‑Earth orbit where residual atmospheric drag is slowly lowering its altitude; without intervention it will re‑enter and burn up, likely before the end of 2026.
- No other U.S. satellite currently matches Swift’s rapid repointing capability for transient gamma‑ray events.

## Rescue Mission Overview
- NASA awarded Katalyst Space Technologies a $30 million contract to design, build, test, and launch a commercial servicing spacecraft named **Link** to dock with Swift and raise its orbit.
- The mission aims to demonstrate affordable, risk‑tolerant on‑orbit servicing and extend Swift’s scientific life.
- Swift was never designed for capture; this will be Katalyst’s first attempt to dock with an existing satellite.

## Timeline and Technical Challenges
- Katalyst has nine months to complete the entire effort, with a launch scheduled for 1 June 2026.
- By late summer or early fall Swift will descend below ~200 mi (320 km), a regime where drag makes docking unsafe.
- The company is developing hardware, assembling components, and conducting environmental tests simultaneously, working nights and weekends with a 40‑person team.
- Supply‑chain constraints required rapid vendor swaps and in‑house fabrication of some parts.
- Success is not guaranteed; missing the June launch could render the mission infeasible.

## Business Model and Industry Context
- The contract follows NASA’s commercial‑service approach used for crew and cargo programs, emphasizing lower cost and faster development.
- Previous NASA robotic‑servicing projects (e.g., the canceled $1.5 billion effort) never reached orbit; only Northrop Grumman has completed a commercial servicing of a non‑servicing‑designed satellite.
- Katalyst’s Link is an intermediate platform leading to the larger Nexus spacecraft, intended primarily for U.S. military satellite servicing.
- The $30 million price tag is far below the roughly $500 million total cost of Swift, making the rescue financially attractive compared to building a replacement mission.

## Outlook
- Engineers now predict Swift’s decay will occur between late July and October 2026 due to heightened solar activity and atmospheric expansion.
- If Link launches on schedule and successfully docks, Swift could receive a new lease on orbit, preserving its unique capabilities for future gamma‑ray burst observations.
- Failure to launch in time or to achieve a reliable dock could result in Swift’s uncontrolled re‑entry, ending the mission.
