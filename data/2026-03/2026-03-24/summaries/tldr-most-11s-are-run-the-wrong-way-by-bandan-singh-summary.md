# Most 1:1s are run the wrong way

## Two common assumptions
- Managers often think the 1:1 is either their meeting to drive or that the direct report will come fully prepared, leaving the manager with little to do.  
- Both assumptions hand over the most important part of the conversation before it even starts.

## Who should own the agenda?
- The direct report’s topics should generally come first; the manager’s items follow unless something is truly time‑critical.  
- Front‑loading manager priorities can set the frame before the direct report can open up, limiting the conversation’s depth.

## Recommended flow when the direct report leads
1. **Progress on key priorities** – not a status check but a look at real movement and any roadblocks needing manager help.  
2. **Cross‑functional relationships and team topics** – coach and mentor on collaboration challenges.  
3. **Personal dimensions** – goals, aspirations, well‑being; this is more than small talk and can naturally lead to growth discussions.  
4. **Feedback for the manager** – placed on the agenda so the direct feels safe to give honest input.  
5. **Administrative items** – approvals, FYIs, etc.  

After the direct’s section, the manager shares updates, strategic context, requests for goal updates, and feedback for the direct.

## When both sides have many items
- Follow Bill Campbell’s practice: both participants write down their topics beforehand and share the lists simultaneously.  
- The agenda is built from overlaps and gaps, preventing either side from automatically framing the conversation.

## Importance of preparation
- If the direct sends context ahead of time, the manager should read it; the conversation becomes sharper and shorter.  
- Likewise, the manager should share any needed context in advance to avoid “ambush” questions during the call.

## The realistic view of a 1:1
- A perfect weekly 1:1 is unrealistic; sometimes the manager front‑loads, sometimes there isn’t time for all topics.  
- The goal isn’t to tick off a checklist but to ensure the direct report leaves feeling seen, that blockers are taken seriously, and that their growth matters.  

That is the manager’s whole job.
