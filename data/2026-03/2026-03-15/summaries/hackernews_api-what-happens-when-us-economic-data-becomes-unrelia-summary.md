# What happens when US economic data becomes unreliable | MIT Sloan

## Overview
- The U.S. economy is measured by 13 major statistical agencies that collect data from millions of individuals and establishments.  
- Recent political interference, budget cuts, and low survey response rates have weakened the system, creating public mistrust.

## Consequences of unreliable data
- Policymakers may misjudge the economy’s health.  
- Investors may lose confidence in the data’s reliability.  
- The public may disengage from participating in official surveys.

## Main challenges with U.S. data
1. **Declining survey response rates**  
   - Fewer households and firms answer phone or in‑person surveys, introducing bias, delaying revisions, and reducing representativeness.  
2. **Funding constraints**  
   - Shrinking budgets limit adoption of new technologies and the ability to conduct surveys (e.g., USDA halted its food‑insecurity survey).  
3. **Political interference**  
   - Dismissals of statistical leaders, broken advisory committees, and government shutdowns disrupt data collection and erode transparency and credibility.  
   - Revisions to data are routine and essential, but they are increasingly portrayed as failures or bias.

## Recommendations for business leaders
1. **Use private data, but with caution**  
   - Private‑sector data can complement official statistics, offering independent checks and filling gaps.  
   - Limitations: narrower coverage, commercial incentives that may overlook socially valuable areas, and lack of methodological transparency.  
2. **Speak up**  
   - Companies should publicly challenge policies that threaten data integrity, such as tariffs presented as taxes on firms.  
   - Vigilance helps detect early political manipulation and preserves public trust.
