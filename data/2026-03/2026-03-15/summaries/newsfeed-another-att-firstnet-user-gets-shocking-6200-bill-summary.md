# Another AT&T FirstNet user gets shocking $6,200 bill, at $2 per megabyte - Ars Technica

# Summary of “Another AT&T FirstNet user gets shocking $6,200 bill, at $2 per megabyte”

## Incident overview
- AT&T FirstNet customers have received erroneous $6,200‑plus bills for roughly 3 GB of data usage.
- The first known case involved a Texas police officer in December 2024; the second case involves an active‑duty Florida service member in February 2025.
- Both bills list a charge of “$2 per megabyte,” resulting in a charge of about $6,200 for 3.1 GB of data.

## Details of the Florida case
- Service period: January 19 – February 14, billed as “FirstNet Unlimited.”
- Line item: “FN Data PPU 3,098 MB at $2.00 per MB” totaling $6,196.
- The customer reported the issue to Ars Technica; AT&T corrected the bill within a few hours after the story was published.

## AT&T’s response
- AT&T did not answer specific questions about the cause or any changes made after the December 2024 incident.
- The carrier issued a generic statement apologizing and saying it “quickly addressed their billing concerns,” but did not mention a new investigation.
- Customer‑service numbers provided were the general AT&T support line (1‑800‑331‑0500) rather than the dedicated FirstNet line (1‑800‑574‑7000).

## Billing promises versus reality
- AT&T markets FirstNet as “hassle‑free” billing with unlimited talk, text, and data starting at $42.99 per month per line.
- Official pricing for excess data is $10 per gigabyte; at that rate, 3.1 GB would cost about $31, not $6,200.
- The $2‑per‑MB charge is not explained in FirstNet pricing but appears in AT&T’s legal terms as a possible “pay‑per‑use” rate “up to $2 per MB,” suggesting a misapplied internal charge.

## Possible cause of the error
- AT&T has not disclosed the root cause; the $2‑per‑MB rate may stem from a different part of AT&T’s billing system that was incorrectly applied to FirstNet accounts after a line was moved to the FirstNet platform.

## Context
- FirstNet is a government‑funded public‑safety network promising reliable 5G, no throttling, and up to 20 % savings on family plans.
- The repeated billing errors undermine the “hassle‑free” promise and raise concerns about AT&T’s internal billing controls for the program.
