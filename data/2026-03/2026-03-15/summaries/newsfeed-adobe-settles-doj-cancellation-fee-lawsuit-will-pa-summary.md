# Adobe settles DOJ cancellation fee lawsuit, will pay $75 million penalty

## Background
- Adobe shifted its Creative Cloud suite to a monthly subscription model in 2013, moving customers from costly perpetual licenses (CS6 ranged $700‑$2,600) to plans costing $10‑$70 per month.
- Customers who later tried to cancel faced hidden termination fees: Adobe charged 50 % of the remaining subscription term, often amounting to hundreds of dollars on annual plans.
- The Department of Justice (DOJ) alleged that Adobe concealed these fees in fine‑print or behind hyperlinks and used complex phone trees to make cancellation difficult, violating the 2010 Restore Online Shoppers’ Confidence Act.

## Settlement Details
- Adobe agreed to pay the DOJ a $75 million civil penalty.
- The company will also provide $75 million worth of free services to affected customers, though the exact eligibility criteria and service specifics have not been disclosed.
- Adobe will notify qualifying customers after completing the required court filings.

## Adobe’s Response
- Adobe maintains that it did not violate the law and “disagrees with the government’s claims,” but expressed satisfaction in resolving the matter.
- The settlement does not constitute an admission of wrongdoing.
- Adobe states it has already updated its sales pipeline to make cancellation fees clearer at the point of purchase.

## Business Implications
- Adobe’s subscription revenue continues to grow; the company reported over $7 billion in net profit last year.
- The $75 million payment is relatively small compared with Adobe’s overall earnings, allowing the company to maintain its subscription‑focused strategy.
- The settlement is unlikely to alter Adobe’s current business practices beyond the promised transparency improvements.
