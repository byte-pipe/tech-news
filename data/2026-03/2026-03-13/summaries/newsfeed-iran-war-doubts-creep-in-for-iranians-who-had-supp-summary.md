# Iran war: Doubts creep in for Iranians who had supported war

# What if we're left with ruins? Doubts creep in for Iranians who supported war

## Background
- The conflict began on 28 February with US‑Israeli strikes aimed at degrading Iran’s nuclear and missile capabilities and, according to some officials, achieving regime change.
- Many Iranians who oppose the clerical regime initially welcomed the attacks, hoping they would accelerate political change after the brutal crackdown on nationwide protests.

## Shifting sentiments among opponents of the regime
- **Sama**, a 31‑year‑old engineer in Tehran, celebrated early reports that Supreme Leader Ayatollah Khamenei had been killed, but after two weeks she now fears nightly explosions and cannot sleep.
- **Mina**, a 28‑year‑old teacher, worries that the war may leave the country in ruins while the same mullahs remain in power.
- **Ali**, a 31‑year‑old shopkeeper injured in earlier protests, questions who would govern if the regime fell and fears chaos.
- **Fatima**, a 27‑year‑old graphic designer, says external attacks reinforce the government’s “enemy” narrative and increase street repression.

## Humanitarian impact
- UNHCR estimates 600 000–1 million households (up to 3.2 million people) are temporarily displaced within Iran due to the fighting.
- Massive fires, explosions, and civilian casualties are heightening concerns about long‑term societal damage.

## Concerns about regime change and post‑war stability
- Many interviewees express uncertainty about who would replace the current leadership and whether the country could avoid descending into chaos.
- The possibility of a devastated infrastructure combined with unchanged authoritarian rule is a recurring fear.

## Voices still supporting external military pressure
- **Reza**, a 40‑year‑old engineer in Isfahan, argues that internal change has repeatedly failed and that weakening the regime’s military and security apparatus is essential.
- **Milad**, a software developer in Khuzestan, believes the cost of the regime’s continued rule (economic collapse, repression) outweighs the destruction caused by war.
- These supporters view foreign strikes as the only realistic path to alter the power balance.

## Skepticism about external promises
- **Saeed**, a participant in the January protests, doubts President Trump’s commitment to genuine regime change after his public message promising Iranians a chance to take over their government.
- The inconsistency of external statements fuels mistrust among even the most anti‑regime Iranians.

## Overall assessment
- Initial optimism among anti‑regime Iranians has given way to a complex mix of fear, doubt, and pragmatic resignation.
- While some still see foreign military action as the only viable route to change, many worry that the war may leave Iran in ruins without delivering the hoped‑for political transformation.
