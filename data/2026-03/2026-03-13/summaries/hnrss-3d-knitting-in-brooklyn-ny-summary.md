# 3D‑Knitting: The Ultimate Guide

## What Is 3D‑Knitting?
- Uses Wholegarment machines that knit an entire garment in one seamless piece.
- Design starts as a 3‑D digital model (CAD) and is transferred to any 3‑D‑knitting machine.
- Machines have four needle beds; each bed can knit different parts (e.g., sleeves, body) simultaneously.
- Material utilization reaches about 99 % of the yarn intended for the sweater.

## Why Use 3D‑Knitting?
- Eliminates seams, resulting in superior durability.
- Adjusts every stitch for optimal style and fit.
- Achieves >99 % material efficiency, producing less than 1 % waste.
- Creates lighter garments (≈10 % lighter) without added seam weight.
- Supports the shift from fast fashion to slow, on‑demand fashion with reduced environmental impact.

## When Was 3D‑Knitting Invented?
- Concept originated with Japanese company SHIMA SEIKI; first Wholegarment machine debuted at ITMA 1995.
- Founder Masahiro Shima patented a glove‑knitting machine at age 16, inspiring later Wholegarment development.
- SHIMA SEIKI’s technology has evolved to the 5th‑generation machines used by Oliver Charles for expressive, sustainable designs.

## Pros and Cons of Traditional Methods
- **Cut‑And‑Sew**
  - *Pros*: Very cheap for large‑scale production.
  - *Cons*: Labor‑intensive, generates ~30 % fabric waste, seams are weak points.
- **Flatbed (2D) Knitting**
  - *Pros*: Less waste than cut‑and‑sew, inexpensive at scale.
  - *Cons*: Still requires seams, prone to human error, high labor demand.
- **Circular Knitting**
  - *Pros*: Produces tubular fabrics with fewer seams, stronger garments.
  - *Cons*: Limited fit flexibility, seam vulnerability remains where pieces join.

## Is 3D‑Knitting Better for the Consumer?
- Enables complex stitch patterns (jersey, rib, jacquard, mesh) within a single garment for function or aesthetics.
- Eliminates common defects from cut‑and‑sew (mis‑cuts, raw edges, seam pinching).
- Produces garments ~10 % lighter than seamed equivalents.
- Offers true on‑demand customization (“chipotle effect”) to solve size‑and‑fit returns.
- Anticipated future ability to knit any size, style, material, and color instantly.

## Inventory‑Less Knits
- Production is triggered only after an order is placed.
- Order details are sent to Brooklyn‑based 3D‑knitting partners.
- Machine knits the sweater, which is then plucked, packaged, and shipped directly to the customer—no inventory storage.

## Cost Considerations
- 3D‑knitting is currently more expensive than mass‑produced fast‑fashion items, reflecting higher quality and zero‑waste goals.
- Expected to become cost‑competitive with 2D knitting, offering longer‑lasting garments at lower overall production costs.
- Projected to save up to 40 % of production costs as the technology scales.
