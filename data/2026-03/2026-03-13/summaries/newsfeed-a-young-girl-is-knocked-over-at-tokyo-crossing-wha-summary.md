# A young girl is knocked over at Tokyo crossing – what’s behind Japan’s ‘bumping’ trend?

## Overview
- A video filmed on 25 February at Shibuya’s scramble crossing shows a Taiwanese girl being shoved to the ground by a woman in a mask.  
- The clip quickly went viral (over 1 million Instagram views) and reignited public debate about “butsukari” – deliberate shoulder‑bumping in crowded spaces.  
- The incident highlights a growing pattern of intentional shoving that experts link to stress, gender dynamics and the surge of tourists seeking Instagram moments.

## Recent incidents
- 2018: blurred video of a man barging into several women at Shinjuku Station first brought “butsukari” to national attention.  
- May 2024: a 59‑year‑old associate professor in Fukuoka was arrested for assaulting passersby with his bag; faces up to two years’ imprisonment or a ¥300,000 fine.  
- Other reports include a woman at Tamachi Station who broke a victim’s ribs, prompting the installation of bollard‑separated lanes.  
- Social‑media clips show both men and women deliberately striking strangers with shoulders or bags in dense crowds.

## Causes and motivations
- **Gender politics:** sociologist Kiryu Masayuki notes lingering “men are superior” attitudes combined with modern job‑market anxiety, leading some men to vent frustration on women, whom they perceive as low‑risk targets.  
- **Stress of modern life:** uncertainty about the future erodes traditional masculine identity, making public shoving an outlet for pent‑up tension.  
- **Tourism boom:** large numbers of visitors pause to photograph busy intersections, slowing foot traffic and creating opportunities for intentional bumps.  

## Statistical snapshot
- A 2024 survey of 21,000 Japanese adults found:  
  - 14 % reported being victims of butsukari.  
  - 6 % had witnessed an assault.  
  - 5 % experienced both victimisation and witnessing.  
- No official crime statistics exist because the behaviour is relatively new and often goes unreported.

## Reactions and official advice
- The Chinese embassy in Tokyo warned its citizens to keep distance in crowds and avoid using smartphones while walking after the Shibuya incident.  
- The girl’s mother, who filmed the event, said she was “terrified” and later received online criticism for photographing her daughter, despite following Go Tokyo’s Chinese‑language guidance that permits standing on the crossing to take pictures.  
- Local authorities have begun installing physical barriers (bollards) at stations to separate pedestrian flows.

## Expert commentary
- Kiryu Masayuki (Toyo University, criminal psychology) describes butsukari as “a reflection of modern society,” where outdated gender hierarchies meet contemporary economic pressures.  
- He argues that targeting women is seen as a “low‑risk” way to release frustration, with perpetrators believing they are unlikely to be caught.  

## Broader implications
- The phenomenon illustrates how rapid tourism growth and urban crowding can exacerbate latent social tensions.  
- It raises questions about public‑space etiquette, law‑enforcement response, and the need for clearer guidance for both residents and visitors in densely populated Japanese cities.
