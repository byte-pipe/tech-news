## The 49MB Web Page Phenomenon

The modern web page experience can feel overwhelming, especially when considering the potential distractions and data transfers involved. A recent article analyzed a New York Times webpage to illustrate this phenomenon.

### Key Points:

* The New York Times received 422 network requests for a single webpage loading.
* This resulted in 49 megabytes of data being transferred in two minutes, which is equivalent to about 1/10th the total data used by Windows 95 (28 floppy disks).
* The standard high-quality MP3 song was approximately 4-5 MB long and would take around 12-15 minutes to load alone.
* According to the Internet Telecommunication Union, most websites have average broadband internet speeds of about 1.5 Mbps.
* However, this speed is often inadequate for handling the complexity of modern web page requests.

### Abstraction and Bloat

The article further explained that despite the advancements in hardware, the modern framework/ad-tech stack has not led to significant progress in terms of abstraction and reduced bloat. Instead:

* Publishers use complex ad auction systems in their browsers.
* Ad tracking is enabled through JavaScript code download and compilation on the client-side.
* Interstitial ads can be injected silently into the browser without user consent, causing a "relentless heartbeat" of surveillance.

### Summary

In conclusion, the 49MB webpage phenomenon is a real issue that affects many websites today. The increasing complexity of modern web page requests has led to significant data transfers and slow loading times, often due to ad injection mechanisms in browsers. This problem may require attention from designers, publishers, and users alike to create more efficient and user-friendly online experiences.
