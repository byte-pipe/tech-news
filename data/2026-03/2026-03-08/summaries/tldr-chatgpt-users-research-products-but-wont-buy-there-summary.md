# ChatGPT users research products but won't buy there, forcing OpenAI to rethink its commerce strategy

## Key developments
- OpenAI is abandoning direct checkout inside ChatGPT; future purchases will be routed through partner apps (Instacart, Target, Expedia, Booking.com, etc.).
- The original checkout feature launched in September 2025 with partners such as Shopify, Etsy, and Stripe but saw very low merchant adoption.
- Integration required manual onboarding of each retailer, and OpenAI had not yet built systems for collecting and remitting state sales taxes as of February 2026.

## Reasons for the shift
- Only a handful of merchants (e.g., about a dozen of Shopify’s millions) actually sell through AI tools; the bottleneck is on the AI companies’ side.
- Users tend to research products in ChatGPT but do not complete purchases within the chatbot.
- The hands‑on onboarding process and lack of tax infrastructure made the direct‑checkout model unsustainable.

## Implications for OpenAI
- Revenue from sales commissions that OpenAI expected from direct checkout will now flow to app partners, reducing potential earnings.
- With only ~5 % of ChatGPT users paying for the service, OpenAI may increase ad exposure, though ad revenue in chatbots has proven unreliable (e.g., Perplexity’s experience).
- The move adds pressure ahead of OpenAI’s planned IPO, especially as competitor Anthropic gains ground in the enterprise market and moves closer to profitability.

## Broader context
- Amazon holds a $15 billion stake in OpenAI with an option for up to $50 billion, likely aiming to channel ChatGPT commerce toward its own platform after previously restricting AI access to its product data.
- OpenAI continues to spend more than it generates and is seeking new income streams beyond subscriptions.
