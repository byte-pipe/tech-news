# Summary of “I’m obviously taking a risk here by advertising emoji directly.” – Unsung  

## Background  
- When the iPhone launched (2007) it had no emoji support.  
- Only in 2011, with iOS 5, did Apple ship a universal emoji keyboard.  
- Between 2008 and 2011 emoji existed solely on Japanese‑market iPhones.  

## Early Hacks  
- A developer discovered that toggling a hidden preference called `KeyboardEmojiEverywhere` enabled emoji on any iPhone.  
- The toggle required command‑line or configuration‑file edits, which were too technical for most users.  

## App‑Based Workarounds  
- Developers wrapped the toggle in simple iOS apps and posted them to the App Store.  
- Apple initially rejected some of these apps (e.g., “Freemoji”).  
- To bypass rejection, developers hid the toggle inside unrelated apps:  
  - **FrostyPlace** – a $0.99 RSS reader that unlocked emoji after a specific tapping sequence.  
  - **Spell Number** – a free app where entering a secret number (originally 9876543.21, later 91929394.59) enabled emoji; the setting persisted even after the app’s removal.  

## Apple’s Reaction  
- After a period of resistance, Apple seemed to relent; developers could openly advertise emoji support.  
- An Ars Technica interview (2009) with the creator of “Typing Genius – Get Emoji” shows the developer acknowledging the risk but expecting Apple to “turn a blind eye.”  

## Universal Availability  
- iOS 5 (2011) finally provided an official emoji keyboard, though users still needed to enable it in Settings.
