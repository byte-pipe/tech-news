# Working and Communicating with Japanese Engineers | TokyoDev

## Introduction
- International developers in Japanese companies often encounter communication friction, regardless of the language mix used in the team.  
- Poor communication can cause wasted effort, bugs, and low morale.  
- While learning technical Japanese helps, effective communication requires broader strategies.  
- The author draws on 10 years of experience in Japan, including six years at Mercari, first as an English‑Japanese interpreter and later as a software engineer, to share practical advice.

## Advice Overview
- Make your English more understandable  
- Practice technical terms in Japanese  
- Create new meeting strategies  
- Look beyond language  

## Make Your English More Understandable
- Be aware of speaking habits that can overwhelm non‑native listeners.  
- Avoid the misconception that speaking slowly or loudly is helpful; it can feel condescending.  
- Reflect on the following when speaking:
  - Are sentences run‑on?  
  - Do I jump between ideas?  
  - Am I using vague or overly complex vocabulary?  
  - Do I rely on slang, idioms, or corporate buzzwords?  
  - Is my message direct or overly indirect?  
  - Are my pronunciations clear?

### Scenario 1 – Simplifying a PM’s Message
**Original (buzzword‑heavy):**  
“...focus on user‑facing touchpoints, reduce sign‑up friction, 10x the stickiness of the landing page, pivot the North Star from legacy technical debt to user retention…”

**Simplified (low‑context):**  
- Stakeholders noticed the landing‑page UI is complicated and many users abandon sign‑up.  
- Goal for the week: simplify the sign‑up process and increase user retention.  
- Suggest shifting priority from technical debt to UI improvements.

### Scenario 2 – Clarifying a Developer’s Update
**Original (stream of consciousness):**  
Detailed log of timeout, API policy changes, missing auth token, reducer behavior, etc.

**Simplified:**  
- The bug: users cannot save profiles because the app clears login info before sending the update.  
- Cause: the page updates too quickly, wiping the data.  
- Planned fix: hold the data for a few extra seconds.

## Other Best Practices
- **Give context first:** state the goal, then the actions.  
- **Standardize vocabulary:** avoid interchangeable synonyms (e.g., release / deploy / ship) that confuse non‑native speakers.  
- **Use clear transitions:** “However…”, “Because of that…”, “First, …”, “Finally, …”.  
- **Check for understanding:** ask open‑ended prompts like “Is there any part I should explain again?” instead of “Do you understand?”  
- **Prefer active voice:** “The engineer deployed the code” rather than “The code was deployed by the engineer.”  

These practices improve clarity in any team, not only in international tech groups.

## The Better Your English, the Harder It Is
- At Mercari, the author observed that native English speakers often assumed a shared linguistic background, making their speech harder for Japanese teammates to follow.  
- Non‑native speakers who consciously simplify their language tend to be better understood, highlighting the importance of intentional communication adjustments.
