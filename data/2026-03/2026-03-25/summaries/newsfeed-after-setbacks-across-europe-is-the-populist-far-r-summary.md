# After setbacks across Europe, is the populist far right losing ground?

## Recent electoral setbacks
- **France:** Centrist and left‑wing coalitions won mayoral races in Paris, Lyon and Marseille; the National Rally (RN) failed to capture the major cities but kept control of many smaller towns and mid‑size municipalities.
- **Slovenia:** Liberal Prime Minister Robert Golob’s Freedom Movement narrowly defeated former prime minister Janez Jansa’s Slovenian Democratic Party (29 seats to 28), creating a fragile centre‑left bloc.
- **Italy:** A constitutional referendum on Giorgia Meloni’s judicial reforms was rejected (53.5 % “no” vs 46.5 % “yes”), signalling a significant rebuke of the right‑wing government ahead of next year’s general election.

## Analysts’ interpretations
- **Mixed picture:** Gabor Scheiring (Georgetown University) says the results show a “mixed picture” and warn against simplistic narratives.
- **Ceiling in major cities:** The far right appears to have hit a ceiling in large urban centres while still expanding its base in smaller towns (e.g., RN’s success in Nice under Eric Ciotti).
- **Polling strength:** Jacobin’s David Broder notes that RN’s overall polling is “better than ever,” but questions whether it can achieve an absolute majority.
- **Structural factors:** Persistent economic stagnation, low real wages, deindustrialisation and housing costs continue to fuel far‑right support across Europe.

## Role of personality‑driven populism
- **Vulnerability:** Parties built around charismatic leaders (e.g., Meloni in Italy, Viktor Orban in Hungary) are strong assets but become vulnerable when those leaders face setbacks.
- **Hungary example:** Orban’s dominance may mask a lack of party depth; voters may prioritize practical concerns such as the economy and energy prices over ideological appeals.

## Longer‑term outlook
- **Tactical vs. lasting shift:** Recent defeats may represent tactical losses rather than a permanent decline; the far right’s underlying support base remains largely intact.
- **“Illiberal pendulum”:** Historical cycles show the far right surging, faltering, and then the centre regaining ground, only to swing back if structural problems are not addressed.
- **Upcoming elections:** Results of Denmark’s snap election (expected to keep the Social Democrats as the largest party) will further test the resilience of right‑wing populism in the region.
