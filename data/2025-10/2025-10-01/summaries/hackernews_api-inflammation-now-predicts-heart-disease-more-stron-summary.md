# Inflammation now predicts heart disease more strongly than cholesterol | Empirical Health

**Predicting Heart Disease with Inflammation: A Stronger Predictor Than Cholesterol**

Chronic inflammation has long been linked to an increased risk of heart disease, but prior to now, it was not considered a primary predictor compared to cholesterol. According to the American College of Cardiology (ACC), the current approach may be flawed.

**The Rise of Inflammation as a Predictor**

The ACC recommends universal screening of inflammatory biomarkers, such as **hs-CRP** (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein), in both primary and secondary prevention patients in combination with cholesterol. This approach aims to identify individuals at high risk who may benefit from more aggressive treatment.

**Why Cholesterol is No Longer the Primary Predictor**

Cholesterol has become a victim of its own success, as widespread screening for high levels often leads to statin therapy, even among those without traditional cardiovascular risk factors. In contrast, **inflammation** remains a key driver of heart disease events in certain populations, particularly those on statins or without traditional risk factors.

**Red Flags: What Can You Do to Lower Inflammation?**

The ACC has reviewed clinical trials evaluating the use of anti-inflammatory medications and lifestyle interventions for reducing inflammation and cardiovascular risk. These investigations have identified several promising areas for improvement:

*   **Infliximab (TNF inhibitor)**: 150 participants with chronic heart failure showed no improvement or worsening in Clinical Status, with the highest death rate occurring at high doses.
*   **IVIG**: 2314 participants received intravenous immunoglobulin treatment, showing a range of outcomes depending on NYHA class.

**A New Era in Heart Health Management**

The ACC's recommendation highlights the importance of prioritizing inflammation as a predictor of heart disease. By measuring inflammatory biomarkers and addressing underlying chronic conditions, clinicians can provide more effective care for individuals at high risk. As research continues to uncover the complexities of inflammation and its role in cardiovascular health, it is likely that this approach will become even more refined and effective in managing heart disease risk.
