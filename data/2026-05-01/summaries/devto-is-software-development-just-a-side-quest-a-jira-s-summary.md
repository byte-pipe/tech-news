# Is Software Development Just a Side Quest? A Jira Story

## Overview
- The author, a senior developer who informally acts as a tech lead, reflects on the amount of time spent updating Jira tickets versus actually coding.  
- The post questions whether excessive ticket maintenance turns development into a “side quest.”

## Personal Experience
- Works on a government system where failures have real consequences.  
- Performs typical senior duties: coordinating work, unblocking teammates, interacting with stakeholders, testers, and other teams.  
- Enjoys having a tracking tool for visibility, history, and clarity, but prefers a simple workflow: **todo → in progress → done**.

## Issues with Jira Overhead
- Additional fields, statuses, and customizations are introduced to improve reports, burn‑down charts, or dashboards.  
- These incremental changes gradually increase mental load, making the board harder to understand and maintain.  
- Developers are often blamed for “not keeping Jira up to date,” adding friction to the development process.  
- The author observes that the most effective developers tend to engage minimally with the tool, focusing on solving real problems instead.

## Consequences
- Time that could be spent coding is consumed by administrative tasks.  
- The board can become a “boiling frog” situation: small tweaks accumulate until the system feels overly complex.  
- Frustration builds when the tool feels like a full‑time job rather than a support aid.

## Community Reflection
- The author asks readers whether they share this frustration and if anyone has found a sustainable system that avoids turning Jira into a full‑time responsibility.  
- Invites discussion on balancing necessary tracking with minimal overhead.
