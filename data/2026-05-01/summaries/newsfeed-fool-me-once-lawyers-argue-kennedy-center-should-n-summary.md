# ‘Fool me once…’ Lawyers argue Kennedy Center should not meet same fate as the East Wing

## Key lawsuits
- Two separate suits seek to stop a two‑year closure of the Kennedy Center for renovations.  
- One is filed by Rep. Joyce Beatty, an ex‑officio trustee, demanding removal of President Trump’s name from the building and an end to the shutdown.  
- The other is brought by a coalition of cultural‑preservation and architecture groups (including the AIA and the National Trust for Historic Preservation) that wants the Center to submit a detailed renovation plan to Congress for approval before any closure.

## Background
- Less than two months into his second term, President Trump fired the Center’s leadership, replaced Biden‑appointed board members with loyalists, and had the trustees name the venue after himself, despite the law designating the Center as a living memorial to John F. Kennedy.  
- Congress previously authorized $257 million for “capital repair, restoration, maintenance backlog, and security structures,” to be spent by 2029.

## Testimony and factual issues
- Matt Floca, executive director and COO, testified that he was “dumbfounded” by the building’s condition, citing water damage, outdated electrical systems, and degraded concrete.  
- Floca recommended a temporary closure to address safety concerns and meet the spending deadline.  
- Plaintiffs’ lawyers acknowledge the need for repairs but argue no comprehensive renovation plan has been presented to Congress, and the Center has remained operational during prior work.

## Legal arguments
- **Trust and precedent:** Attorney Abbe Lowell invoked the “Fool me once…” adage, warning that Trump’s earlier demolition of the White House East Wing without congressional approval could repeat here.  
- **Scope of work:** Plaintiffs contrast Trump’s vision of a “new and spectacular Entertainment Complex” with Floca’s description of necessary repairs that will not alter the exterior.  
- **Recent alterations:** Lawyers point to already‑made changes—adding Trump’s name to the façade, painting gold columns white, and removing weeping willows—as evidence of a shift beyond simple repair.  
- **Statutory violation:** Rep. Beatty’s suit argues that adding Trump’s name breaches an amendment to the Center’s founding statute, which forbids new memorials or plaques after Dec 2 1983.  
- Government counsel contends the name is an “acknowledgment,” not a memorial; plaintiffs label that claim “brazen and wrong.”

## Additional context
- Both hearings were overseen by U.S. District Judge Christopher Cooper in Washington, D.C.  
- Since Trump’s takeover, there have been further layoffs and departures from the Center’s staff.  
- The lawsuits seek both preservation of the Center’s historic character and adherence to congressional oversight of the funded renovation project.
