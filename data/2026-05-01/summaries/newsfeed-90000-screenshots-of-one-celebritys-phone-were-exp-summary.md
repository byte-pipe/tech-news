# 90,000 Screenshots of One Celebrity's Phone Were Exposed Online | WIRED

## Overview
- A cloud storage bucket with no access controls was found publicly accessible, containing 86,859 screenshots taken from a European celebrity’s phone.  
- The images appear to have been collected using the stalkerware app **Cocospy**, showing private messages, photos, invoices, phone numbers, partial credit‑card numbers, and other sensitive data.  
- Security researcher Jeremiah Fowler (Black Hills Information Security) discovered the repository, reported it to law enforcement and the cloud provider, and notified the victim’s contacts.

## Stalkerware Context
- Stalkerware is malicious software that covertly monitors a target’s device, harvesting texts, photos, location, and app content.  
- Cocospy marketed itself as “parental control, tracking, and remote surveillance,” offering features such as screenshot capture, WhatsApp chat reading, location alerts, and browsing‑history access.  
- The app required brief physical access to install on Android devices and claimed to operate “100 % discreetly.”  
- Earlier in 2024, Cocospy and related apps suffered a breach that exposed millions of user email addresses.

## Details of the Exposed Data
- Screenshots captured private conversations with models, influencers, and other high‑profile individuals.  
- Business‑related information included invoices, payment details, and partial credit‑card numbers.  
- The dataset spanned roughly mid‑2024 to mid‑2025.  
- The repository’s name, “Cocospy,” directly linked it to the spyware tool.

## Broader Implications
- Victims of stalkerware not only lose personal privacy but also endanger anyone they communicate with, creating a cascade of exposure.  
- Data leaks from such surveillance can lead to harassment, identity theft, and further targeting, especially against women who are disproportionately affected by technology‑facilitated abuse.  
- Researchers note a growing trend of men using online communities to share non‑consensual images, doxx personal details, and purchase hacking tools for abuse.

## Response and Mitigation
- Fowler contacted the cloud service, which then reached out to the data owner to secure the bucket.  
- The incident was reported to local law enforcement.  
- No public identification of the celebrity or the cloud provider was made.  

## Key Takeaways
- Misconfigured cloud storage can turn already invasive spyware data into a massive public privacy disaster.  
- Stalkerware apps like Cocospy enable comprehensive, hard‑to‑detect surveillance of a target’s entire digital life.  
- The intersection of spyware breaches and existing technology‑facilitated abuse amplifies risks for victims and their contacts.  
- Ongoing vigilance, proper cloud security practices, and legal enforcement are essential to curb such privacy catastrophes.
