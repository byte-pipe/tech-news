# 5 Myths About Legacy Code You Should Stop Believing - DEV Community

## Debunking 5 Myths About Legacy Code

When faced with migrating old frontends into modern tools, developers often face numerous questions and frustrations. However, it's time to challenge these myths that perpetuate the struggles around legacy code migration.

### Myth #1: "Legacy means ancient or exotic."

* **Reality:** Legacy doesn't necessarily mean it's from several decades ago. Sometimes, it's just an outdated version of Angular, a React codebase full of class components, or a Webpack 3 setup that "still works."
* **Understanding:** A tool becomes legacy when the team, ecosystem, or business has moved on but the code hasn't.

### Myth #2: "Legacy is the previous team's fault."

* **Reality:** Understanding the context behind the code is crucial. Maybe the project was frozen for 6 years, the team didn't have experience to migrate, or one person left in 2018. Blaming people oversimplifies the complexity involved.
* **Productivity benefits:** Considering the broader story can lead to more productive discussions.

### Myth #3: "Migrating is just a developer obsession."

* **Reality:** Legacy migration involves significant risks and time investments. The decision should not be based solely on dev enthusiasm, but rather risk management for business impact.
* **Smart decisions:** Sometimes, a simple rewrite or refactor can be effective in minimizing problems.

### Myth #4: "A big-bang rewrite is always an antipattern."

* **Reality:** A clean rewrite can have several advantages, including simplicity, security, performance benefits, and speed. Not every migration requires a full-scale reimplementation.
* **Hybrid approaches:** Combining strategies like strangler and big-bang migrations can be more effective than relying solely on one approach.

### Myth #5: "The strangler pattern is the only correct approach."

* **Reality:** This approach has trade-offs, such as time-consuming development (time), potential frustration for other team members ("never-ending migration"), knowledge of multiple technologies, and messy boundary lines between old and new.
* **Choosing wisely:** The best strategy depends on the project's scope and complexity; a hybrid approach can balance these factors.
