# "Technical Debt Will Bite Us in the Ass": How to Make Non-Technical Stakeholders Actually Care - DEV Community

Here is a concise and informative summary of the article:

**Main Points:**

* The author believes that communicating technical debt to non-technical stakeholders is crucial for solving problems.
* Traditionally, technical teams have talked about their "architecture" and "technical debt," but stakeholders are confused by these terms, thinking they relate to features rather than code quality.

**Key Takeaways:**

* To connect with non-technical stakeholders, it's essential to explain the impact of technical debt (e.g., it can lead to infections, slowing down the codebase). Here are a few examples:
	+ "We need to refactor this code because the architecture is getting messy."
	+ "Imagine you just cut half your finger off. You could properly clean it and put on a bandage..."
* When using technical terms, non-technical stakeholders often think they know what's being discussed but don't see the relevance.
* To bridge the gap, the author suggests finding shared language that focuses on customer needs (e.g., features, revenue, user satisfaction).

**Lessons Learned:**

* The author realized its own communication was ineffective and needed improvement to involve non-technical stakeholders in discussions about code quality.
* Changing the vocabulary used by technical teams can help them connect with product managers who are concerned with feature development, revenue targets, customer satisfaction scores.

**Why This Matters:**

* The article highlights the importance of effective communication between technical and non-technical teams in solving problems related to technical debt.
* It emphasizes that stakeholders need a shared language to understand the value impact of technical decisions, such as refactoring or delaying feature work.
