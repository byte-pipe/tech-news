# "Technical Debt Will Bite Us in the Ass": How to Make Non-Technical Stakeholders Actually Care - DEV Community

## Refactoring: Breaking Through The Technical Debt Gap

We've been having this conversation for years, but it still needs to happen. Non-technical stakeholders typically interpret technical discussions in a way that doesn't align with their priorities. They hear "refactoring" as an optional, cosmetic task, while the actual concerns are more pressing.

They're only interested in features being released on time, profit margins, and customer satisfaction ratings. If we don't address this technical debt now, other parts of our product will be delayed or dropped later due to accumulated issues.

Breaking through this misunderstanding requires a shift in perspective. We need a new vocabulary that acknowledges the complex interplay between code quality, user experience, business goals, and time constraints.

Technical jargon can exacerbate the issue. For example:
* Refactoring is often described as "optional polish," making stakeholders focus on neatness over functionality.
* Discussing technical debt typically leads to complaints about developers wanting a perfect codebase, neglecting other valuable features.
* Architecture is interpreted as a concern that doesn't directly impact users.

The goal isn't to dumb down our conversation but to find common ground. We need to communicate the importance of addressing technical debt and how it affects various aspects of our product, not just features being developed.

## Building A Common Language

Building this bridge requires cooperation across teams and functions:
- **Product managers** can explain customer-centric goals: delivering promised features, meeting revenue targets, staying ahead of competitors.
- **Development teams** see their concerns more objectively: keeping the codebase in a reasonable state, avoiding crashes or performance issues that could harm users.

It's not about communicating simply but finding a shared language to convey problems like:
- Technical debt as an infection causing problems over time
- Refactoring as a critical process for cleaning up messy architecture

Through this bridge-building effort, non-technical stakeholders will adapt their perspective and start considering technical considerations alongside product development priorities. It takes communication, self-awareness, and vocabulary adjustment – ultimately leading to better cooperation in resolving complex situations like technical debt.
