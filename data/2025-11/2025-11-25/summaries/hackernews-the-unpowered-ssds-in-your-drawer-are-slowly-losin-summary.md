# The unpowered SSDs in your drawer are slowly losing your data

*   **Introduction**: Solid-State Disks (SSDs) have largely replaced hard drives in primary storage due to their speed, convenience, and power efficiency.
*  *Main Points:
    1.  SSDs can lose data if not powered for extended periods, which can be a concern for users who store critical information or have sensitive materials on their devices.
    2.  The reliability of SSDs depends on the type of NAND flash used in them; cheaper models with QLC NAND, such as those found in cheap consumer SSDs, can safely retain data for about a year without power, while more expensive TLC NAND models like those in high-end SSDs can retain data for up to 3 years.
    3.  Without proper protection, the voltage stored in Nand cells can be lost from unpowered states, resulting in missing or unusable drives.
*   *Archives are Still Not Suitable as a Long-Term Storage Solution:*
       The reliance on consumer SSDs for long-term data storage puts them at risk of losing integrity during periods without power. Users should consider storing their archives in HDDs instead.

### Additional Advice:

*   Regularly check and update driver software to ensure optimal performance.
*   Consider using a thermal pad or heat sink to maintain the internal temperature of the SSD and prevent damage from temperature fluctuations.
*   Monitor the health and condition of your devices, especially those with sensitive electronic components, to identify potential issues early on.

### FAQs:

*   **Q:** Can I safely use an older SSD for archival purposes?
  A: No, it is not recommended. While some older models might retain data without power for several years, other factors may cause the data loss or corruption.
*   **Q:** Do all solid-state drives (SSDs) have reliable performance?
  A: No, they are prone to data retention deficiency; use in cold storage requires more caution than regular usage.
