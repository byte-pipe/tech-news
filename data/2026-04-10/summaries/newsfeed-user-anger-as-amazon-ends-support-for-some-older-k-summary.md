# Summary of “User anger as Amazon ends support for some older Kindles”

## Key points
- Amazon will stop supporting Kindle and Kindle Fire devices released in 2012 or earlier, effective 20 May 2026.  
- Affected models will no longer be able to purchase, borrow, or download new e‑books from the Kindle Store.  
- Existing downloaded books remain readable; accounts and libraries stay accessible via mobile and desktop apps.  
- Performing a factory reset on the affected devices will render them unusable.  
- Amazon offers discounts to help users transition to newer devices.

## Affected devices
- **Kindle e‑readers**: 1st‑generation (2007), Kindle DX/DX Graphite (2009‑2010), Kindle Keyboard (2010), Kindle 4 (2011), Kindle Touch (2011), Kindle 5 (2012), Kindle Paperwhite 1st Gen (2012).  
- **Kindle Fire tablets**: Kindle Fire 1st Gen (2011), Kindle Fire 2nd Gen (2012), Kindle Fire HD 7 (2012), Kindle Fire HD 8.9 (2012).

## User reactions
- Many long‑time Kindle owners expressed outrage, calling the move “obsolete” and questioning why a simple text device needs updates.  
- Example quotes:  
  - “I have a Kindle Touch that I’ve had since 2013… and suddenly it’s obsolete.” (X user)  
  - “A Kindle is a text device! There is no need for updates.” (another user)  
- Concerns were raised about being forced to replace a perfectly functional device and about ads on newer, discounted Kindles affecting the reading experience.

## Industry commentary
- Analyst Paolo Pescatore: the decision is understandable for security and support reasons; older hardware cannot handle newer, data‑intensive services.  
- Ugo Vallauri (Restart Project): ending support “soft‑bricks” millions of functional devices; estimates suggest up to 2 million Kindles could become obsolete, creating over 624 tons of e‑waste.

## Environmental impact
- Potential generation of more than 624 tons of e‑waste if ~2 million devices are discarded.  
- The move highlights broader concerns about planned obsolescence and electronic waste in the tech industry.
