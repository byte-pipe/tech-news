# Argentina approves Milei’s glacier mining bill amid environmental protests

## Key points
- The lower house of Argentina’s congress passed an amendment to the “glacier law” with 137 votes in favour, easing mining in glacier and permafrost areas of the Andes.
- The bill, promoted by President Javier Milei, targets metals such as copper, lithium and silver and is intended to attract large‑scale mining projects.
- Thousands of protesters gathered outside parliament, displaying slogans like “Water is more precious than gold!” and “A glacier destroyed cannot be restored!”; several activists were arrested.

## Legislative details
- The amendment clarifies ambiguities in the 2010 law, giving provinces greater authority to designate protected versus exploitable periglacial zones.
- Support came from governors of mining‑heavy northern Andean provinces (Mendoza, San Juan, Catamarca, Salta) and from industry representatives seeking legal certainty.
- The Senate had already approved the amendment in February; the deputies’ vote finalised its passage.

## Environmental reaction
- Environmental groups argue the change weakens safeguards for water sources that serve about 70 % of the Argentine population.
- Greenpeace activists scaled a statue outside parliament and unfurled a banner urging lawmakers not to betray the people; seven were detained.
- Scientists note that Argentina’s 17,000 glaciers and rock glaciers have already shrunk by 17 % in the past decade due to climate change, making further exploitation risky.

## Economic context
- Argentina is a major lithium producer, a key material for global tech and green‑energy markets.
- The Central Bank estimates mining exports could triple by 2030 if the sector expands.
- Milei, who denies human‑made climate change, frames the bill as essential for economic growth, claiming environmentalists would rather see the country “starve” than develop.

## Implications
- The new law could open extensive areas of the Andes to mining, potentially increasing export revenues but also threatening water supplies and glacier stability.
- Legal responsibility for environmental protection shifts from a national scientific body to individual provinces, raising concerns over inconsistent enforcement.
- The episode highlights the broader tension in Milei’s agenda between deregulation, labor reforms, and mounting public opposition to environmental roll‑backs.
