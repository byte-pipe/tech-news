# Academics demand apology for scientist investigated for China ties but never charged

## Background
- Jane Ying Wu was a tenured neuroscientist at Northwestern University’s Feinberg School of Medicine.  
- She is a naturalized U.S. citizen born in China.  
- Beginning in 2019, the U.S. National Institutes of Health (NIH) investigated her under President Donald Trump’s “China Initiative.”  
- The investigation ended in December 2023, and Wu was never charged or found to have committed wrongdoing.

## Alleged mistreatment by Northwestern
- During the investigation the university allegedly:
  - Limited Wu’s research activities and partially closed her lab space.  
  - Disbanded her research team and reassigned her NIH grants to white male faculty colleagues.  
  - Cut her salary for “lack of funding” and imposed new requirements to restore funding, giving her only a limited chance to meet them.  
  - Refused to return a previously taken‑away but still‑active grant.  
- In May 2024 the university reportedly told Wu it would close her laboratory permanently, preventing future grant applications.  
- The lawsuit claims university police and Chicago police forcibly evicted Wu from her office, handcuffed her, and admitted her against her will to Northwestern Memorial Hospital’s psychiatric institute less than two months before her death.

## Suicide and lawsuit
- Wu died by suicide in July 2024.  
- Her estate filed a lawsuit alleging discriminatory and unlawful treatment.  
- More than 1,000 academics signed a letter demanding an apology from Northwestern president Henry Bienen and dean Eric Neilson, asserting that the university ignored the principle of “innocent until proven guilty.”

## Academic community response
- The letter was organized by the Asian American Scholar Forum and the Federation of Asian Professor Associations.  
- Northwestern’s spokesperson declined to comment on the pending litigation and “vehemently denies the allegations.”  
- Margaret Flanagan, a physician‑scientist mentored by Wu, described her as resilient and unwavering.  
- Arnold Strauss, a retired pediatric cardiologist, contrasted Wu’s experience with two colleagues who received emotional, financial, and administrative support during their own China Initiative investigations.

## Context of the China Initiative
- Launched in 2018 to combat “economic espionage” and target foreign influence in U.S. research.  
- NIH report (December 2025) noted 691 scientists investigated; over 85 % had at least one serious compliance violation, but only six cases resulted in criminal or civil actions.  
- Investigations peaked in 2019 and have been declining since.

## Related concerns
- The case has heightened fear among Asian‑descent scholars about racial bias and institutional retaliation.  
- Related articles discuss the broader impact of the China Initiative, legal outcomes for other scientists, and ongoing debates about foreign‑influence scrutiny in U.S. academia.
