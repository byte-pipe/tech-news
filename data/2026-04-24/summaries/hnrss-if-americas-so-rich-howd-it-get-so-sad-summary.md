# If America's So Rich, How'd It Get So Sad?

## The Tragic Twenties
- Sam Peltzman’s 2026 study shows a sharp, unprecedented drop in U.S. self‑reported happiness after COVID, persisting through 2024—a “regime change” after five decades of stability.  
- Federal Reserve worker‑satisfaction data and the University of Michigan consumer‑sentiment index both hit historic lows, even lower than during the Great Recession or 1970s stagflation.  
- The World Happiness Report ranks the U.S. at its lowest ever, driven largely by a rapid decline among young people.  
- Traditional “hard” economic indicators (unemployment < 5 %, strong growth relative to Europe, rising incomes, expanding upper‑middle class) mask the deep‑seated malaise captured by “soft” survey data.  
- The author argues that feelings are factual; they shape politics, policy, and ultimately the economy, making the nation’s gloom a critical issue to understand.

## Who Killed the Vibes?
- Peltzman finds the happiness decline is broad‑based: a 10‑15‑point drop across almost every demographic, not limited to youth, low‑income, or unmarried groups.  
- Potential culprits that don’t fit the timing or pattern:
  - **Cultural shifts** (e.g., secularization) – a gradual 30‑year trend, not a 2020 shock.  
  - **Wage inequality** – low‑income wages have risen, and the biggest happiness drops appear among affluent groups (older, white, college‑educated).  
  - **Phones/social media** – long‑standing impact on youth, but the sudden 2020 plunge suggests a different primary driver.  
- The author concludes the pandemic itself, persisting as a cultural‑political force, is the most plausible explanation for the nationwide emotional collapse.

## The Permademic
- The COVID‑19 pandemic introduced a cascade of economic shocks—supply‑chain breakdowns, global inflation, soaring interest rates—that have not fully receded.  
- Inflation’s impact is felt in daily “holy‑shit” moments at the checkout, reflecting years of above‑average price growth rather than a smooth 12‑month average.  
- This sustained economic strain contributes heavily to the ongoing decline in American well‑being.
