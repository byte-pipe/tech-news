# AI‑powered robot beats elite table tennis players

## Overview
- Sony AI’s robot “Ace” played five matches under official table‑tennis rules, winning three and losing two.  
- The achievement is described as a milestone for robotics because table tennis demands extremely fast perception, decision‑making and precise motor control.  
- A research paper on Ace was published in *Nature* on the day of the matches.

## Technical approach
- **Hardware**: an eight‑joint arm mounted on a mobile base; stability is provided by the base rather than bipedal legs.  
- **Vision**: multiple cameras surround the court, tracking the ball’s position and spin by zooming in on the ball’s logo within milliseconds.  
- **Training**: 3,000 hours of simulated games taught the system how to handle spin and choose shots; serving techniques were modeled on expert human players.  
- **Key capability**: rapid response to altered trajectories, such as balls that catch on the net, and execution of a back‑spin shot previously thought impossible for a robot.

## Performance and player feedback
- Early versions struggled with slow, low‑spin balls, returning them weakly.  
- Elite players noted that complex‑spin serves forced Ace to return equally complex shots, while simple “knuckle” serves yielded easier returns.  
- Former Olympic player Kinjiro Nakamura said Ace’s early‑intercept back‑spin shot was something he had not believed a robot could perform.  
- Players found Ace’s lack of eyes or body language unsettling; the robot shows no emotion or intention, even in deuce situations.

## Significance and future outlook
- Professor Jan Peters called the project “truly impressive” but cautioned that table‑tennis research alone will not solve broader manipulation challenges in robotics.  
- He added that practical, consumer‑ready robots will still require substantial conventional engineering.  
- Dürr (project lead) suggested that a transformative robotics breakthrough could arrive within the next decade, potentially reshaping the world as dramatically as ChatGPT did in 2022.
