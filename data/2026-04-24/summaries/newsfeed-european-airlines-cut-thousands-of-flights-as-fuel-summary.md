# Summary of “European airlines cut thousands of flights as fuel costs soar”

- Jet fuel prices have roughly doubled since the start of the U.S.–Israeli war in Iran, pressuring European airlines that import about one‑third of their fuel from the Middle East.
- Lufthansa announced the cancellation of 20,000 flights through the fall to curb fuel expenses; other carriers such as KLM and Scandinavian Airlines have also reduced schedules, though on a smaller scale.
- Energy experts warn that a prolonged closure of the Strait of Hormuz could trigger higher fuel prices and possible shortages in Europe.

## Impact on Airlines
- Airlines are cutting short‑haul, often unprofitable, high‑frequency routes, especially within Europe, reducing travel options during the peak summer season.
- The reductions may expand as the conflict continues and fuel costs remain elevated, according to ING Research economist Rico Luman.
- U.S. carriers are passing fuel costs to passengers via higher fares and baggage fees but have not announced cuts comparable to European airlines.

## Fuel Supply Concerns
- The International Energy Agency’s Fatih Birol estimates Europe has roughly six weeks of jet‑fuel reserves left, prompting a shift to imports from the United States and Nigeria.
- The International Air Transport Association (IATA) urges authorities to develop coordinated plans for possible rationing if supply gaps widen.
