# ACLU, Amnesty and 120 Rights Groups Issue US World Cup Travel Advisory  

## Main Findings  
- The ACLU, Amnesty International and over 120 civil‑society organisations released a travel advisory for foreign visitors to the 2026 FIFA World Cup in the United States.  
- The advisory cites a “deteriorating human‑rights situation” in the US and the lack of concrete guarantees from FIFA, host cities, or the US government.  

## Context  
- The tournament will be held across Mexico, Canada and the United States, with an estimated 5‑10 million visitors expected in the US.  
- Concerns focus on President Donald Trump’s hard‑line immigration policies and their impact on tourists, fans, journalists and players.  

## Criticisms of FIFA and the US Government  
- ACLU human‑rights director Jamil Dakwar accuses FIFA of “paying lip service” to human rights while aligning with the Trump administration.  
- Amnesty’s Daniel Norona urges FIFA to secure binding assurances that foreign visitors will not be caught up in immigration enforcement.  
- White House spokesperson Davis Ingle and President Trump claim the event will be “safe and secure,” dismissing the advisory as “scare tactics.”  

## Specific Risks Highlighted  
1. **Arbitrary entry denial, arrest, detention or deportation** at ports of entry, including invasive social‑media screening.  
2. **Expanded travel restrictions** – 39 countries face broad US bans; Iran and Haiti have total visa bans; Ivory Coast, Senegal, Algeria, Cape Verde and Tunisia are subject to the “Visa Bond Program” ($15,000 bond).  
3. **Violent or unconstitutional immigration enforcement**, including racial profiling, drag‑net tactics, and recent shootings involving ICE agents.  
4. **Suppression of speech and increased surveillance**, especially targeting activists supporting Palestinian rights.  
5. **Harsh conditions in detention facilities**, with 32 deaths in ICE custody in 2025 and at least 14 reported deaths in 2026.  

## Recommendations for Attendees  
- Prepare an emergency contingency plan and have a “know‑your‑rights” briefing.  
- Secure electronic devices and consider downloading an app that can alert emergency contacts if detained.  
- Stay informed about visa requirements, possible bond obligations, and any changes to entry policies.  

## Ongoing Developments  
- ICE Acting Director Todd Lyons indicated the agency will play a “key part” in World Cup security.  
- DHS Secretary Markwayne Mullin said the department will work with FIFA to ensure safety.  
- Reports suggest a US envoy asked FIFA to replace Iran with Italy, though FIFA has not confirmed the request.  

The advisory warns that without guaranteed protections, the 2026 World Cup could fall short of FIFA’s promise of a safe, welcoming, and inclusive event for all participants and visitors.
