# Palantir Employees Question the Company’s Role in Government Actions

## Background and Rising Concerns
- Within months of President Trump’s second term, Palantir staff began doubting the firm’s commitment to civil liberties.  
- The company became a key technology provider for the Department of Homeland Security’s immigration enforcement, supplying tools to identify, track, and deport immigrants.  
- Former employees, reconnecting by phone, expressed alarm, asking “Are you tracking Palantir’s descent into fascism?” and noting a feeling that the work “feels wrong.”

## Company History and Mission
- Founded after 9/11 with early CIA venture capital, co‑founded by Peter Thiel.  
- Sells high‑powered data aggregation and analysis software to private firms, the U.S. military, and other governments.  
- Historically tolerated external criticism and internal debate about the moral implications of its work.

## Internal Tensions Escalate
- Employees started voicing concerns publicly inside Slack after the killing of Alex Pretti, an ICE‑protesting nurse, demanding transparency about ICE contracts.  
- Management responded by deleting Slack messages after seven days, citing leak prevention, which further angered staff.  
- An updated internal wiki defended the ICE partnership, claiming the technology “mitigates risks while enabling targeted outcomes.”

## Attempts at Dialogue
- Palantir held several AMA sessions with leadership (CTO Shyam Sankar, privacy & civil liberties team) to address questions.  
- One AMA, organized independently by team leads, highlighted internal friction; privacy lead Courtney Bowman was unaware of a colleague’s extensive work on the ICE contract.  
- Employees asked hard questions: could ICE agents delete audit logs? Could they create harmful workflows without Palantir’s help? What is the worst‑case misuse?

## Management’s Stance
- Privacy team admitted that preventing a “sufficiently malicious customer” is currently “basically impossible,” relying on post‑fact auditing and legal action.  
- CEO Alex Karp was described as prioritizing the ICE contract, with staff feeling they could only offer suggestions that were largely ignored.  
- Karp’s prerecorded interview with Bowman avoided direct discussion of ICE work and pushed employees toward signing NDAs for more details.

## External Events Amplify the Crisis
- A U.S. missile strike on an Iranian elementary school (Feb 28) killed over 120 children; investigations linked Palantir’s Maven system to the targeting data used in the attack.  
- The incident intensified employee unease, adding to concerns about the company’s involvement in lethal government actions.

## Company’s Public Response
- A spokesperson emphasized Palantir’s “culture of fierce internal dialogue” and denied being a monolith of belief, asserting that the firm hires talent to “defend America and its allies.”  

## Ongoing Call for Transparency
- Employees continue to demand clearer information about contracts with ICE and other government agencies, fearing that internal dissent is met with procedural redirection rather than substantive change.  
- The situation reflects an identity crisis within Palantir, as staff grapple with the gap between the company’s stated mission to prevent abuse and its perceived role in enabling it.
