# Artemis II is going so well that we're left to talk about frozen urine

## Overview
- Orion is now closer to the Moon than Earth on its 10‑day deep‑space mission, and overall performance is excellent.
- Daily briefings at Johnson Space Center have little substantive content, leading the conversation to focus on the spacecraft’s toilet.

## Initial toilet issue
- During early system checkout the toilet’s pump was not primed because insufficient water was added.
- Adding more water restored pump function; the incident generated a brief surge of online attention.

## New problem: frozen urine
- By Friday night, urine collected in a tank the size of an office trash can was found to be frozen, preventing venting to space.
- The toilet works for solid waste, but liquid waste (urine) could not be processed.
- Controllers rotated Orion to expose the tank and vent lines to maximum sunlight, partially thawing the urine; astronauts continue using bags for urine.

## NASA response and commentary
- Mission Management Team chair John Honeycutt acknowledged public fascination, calling it “human nature.”
- He emphasized that the issue is not a mission‑critical risk but would complicate longer‑duration missions.
- Honeycutt expressed a desire to have the system in the best possible state.

## Why functional space toilets matter
- Short trips to the Moon can tolerate makeshift solutions, but Mars missions will require reliable waste management for months.
- Orion’s test flight serves to validate life‑support systems, identify flaws, and implement fixes before longer missions.

## Overall mission status
- Deputy Orion program manager Debbie Korth described the spacecraft’s performance as “remarkably well” and “pleasantly surprised” the engineers.
- The frozen‑urine discussion is seen as a minor win given the many potential hazards of deep‑space travel.
