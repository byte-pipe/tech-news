# Apple at 50: Successes and Misses  

## Overview  
- Apple celebrated its 50th anniversary; founded by Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak in a garage.  
- About one‑third of the global population now owns an Apple device.  
- Success attributed to strong branding and marketing (“selling a dream”) as well as hardware.  
- Post‑Jobs era sees a focus on refining existing products; Tim Cook praised for keeping profitability, though some purists miss the earlier “Steve Jobs” excitement.  

## Hit products  

- **iPod (2001)**  
  - Not the first MP3 player, but its click‑wheel and iTunes integration transformed music consumption.  
  - Made digital music downloading mainstream and gave Apple the financial and operational base for later ventures like the iPhone.  

- **iPhone (2007)**  
  - Combined phone, iPod, and internet communicator in one device; sold >200 million units annually.  
  - Strong ecosystem lock‑in (“Hotel California of smartphones”).  
  - Success driven by sleek design, marketing that framed it as a romantic device, and continual innovation.  

- **Apple Watch (2015)**  
  - Generated roughly $15 bn in revenue, comparable to a top‑250 U.S. company.  
  - Pioneered health‑tech features (ECG, fall detection) and now outsells the entire traditional Swiss watch industry.  

## Missed products  

- **Apple Lisa (1983)**  
  - Early GUI PC with mouse, but priced near $10 000, too costly for business market.  
  - Demonstrated that being technologically ahead is insufficient without proper pricing and positioning.  

- **Butterfly keyboard (2015‑2019)**  
  - Thin‑profile keyboard mechanism introduced in MacBook Air and other laptops.  
  - Criticised for reduced durability and typing comfort; Apple reverted to a more reliable design in the 2019 16‑in MacBook Pro.  

- **Vision Pro headset (2023)**  
  - High‑price mixed‑reality device ($3 500) suffered from low demand and limited content.  
  - Production scaled back shortly after launch; Apple likely to be cautious about future smart‑glass ventures.  

## Analyst perspectives  

- Emma Wall (Hargreaves Lansdown): Apple’s market dominance stems from equal parts product and branding.  
- Ken Segall (former Jobs creative director): Tim Cook has adapted well, but many fans still associate “real” Apple with Steve Jobs.  
- Various analysts (Craig Pickerell, Ben Wood, Paolo Pescatore) highlighted how each product’s design, price, and ecosystem impact determined its success or failure.
