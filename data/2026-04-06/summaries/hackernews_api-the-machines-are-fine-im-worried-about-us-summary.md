## Scenario
- New assistant professor hires two PhD students, Alice and Bob, each given a solvable, year‑long project in astrophysics.
- Alice works through papers, code, and concepts herself; Bob relies heavily on an AI agent for summarizing papers, explaining methods, debugging code, and even writing the manuscript.
- Both produce solid, publishable papers with similar quantitative metrics.

## Institutional Incentives
- Universities, funding agencies, and hiring committees evaluate scientists primarily by countable outputs (papers, citations).
- From an institutional perspective, the internal learning of a student is irrelevant; the produced paper is the valuable commodity.
- Most PhD graduates leave academia, so the long‑term development of independent thinkers is not a priority for departments.

## Value of Training vs. Output
- David Hogg argues that graduate students should be hired as ends (for their intellectual growth), not merely as means to produce results.
- In astrophysics, the specific scientific results have limited practical impact; the true value lies in training researchers who can tackle hard problems.
- If AI performs the work, the essential learning process is lost, meaning science does not truly accelerate.

## AI’s Effect on the Student Experience
- Alice gains a durable, portable mental model: she can read new papers, write code, and spot errors independently.
- Bob, without the AI, would retain little knowledge; his year’s work was externalized to the tool, leaving him unprepared to continue without it.

## Reflections on Recent Discussions
- Several recent essays explore AI in academia:
  - David Hogg’s white paper warns against both full adoption and outright bans of LLMs.
  - Natalie Hogg describes her shift from skeptic to daily user, noting context‑dependent principles.
  - Matthew Schwartz’s experiment shows Claude can draft a publishable physics paper in weeks, suggesting LLM performance comparable to a second‑year graduate student.

## Schwartz’s Experiment Re‑examined
- The key insight is that the supervision, not the LLM, provides the physics expertise.
- Claude follows detailed prompts; the human supervisor supplies the conceptual guidance that makes the output scientifically valid.

## Conclusion
- Quantitative metrics mask a deeper issue: the loss of genuine intellectual development when AI does the heavy lifting.
- The current academic system, designed to count outputs, inadvertently treats students as interchangeable “machines,” risking the erosion of the very human capacity that drives scientific progress.
