# The Fastest Way to Board an Airplane | Navendu Pottekkat - The Open Source Absolutist

This article discusses the inefficiency of traditional airline boarding methods, particularly the back-to-front approach, and proposes a significantly faster alternative. The author argues that the current system leads to bottlenecks and congestion in the aisles as passengers struggle to stow luggage in the overhead bins.

## Key Points:

* **Inefficiency of Back-to-Front Boarding:** The article demonstrates through simulations that back-to-front boarding is slow because queues form behind individuals, blocking the aisle and causing congestion.
* **Random Boarding is Faster:** Surprisingly, boarding passengers at random times is consistently faster than the standard back-to-front method because passengers are naturally spaced out and can stow bags simultaneously.
* **Window-Middle-Aisle (WilMA) Method:** This method, which groups boarding by seat column (windows first, then middle, then aisle), is slightly faster than random boarding and avoids awkward seat shuffling.
* **Jason Steffen's Optimal Method:** A theoretical method proposed by Jason Steffen involves passengers boarding in a strict back-to-front order, alternating rows and sides, allowing for parallel bag stowing.
* **Practical Variant:** A practical adaptation of Steffen's method uses four boarding groups, alternating sides and rows, which is more feasible for real-world implementation and accommodates families.
* **Human Factors:** The article acknowledges that real-world boarding involves various factors like ticket classes, loyalty programs, and passenger demographics, which influence boarding priority.
* **Simulation Evidence:** The author conducted simulations to compare the step counts of different boarding methods, consistently showing faster times with random and grouped approaches.
* **Further Exploration:** The article recommends exploring the original research by CGP Grey and Jason Steffen for a deeper understanding of the topic.

## Proposed Fastest Method:

The article advocates for a three-group system (window, middle, aisle) or a four-group system (alternating sides and rows) for boarding, which mimics the efficiency of random boarding while providing some structure and practicality.
