# Do not apologize for replying late to my email

This article argues against apologizing for delayed email responses, a trend the author finds prevalent due to instant messaging culture. The author emphasizes that asynchronous communication inherently allows for individual rhythms and that they rarely expect immediate replies.

## Key Points
- **No need to apologize:** The author strongly advises against apologizing for taking time to reply to emails, unless a specific timeframe was communicated.
- **Asynchronous nature of email:** Email is designed for asynchronous communication, meaning there's no expectation of immediate responses. The author often forgets about emails once sent.
- **Context is often unnecessary:** The author doesn't need detailed explanations for delays, even for trivial emails. Life happens, and a reply is a bonus, not an obligation.
- **Offer a delay option:** If someone wants to reply but lacks time, they should suggest a later timeframe rather than apologizing for the delay.
- **No obligation to reply:** Recipients should feel free to read, discard, or think about emails without feeling pressured to respond.
- **Avoid "I'll reply later":** This is often a non-reply in itself, creating cognitive load. A simple "thanks" or "received" is sufficient for acknowledgment.
- **Remind of context:** If replying after a delay, it's helpful to include the original email thread for context, as the author's priorities may have shifted.
- **Don't expect a reply:** The author explicitly states they don't expect replies and have a life beyond email.
- **Use plaintext:** The author prefers plaintext emails.
