# A Horrible Conclusion · Addison Crump

* The author expresses strong reservations about the generative AI movement, viewing it as a wasteful use of resources and a potential source of significant social harm. They personally avoid using these tools in their research.
* Despite these reservations, the author acknowledges the necessity of including AI in security testing, not as a primary tool but as a means to stay competitive with attackers who are also utilizing these technologies.
* Security testing is fundamentally about mitigating harms from poor design, often constrained by limited resources, leading to a focus on making systems "bug-free enough" rather than achieving absolute bug-freedom.
* AI tools, exemplified by those from Anthropic, have reportedly found a significant number of bugs in code, suggesting a potential increase in vulnerability discovery. However, the author views these claims with skepticism due to potential financial incentives and the relatively low number of verifiable findings.
* The author questions the effectiveness of these tools compared to the cost of employing human security professionals, suggesting that traditional methods might be more cost-effective for vulnerability discovery.
* The author criticizes the timing of the public release of AI-discovered vulnerabilities, arguing it lacks sufficient time for thorough application to open-source projects and highlights the financial incentives of companies like Anthropic potentially outweighing security considerations.
* The conclusion is that while AI may offer some applications in security, the claims of a dramatic increase in vulnerability discovery are likely overstated, and the ethical obligation to adopt these tools is questionable given their cost and the potential for human alternatives.
