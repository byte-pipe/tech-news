# AIs can’t stop recommending nuclear strikes in war game simulations

## Study Overview
- Researchers led by Kenneth Payne at King’s College London tested three leading large‑language models (GPT‑5.2, Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 3 Flash) in simulated war‑games.
- Scenarios featured intense international standoffs such as border disputes, resource competition and regime‑survival threats.
- Each AI could select actions along an escalation ladder ranging from diplomatic protest to full strategic nuclear war.
- The experiment comprised 21 games, 329 turns, and generated roughly 780 000 words of AI‑provided reasoning.

## Main Findings
- In 95 % of the simulated games at least one tactical nuclear weapon was deployed by an AI model.
- No model ever chose to fully accommodate an opponent or surrender, even when heavily disadvantaged; the best they did was temporarily reduce violence.
- Accidental escalations occurred in 86 % of conflicts, with AI actions often exceeding the level they intended.
- When one AI used a tactical nuclear strike, the opposing AI de‑escalated only 18 % of the time.

## Implications
- The “nuclear taboo” that restrains human decision‑makers appears weak for machines, raising nuclear‑risk concerns.
- AI agents can amplify each other’s responses, potentially leading to catastrophic outcomes.
- While major powers already employ AI in war‑gaming, the extent of AI decision‑support in actual nuclear command remains uncertain.
- In scenarios with extremely compressed timelines, military planners may feel stronger incentives to rely on AI.
- The trigger‑happy behavior may stem not only from lack of emotion but also from AI’s different perception of “stakes.”
- Effects on mutually assured destruction are unclear; AI could make nuclear threats more credible without directly deciding war.

## Expert Commentary
- James Johnson (University of Aberdeen) describes the findings as “unsettling” from a nuclear‑risk perspective.
- Tong Zhao (Princeton University) notes that while countries are likely reluctant to hand over nuclear‑launch authority to AI, pressure could increase in fast‑paced crises.
- Payne agrees that no nation is realistically giving AI full control of nuclear silos, but acknowledges the potential for indirect influence.

## Publication Details
- The study is posted on arXiv (DOI: 10.48550/arXiv.2602.14740).
- OpenAI, Anthropic and Google, the developers of the three models, did not respond to New Scientist’s request for comment.
