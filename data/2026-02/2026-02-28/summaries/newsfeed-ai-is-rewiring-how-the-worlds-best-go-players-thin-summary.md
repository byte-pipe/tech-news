# AI is rewiring how the world’s best Go players think

## Overview
- The Korea Baduk Association’s headquarters in Seoul has shifted from traditional stone‑bowls to computer screens where professionals analyze games with AI.
- Since AlphaGo’s 2016 victory over Lee Sedol, AI has transformed Go: overturning long‑standing principles, introducing new moves, and making AI assistance essential for professional competition.
- The change sparks debate: some claim AI erodes creativity, while others note increased accessibility and a rise in female players.

## Professional training with AI
- **Shin Jin‑seo**, world No. 1, treats KataGo as a daily “training partner,” following its suggested “blue spot” moves and reflecting on the AI’s reasoning.
- Shin’s games match AI recommendations 37.5 % of the time, well above the 28.5 % average for Korean pros (2022 Korean Baduk League study).
- He likens the routine to an ascetic practice and believes he could defeat AlphaGo by exploiting its known weaknesses.

## Evolution of Go‑playing AI
- **AlphaGo** learned from 30 million human moves and self‑play; its successor **AlphaGo Zero** started from scratch, mastering Go by self‑play alone and beating AlphaGo Lee 100‑0 after three days of training.
- Open‑source descendants emerged; **KataGo** now dominates Korean professional use, offering faster analysis, whole‑board evaluation, and score‑maximizing predictions.
- KataGo’s holistic reading of the board yields superior long‑term strategy compared with earlier, more localized AI approaches.

## Impact on game theory and style
- Traditional heuristics and opening conventions have been replaced by AI‑derived, highly efficient openings; the first 50 moves of many modern games mirror AI suggestions.
- The creative “opening canvas” has shrunk; the middle game now emphasizes raw calculation over personal expression.
- Homogenization of style is evident: over a third of top‑player moves align with AI recommendations (2023 study), and fans find repetitive openings tiring (Ke Jie, 2021).

## Cultural and personal reactions
- **Lee Sedol** describes the shift from art to “answer‑key copying,” feeling his purpose in Go has vanished.
- Some players, like **Kim Chae‑young**, had to discard years of intuition, confronting AI’s opaque probability displays and “higher‑dimensional” thinking.
- Despite uncertainty, AI democratizes training resources, helping more women ascend the ranks.

## Future outlook
- The Korea Baduk Association seeks a commemorative match between Shin and AlphaGo; DeepMind has not confirmed details.
- Professionals continue to grapple with AI’s mysterious logic while exploring ways to integrate human creativity with machine precision.
