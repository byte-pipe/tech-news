# Why I’m not worried about AI job loss

## Summary of David Oks' Essay

*   The essay discusses the recent surge in public anxiety surrounding AI's potential impact on jobs, drawing parallels to the initial stages of the COVID-19 pandemic in February 2020.
*   Oks criticizes an essay by Matt Shumer, which he believes exaggerates the imminent threat of mass job displacement due to AI and offers simplistic solutions like subscribing to AI products. He argues Shumer's essay, while widely read, is fundamentally flawed in its assessment of AI's impact.
*   Oks contends that the current situation is not comparable to the pre-COVID-19 period and that ordinary people have little to genuinely worry about regarding AI and job loss. He believes the doomsday forecasts are not realistic.
*   He acknowledges AI's significant future importance, comparing it to the discovery of electricity or the steam engine, but argues that this doesn't equate to a sudden, catastrophic shift in human labor.
*   Oks emphasizes that labor substitution is about comparative advantage, not absolute advantage. The combination of human skills and AI is often superior to AI alone, leading to increased productivity.
*   He predicts that human complementarity with AI will persist longer than many currently believe because the world is run by humans, who are inherently inefficient and create bottlenecks. These bottlenecks will continue to require human intervention.

## Key Points

*   The current panic around AI job loss is being fueled by exaggerated fears and a misunderstanding of how AI will integrate with human labor.
*   AI will likely augment human capabilities rather than completely replace them, leading to a "cyborg" era where human skills remain valuable.
*   Human inefficiencies and bottlenecks will continue to necessitate human involvement in various processes, ensuring ongoing complementarity between humans and AI.
*   The advancements in AI will be gradual and uneven, unlike the sudden, transformative shifts some currently predict.
*   The focus should not be on fearing AI but on addressing the inherent inefficiencies within human-driven systems that AI can help mitigate.
