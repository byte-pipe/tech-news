## Consumers will accept ads in AI chatbots but trust issues remain

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Voice, chatbots

Brand trust

A large majority of consumers will continue to use AI chatbots that introduce ads in exchange for getting the information they want for ‘free’, according to a new poll, but trust remains an area of concern.

Research and advisory firm Forrester polled its ConsumerVoices panel – 409 online adults in the US, UK and Canada – to test opinion, with 83% of respondents saying they’d accept ads for access to free tiers.

###### Why AI ads matter

There are still many unknowns about what kind of ads people will see as part of a chatbot query, but it’s clear that advertising will be needed to help pay for some of the enormous costs involved in developing the technology.

OpenAI, for example, is betting that ads in ChatGPT will help pay for theroughly $1.4tr it’s expected to payin computing costs. According to reports, the company projects that about 8.5% of its user base, or about 220 million people, will pay for a premium tier that avoids ads.

Anthropic, on the other hand, has doubled down on its promise not to introduce ads because it’s chasing enterprise customers. These are two very different approaches to monetizing the technology.

It’s yet unclear how this will shake out in time, but expect the experience to change. Advertisers will need to think carefully about their visibility on these platforms.

###### What’s happening

* OpenAI announced plans tointroduce ads to ChatGPT, one of the most popular chatbots, in January despite earlier promises from CEO Sam Altman that the company would not do so.
* Microsoft has some supported ads for Copilot; Google began testing ads in AI Mode last year; Perplexity’sads business appears to be in flux. Anthropic, meanwhile, has said it will befocusing on enterprise customers.
* Forrester said respondents to its poll were “generally sensitive to ads blurring the line between helpful information and paid promotion”.
* 6% of those polled said they would switch to a paid tier rather than see ads.

###### In context: OpenAI, Anthropic and search

OpenAI’s about-turn on introducing ads has not done the company many favours – it will need to convince users that its ads are not skewing ChatGPT’s answers, for instance, when people already view Big Tech with deep suspicion.

A Gallup poll – cited by Nate Silver’srecent blog on AI– found that the percentage of people who have “a great deal” or a “large amount” of trust in large technology companies fell from 32% to 24% between 2020 and last year.

OpenAI’sSuper Bowl adwas meant to address trust concerns but most of the focus has been on rival Anthropic poking fun at OpenAI’s ad ambitions with its own ads. (Yes, the whole thing is weirdly circular and somewhat solipsistic.)

There is a lot at stake, however, because LLM search – where chatbots provide conversational answers to user questions – is evolving quickly, potentially disrupting an advertising ecosystem that has relied heavily on SEO.

Just recently, Future – the owner of comparison site Go Compare –saw its stock price fallbecause investors are worried that you can now find insurance quotes inside ChatGPT.

In itsFY 2025 results, Future said that 67% of its audience was coming through Google search in 2019. Today, it’s just 27%. It also added that AI overviews now reach 50% of its key terms on SEO.

As marketers well know, you go where the audiences are – and the AI companies such as OpenAI are betting that the price to play in these spaces will be high.
