# Poor Demiing never stood a chance – Surfing Complexity

* The article discusses the contrasting influences of Peter Drucker and W. Edwards Deming on management, noting Drucker's greater impact in the U.S. compared to Deming's prominence in Japan.
* Drucker's approach, exemplified by OKRs, simplifies complex organizational issues by focusing managers on a few key results, reducing the bandwidth needed for understanding the system.
* Deming advocated for a fundamentally different approach, emphasizing systemic change and understanding the underlying processes rather than relying on numerical targets. He viewed numerical goals as a "deadly disease."
* Drucker's method is likened to classical control, setting a desired outcome and letting the system achieve it. Deming's approach, rooted in statistical process control, involves observing system variability to identify areas for improvement.
* Drucker's framework is easier for managers to implement, providing a bounded amount of necessary information. Deming's requires a continuous, potentially endless research program with no guaranteed limits to the relevant information.
* The author concludes that Drucker's ideas were more "sticky" in the U.S. because they make the "right thing" (simplifying and focusing) easier for managers to do, while Deming's approach, though valuable, requires more effort and commitment.
