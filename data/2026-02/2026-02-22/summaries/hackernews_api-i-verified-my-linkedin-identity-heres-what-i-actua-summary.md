# I Verified My LinkedIn Identity. Here's What I Actually Handed Over.

This article details the data shared with Persona Identities, Inc. when verifying a LinkedIn profile, revealing a significant amount of personal information is disclosed to a third-party company rather than LinkedIn itself. The author's experience of verifying their LinkedIn identity by scanning their passport and taking a selfie triggered a deeper investigation into Persona's privacy policy and data handling practices.

## Data Collected by Persona Identities

For a three-minute identity verification, Persona collected a wide array of personal data, including:

*   Full name, passport photo (both sides), selfie, facial geometry (biometric data), NFC chip data from the passport, national ID number, nationality, sex, birthdate, age, email, phone number, postal address, IP address, device information, geolocation.
*   Behavioral biometrics tracking hesitation and copy-paste detection during the verification process.

## Third-Party Data Sources

Persona doesn't solely rely on the information provided; it cross-references data with a "global network of trusted third-party data sources," which includes:

*   Government databases, national ID registries, consumer credit agencies, utility companies, mobile network providers, and postal address databases.

## Data Usage and Training

The uploaded passport image and selfie are used to train Persona's AI to recognize identity documents across different countries and to improve the service. This is based on the "legitimate interest" of improving the service, not explicit consent.

## Data Sharing with LinkedIn and Others

Upon verification, Persona shares the following data with LinkedIn:

*   Full name, birth year, government ID type and issuing authority, verification result, and a blurred version of the ID (name and portrait visible).

Access to the data is also granted to:

*   Persona's service providers, data partners, affiliates, subsidiaries, potential acquirers, and law enforcement.

## Extensive Network of Subprocessors

Persona utilizes a network of 17 subprocessor companies, primarily located in the United States, for various functions such as data extraction, analysis, infrastructure, document analysis, database services, and analytics. Notably, there are no subprocessors based in the EU.

## The CLOUD Act and Data Location

Despite Persona having data centers in the US and Germany, it is subject to the US CLOUD Act. This law allows US law enforcement to compel US-based companies like Persona to disclose data stored anywhere in the world.

## Data Privacy Framework

Persona claims compliance with the EU-US Data Privacy Framework (DPF), but the author highlights that this framework does not prevent US law enforcement from accessing data.

## Conclusion

The author's attempt to obtain a LinkedIn verification badge resulted in the disclosure of a substantial amount of personal data to a third-party company with a broad network of data sources and subprocessors. This process also involves the potential for US law enforcement access to data, even if it is stored outside the United States.
