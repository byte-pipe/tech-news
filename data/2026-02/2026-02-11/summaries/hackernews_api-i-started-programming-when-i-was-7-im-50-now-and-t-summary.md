# I Started Programming When I Was 7. I'm 50 Now, and the Thing I Loved Has Changed | James Randall

This article reflects on the author's 42-year journey in programming and how the essence of the craft has shifted with technological advancements, particularly the rise of Artificial Intelligence (AI). The author contrasts the early days of computing, where understanding the underlying hardware was crucial and fostered creativity, with the current era dominated by abstraction and AI-driven code generation.

## The Era That Made Me
The author fondly remembers the 8-bit to 486DX2-66 era as a time of characterful machines like the Sinclair Spectrum, Commodore 64, and NES. These machines demanded a deep understanding of their inner workings, requiring developers to be akin to systems engineers to achieve desired results. The software landscape was also unique, with small teams pushing technical boundaries due to fewer established rules.

## The Shifts I Rode
Over four decades, the author has navigated numerous technological transitions, from CLI to GUI, desktop to web, and monoliths to microservices. While new technologies emerged, the core skill of understanding system behavior and how things work remained constant. Experienced developers adapted by applying their foundational knowledge to new platforms.

## This Time Is Different
The author argues that the advent of AI represents a fundamentally different technological shift. Previous changes involved learning new tools while applying existing skills. AI, however, impacts the very nature of being a skilled developer. The "elegant solution" and the satisfaction of problem-solving are increasingly handled by AI models, leading to a feeling that the core craft is being hollowed out. The feedback loop has changed, and the intimate connection with the technology has diminished.

## The Abstraction Tower
The author uses the metaphor of an abstraction tower to illustrate how developers have progressively moved up layers of abstraction over the decades. AI is the layer that has made it difficult to maintain a comprehensive understanding of the entire stack. The author laments the loss of the experience of understanding the whole machine, a feeling that those who haven't had that experience cannot fully grasp.

## What Remains
Despite the changes, the author believes that core skills like systems thinking, architectural judgment, and the ability to make crucial decisions remain valuable and are not easily replaceable by AI. The craft endures, but in a different form, requiring a deeper understanding of the big picture and the ability to guide and correct AI-generated code.
