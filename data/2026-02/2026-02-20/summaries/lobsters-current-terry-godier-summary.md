# Current | Terry Godier

# Current: An RSS Reader

## The Problem with Traditional RSS Readers

Current was created to address the feeling that existing RSS readers present reading the internet as a task and that falling behind is a failure. The author felt that these readers made users feel obligated to consume content. This feeling was traced back to Brent Simmons' NetNewsWire, a design choice that became a convention. The author coined this feeling "phantom obligation"—the guilt of doing something no one asked you to do.

## The River Interface

Current presents feeds as a "river" of content, rather than a list to be processed. This design emphasizes the natural flow of information – it arrives, lingers, and then fades away. There is no "unread" count because the problem was the focus on tasks. Each article has a velocity, indicating how quickly it ages. As articles age, they dim and eventually disappear, mirroring the natural lifespan of information.

## Velocity and Half-Life

Velocity determines how long articles remain visible. Users set a half-life for each source, ranging from three hours for breaking news to a week for slower-paced content. This prevents prolific sources from overwhelming the feed. The onboarding process guides users to select a velocity speed (Breaking, News, Article, Essay, Evergreen) for each source.

## Release Instead of "Mark as Read"

Current encourages users to "release" articles instead of marking them as read. This is done with a swipe gesture, allowing users to quickly discard content. Articles can also be released from within the article itself. This approach avoids the feeling of processing and handling information, promoting a more natural flow.

## Tuning Your River

Current allows for granular control over how each source appears in the river. Users can fetch full articles, mark sources as webcomics, mute them, or pin them. These options are easily accessible and adapt the reader to the content of each source.

## The River Speaks: Contextual Notifications

Current observes user habits to provide helpful, non-intrusive notifications. These notifications alert users to heavy posting from a source, skipped articles, or opportunities to pin frequently read sources. This is designed to be like a librarian subtly organizing shelves, not algorithmic recommendations for engagement. All processing happens on the device, ensuring user privacy.

## Voices: Focusing on Individual Creators

A key feature of Current is the "Voices" tab, which groups feeds from individual creators together in a timeline. This emphasizes the difference between news organizations and personal blogs, fostering a more personal connection with the content. Active voices appear in full color, while quieter ones fade to grayscale. Users can tap on a voice to filter the timeline to show only their posts.
