# Why Do I Keep Killing My Side Projects? - DEV Community

# Summary of “Builder identity versus product success”

## Background
- The author shut down two personal projects: **Podiscover** (a podcast discovery social platform) and **Bloudme** (an old‑school RSS reader).
- Both started with genuine interest but ended in abandonment.
- The piece is an attempt to uncover the recurring pattern behind these failures rather than a typical “lessons learned” article.

## Identified Pattern
- Sequence observed in both projects:  
  - Genuine need → Technical excitement → Solitude → Doubt → Death.  
- The gap between “I built it” and “someone cares” was too wide, leading to loss of motivation.

## Specific Modes of Failure
- **Confusing technical curiosity with product belief** – learning a new framework (e.g., Hotwire) drove the start, not a solid market need.  
- **Solo development kills motivation** – being an introvert, the author worked alone without co‑founders, early users, or feedback, turning silence into a decision to quit.  
- **Skipping validation** – coding began without any committed users, falling into the “developer trap” of preferring code over conversation.  
- **Costs become symbolic** – even modest expenses combined with zero usage amplified doubts, though cost itself was not the core issue.

## Uncertainties
- Unclear whether the pattern reflects a lack of perseverance or an ability to spot dead ends early.  
- Uncertain if the desire is to build products or simply to hold the identity of a product builder.  
- Recognizes that the latter requires only a repository and a launch tweet, while the former demands user interaction and tolerance for plateaus.

## Planned Changes for Future Side Projects
- **Start with a person, not a repository** – secure a real user willing to give feedback before writing code.  
- **Separate learning from building** – use experimental projects for tech exploration; choose familiar, “boring” tech for products aimed at users.  
- **Set a cancellation criterion upfront** – e.g., “If I don’t have 10 active users by week 8, I stop.”  
- **Find a accountability partner** – any regular check‑in from someone who cares can sustain motivation.

## Conclusion
- Repeating the same failure pattern (solo work, no users, waning motivation) signals a systemic issue rather than bad luck.  
- The author optimized the enjoyable part (building) while neglecting the crucial part (connecting with users).  
- Recognizing this may be the first step toward change, or it could remain complacent self‑awareness.
