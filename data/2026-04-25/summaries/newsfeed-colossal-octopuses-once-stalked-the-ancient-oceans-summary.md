# Colossal octopuses once stalked the ancient oceans

## Discovery and research context
- Researchers reported in *Science* the first evidence of gigantic octopuses living in the late Cretaceous, about 100 million years ago.  
- The study was led by paleontologist Yasuhiro Iba (Hokkaō University) and involved Jörg Mutterlose (Ruhr University Bochum) and others.  
- The fossils were found in large concretions from northern Japan, a type of rock that forms on the seafloor and can trap soft‑tissue remains.

## Innovative fossil‑mining technique
- The team used a “digital fossil‑mining” approach: concretions were sliced thinly, photographed, and reconstructed in 3 D with the aid of an AI model.  
- This method allowed detection of internal structures that are invisible from the exterior, overcoming the usual scarcity of octopus fossils because their bodies are mostly soft tissue.

## Fossil evidence: octopus jaws
- Inside the concretions were well‑preserved octopus beaks (hard lower and upper jaws) – the only parts of an octopus that readily fossilize.  
- The lower jaws were the largest ever recorded for any octopus, providing a basis for size estimation.

## Size and ecological role
- Calculations based on jaw dimensions suggest the extinct species reached up to 60 feet (≈ 18 m) in total length, larger than a school bus and surpassing the modern giant Pacific octopus.  
- Wear patterns on the jaws indicate a diet of hard‑shelled prey such as shrimp, lobsters, bivalves, and nautilus‑like animals.  
- Asymmetrical wear (right side more worn) hints at lateralized feeding behavior and a relatively advanced brain.  
- The authors propose these octopuses were active carnivores that could have competed with other apex predators of the time, though direct evidence of hunting larger animals remains speculative.

## Evolutionary significance
- The findings push back the emergence of traits associated with modern octopuses—intelligence, flexible predation, and complex feeding mechanics—to the Cretaceous period.  
- The rarity of octopus fossils has limited understanding of their evolutionary history; these specimens provide a rare window into early cephalopod development.

## Late Cretaceous ocean ecosystem
- The newly described octopuses shared the seas with giant mosasaurs, large sharks, and other formidable marine reptiles, contributing to a “majestic” and predator‑rich environment.  
- Their presence adds a new dimension to reconstructions of ancient marine food webs and biodiversity.
