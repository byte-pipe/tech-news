# MacBook Neo and How the iPad Could Be — by Craig Mod

# Summary of “MacBook Neo and How the iPad Could Be”

## Vision for the iPad
- The iPad should be a pure touch‑first device: no keyboards, mice, trackpads, or windowed modes.  
- Each app ought to occupy the full screen, creating a “finger ballet” experience that feels bouncy, precise, and distinct from desktop OSes.  
- Apps should be unconventional and uniquely suited to multitouch, rather than scaled‑up versions of iPhone or macOS software.

## Why iPadOS falls short
- Hardware (starting with the 2015 iPad Pro) is powerful, fanless, and has excellent displays, but the OS restricts professional workflows.  
- Core creative tools (e.g., Lightroom, Adobe apps) lack essential features such as easy duplication, leading to workflow “paper cuts.”  
- Even strong iPad‑centric apps like Procreate remain niche; most serious tasks (email, coding, heavy editing) feel better on a MacBook.  
- Recent AI tools and LLM‑driven workflows run more smoothly on macOS because the platform is more flexible and “adult‑friendly.”

## The MacBook’s resurgence
- The 2020 M1 MacBook Pro liberated Apple laptops from Intel’s limitations, delivering silent, high‑performance silicon.  
- Although early models had only USB‑C ports, the ecosystem quickly added useful ports, solidifying the MacBook as the go‑to “Pro” machine.  
- The M1 era shifted user sentiment: iPads moved from potential to peripheral, while macOS became the primary environment for serious work.

## Apple’s misguided convergence
- Instead of sharpening iPadOS as a touch‑only wonderland, Apple added half‑baked macOS‑like multitasking and windowing, creating a diluted experience.  
- Recent macOS updates (Ventura, “Tahoe”) have increased lock‑downs and pop‑up friction, while rumors suggest a push to merge iPadOS (or visionOS) features into macOS.  
- The author argues this strategy is driven by people who don’t use the devices daily, leading to a clash of fundamentally different interaction paradigms.

## The MacBook Neo experience
- The author has been using a MacBook Neo daily for two weeks and finds it “outstanding”: cheaper than an iPad with a keyboard, more capable, and fluid.  
- It offers a solid keyboard, good screen, reliable processor, and an OS that integrates well with services like Dropbox and LLM tools.  
- It fulfills the author’s wish for a compact, lightweight writing machine that syncs reliably, avoiding iCloud Drive’s past sync failures.

## Outlook on touch screens
- Rumors of touchscreens on future MacBooks are met with disinterest; the author prefers a keyboard‑first experience and does not want to touch the MacBook screen.
