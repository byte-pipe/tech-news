# Cleveland's open data overhaul: From sticky notes to public dashboards

## Background & Leadership
- Elizabeth Crowe serves as Cleveland’s chief innovation and technology officer (CITO) and leads the open‑data initiative.  
- Prior to her interim CITO role (Feb 2026), she founded the Office of Urban Analytics and Innovation (Urban AI) in Aug 2022.  
- Mayor Justin Bibb issued an executive order in Dec 2023 establishing an Open Data Policy and governance board, giving Crowe formal authority to modernize data practices.

## Challenges Faced
- Data was scattered across 130 enterprise systems, local machines, Excel files, and even sticky notes.  
- The city’s IT environment lagged: no electronic calendar until 2014, police department email only in 2018, and overall “Luddite” culture.  

## Strategy & Governance
- The Open Data Policy (Dec 2023) defines data as a strategic asset to improve resource allocation, service delivery, and transparency.  
- All departments must adopt a framework covering:  
  - Data inventory  
  - Data standards  
  - Data use and infrastructure  
  - Open‑data publishing  
  - Governance board oversight  

## Implementation Steps
- **Data inventory:** Mapped existing data sources, exposing hidden repositories (e.g., servers under desks).  
- **Tech‑stack selection:** Chose Microsoft Azure Commercial Cloud, Power BI for visualization, and Esri for GIS mapping, creating a greenfield environment without legacy analytics warehouses.  
- **Team development:** Assessed skills of a 16‑person core team, identified gaps, and recruited a “bench” of analysts citywide.  
- **Departmental data leads:** Over 30 departments appointed data leads (often with analytics degrees) for monthly training and professional development.  
- **Data classification:** Implemented a four‑level system:  
  1. Public data  
  2. Operational information (e.g., help‑desk tickets)  
  3. Compliance data (HIPAA, FERPA)  
  4. Restricted/confidential data  

## Outcomes & Public Tools
- Open‑data portal launched in April 2024; now celebrating its two‑year anniversary.  
- Public dashboards include:  
  - **Cleveland Cemetery Viewer** – lets residents locate burial plots.  
  - **311 Service Request Dashboard** – displays city service request status.  
  - **Property Insights Tool (in development)** – will merge data from 15 city systems into an interactive web map for property ownership, transfers, and sales.  
- Numerous additional public tools have been built and released, expanding transparency across city services.
