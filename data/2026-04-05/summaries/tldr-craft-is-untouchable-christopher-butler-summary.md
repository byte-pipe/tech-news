# Craft is Untouchable – Christopher Butler

## What Craft Actually Is
- Commonly defined as “skill in making things by hand,” but the “by hand” clause is not essential.
- Historical examples (Mozart composing in his head, Beethoven composing while deaf) show craft can exist without physical contact.
- “Craft” is used both as a noun for expertise and as a verb for the disciplined practice of that expertise.
- Early encounters with AI challenge our assumptions about craft, yet they also reinforce craft as an elevated, abstract form of creation.

## The Tool Doesn’t Determine the Craft
- AI should be seen as a tool that transforms ideas into artifacts; the strength of the idea matters more than the tool.
- The synthesizer analogy: it changed the means of making music but did not eliminate musical craft.
- Core design principles—visual hierarchy, negative space, rhythm—remain unchanged when AI generates the output.
- Craft migrates to a higher level of abstraction rather than disappearing.

## Practice Still Builds Skill
- Repetition remains the foundation of skill; “make many things” still holds true.
- Using AI repeatedly improves output, not merely by better prompting but by reshaping the underlying creative process.
- Faster iteration, tighter feedback loops, and more rapid learning enhance judgment.
- The focus shifts from low‑level details (e.g., perfect CSS) to higher‑order decisions (structure, hierarchy, communication), while the discipline itself stays the same.

## The Temptation to Skip the Work
- AI’s speed can produce flashy, seemingly finished products that lack thoughtful craft, leading to a gap between appearance and quality.
- Designers risk becoming “wet blankets” if they do not intervene, allowing inferior designs to ship unnoticed by most users.
- The ease of generating professional‑looking outputs encourages people to skip the iterative, judgment‑building work that defines true craft.
- Example: a video where a designer prompts Claude Code to create a marketing page in minutes, then merely restyles it—raising the question of whether genuine design work occurred.
- While designers are not immune to this seduction, the broader danger is that low‑quality, AI‑generated work could become the norm, eroding the standards of thoughtful design.
