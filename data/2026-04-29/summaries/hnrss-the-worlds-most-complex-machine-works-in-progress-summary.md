# The world’s most complex machine – Works in Progress Magazine

## Overview
- Modern smartphones contain billions of transistors, far surpassing the computing power of early room‑size computers.  
- This miniaturisation is driven by ever‑smaller transistor dimensions, which depend on advanced lithography techniques.  
- Advanced semiconductors have become a critical technology and a geopolitical flashpoint, especially between the US and China.  
- All semiconductor manufacturers rely on a single supplier for the most advanced lithography equipment: **ASML** of the Netherlands.

## ASML’s Role and Scale
- ASML produces the only machines capable of patterning chips at the extreme ultraviolet (EUV) wavelength required for today’s leading process nodes.  
- An EUV scanner is roughly the size of a double‑decker bus; shipping one needs 40 freight containers, three cargo planes, and 20 trucks.  
- Each machine contains over 100,000 components that must be precisely calibrated to generate consistent EUV light.  
- ASML holds a de‑facto monopoly on EUV lithography, with each system priced above $120 million and a market cap exceeding $400 billion.

## Photolithography Basics
- Photolithography transfers circuit patterns onto a silicon wafer using light, avoiding direct contact that could damage the wafer.  
- Process steps: coat wafer with photoresist → expose through a patterned mask → develop to remove exposed areas → etch the silicon → fill with metal interconnects.  
- Shorter wavelengths diffract less, enabling finer patterning. Early systems used mercury‑vapor lamps; later systems adopted argon‑fluorine lasers at 193 nm, achieving 22 nm features by 2010.

## Extreme Ultraviolet Lithography (EUV)
- EUV uses light at a wavelength of 13.5 nm, generated by vaporising a tin droplet into a hot plasma that emits EUV photons.  
- Because virtually all solid materials absorb EUV, the system relies on a series of ultra‑smooth, concave mirrors rather than lenses.  
- The light is reflected off a mask (reticle) that holds a larger version of the desired pattern; successive mirror groups demagnify the pattern fourfold before it reaches the wafer.  
- EUV enables single‑exposure patterning of features as small as 7 nm, collapsing what previously required three or four separate lithography steps into one.  
- Without EUV, producing a 5‑nm node would demand roughly 100 individual processing steps.

## The Shrinking Act Explained
- Longer wavelengths act like blunt tools, requiring multiple exposures to approximate fine details, which introduces blur and complexity.  
- EUV’s short wavelength behaves like a fine chisel, delivering sharp, accurate patterns in a single exposure.  
- This efficiency reduces the number of process steps, lowers defect risk, and accelerates the transition to smaller nodes.

## Historical Development of ASML
- Originated within Philips in the 1970s, when lithography used >400 nm wavelengths for 1 µm features.  
- Philips invested in optics and precision mechanics to develop its own lithography prototype but faced escalating costs (≈ $280 million in today’s dollars) and technical hurdles by the early 1980s.  
- Overcoming these challenges required unconventional strategies for a European firm: close collaboration with the U.S. government, selling stakes to foreign partners, and betting heavily on the unproven EUV technology.  
- These moves ultimately transformed ASML from a laggard into the indispensable chokepoint of the global semiconductor supply chain.
