# DoW Contracting for Startups 101 – Summary (April 2026)

## Introduction
- Updated primer reflects statutory and regulatory reforms enacted in late 2025.  
- Core framework for selling to the Department of War (DoW) remains evergreen.  
- DoW offers massive, stable spending and long‑term sustainment, but sales cycles are long and compliance is strict.  
- Success requires a strategic, patient mindset and early engagement with DoW sponsors and congressional stakeholders.

## How the DoW Buys Technology Today
- DoW follows the PPBE budgeting process (Planning, Programming, Budgeting, Execution).  
- The Future Years Defense Program (FYDP) is a rolling five‑year plan; each service submits a Program Objective Memorandum (POM) that must be approved by Congress via the NDAA and Defense Appropriations Bill.  
- Failure to pass a full appropriations bill triggers a Continuing Resolution (CR), limiting new initiatives and often extending for 2–6 months.  
- Understanding the “colors of money” (funding categories) is essential for aligning a startup’s technology with the appropriate budget line.

## Funding Categories (“Colors of Money”)
- **RDT&E (Research, Development, Test & Evaluation)** – primary entry point for startups.  
  - Divided into eight Budget Activities (BA):  
    - BA 3 – Advanced Technology Development (common SBIR funding).  
    - BA 4 – Prototyping.  
    - BA 5 – System Development & Demonstration.  
    - BA 6 – Management/contracting overhead.  
    - BA 7 (6.7) – Operational system improvement (largest RDT&E volume).  
- No strict gate between BAs; programs often co‑fund across adjacent activities.  
- Successful progression moves a company from RDT&E into full Procurement contracts.

## Cooperative Research & Development Agreements (CRADAs)
- CRADAs cannot provide direct cash to non‑federal entities but can deliver in‑kind value:  
  - Access to test ranges, facilities, and classified work.  
- Useful for early‑stage validation and building relationships with end‑users.  

## Contracting Pathways: SBIR / STTR
- **SBIR/STTR** provide grant‑based funding for early‑stage defense tech.  
  - Phase I: $50 k–$225 k for feasibility studies.  
  - Phase II: $750 k–$1.5 M for prototype development.  
  - Phase III: Sole‑source contracts that transition to procurement dollars.  
- Direct‑to‑Phase II (D2P2) allows companies with proven feasibility and a DoW stakeholder memo to skip Phase I.  
- SBIR/STTR are “licenses to hunt” – they grant access to DoW program managers but do not guarantee large‑scale contracts.  
- Multiple DoW entities administer SBIR/STTR, each with distinct focus areas:  
  - Air Force/Space Force: AFWERX, SpaceWERX  
  - Army: Army Applications Lab  
  - Navy/Marines: (entity not fully listed in excerpt)  

## Key Takeaways for Startups
- Engage early with both DoW sponsors and congressional staff to influence funding decisions.  
- Target RDT&E (especially BA 3, 4, 5) as the most accessible entry point.  
- Leverage CRADAs for non‑monetary resources and credibility.  
- Use SBIR/STTR to demonstrate feasibility, build stakeholder relationships, and position for Phase III procurement contracts.  
- Prepare for multi‑year sales cycles, rigorous compliance, and the possibility of funding delays due to Continuing Resolutions.
