# Appeals court rules Trump's asylum ban at southern border is illegal

## Key Decision
- A three‑judge panel of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit blocked President Trump’s executive order that suspended asylum access at the southern border.  
- The court held that the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA) guarantees the right to apply for asylum at the border, and the president cannot override that statutory right.

## Background
- On Inauguration Day 2025, Trump declared the border situation an “invasion” and issued an order suspending the physical entry of migrants and their ability to seek asylum until he deemed the invasion over.  
- The order asserted that the INA gave the president authority to suspend entry of any group deemed detrimental to U.S. interests.

## Court Reasoning
- Judge J. Michelle Childs wrote that a presidential proclamation to suspend entry does not contain implicit authority to bypass the INA’s mandatory asylum procedures.  
- The opinion emphasized that the INA’s text, structure, and legislative history show Congress did not intend to grant the executive expansive removal powers.  
- Judge Justin Walker, a Trump nominee, partially dissented: he agreed the president cannot deport migrants to countries where they face persecution, but noted the administration could issue broader denials of asylum applications.

## Government Response
- White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt called the ruling “unsurprising,” blaming politically‑motivated judges and asserting that Trump’s actions are within his presidential powers.  
- The Department of Justice said it will seek further review, potentially before the full appeals court or the Supreme Court.  
- The Department of Homeland Security expressed strong disagreement, reiterating the administration’s focus on screening and vetting all aliens seeking entry.

## Advocacy Reaction
- Aaron Reichlin‑Melnick of the American Immigration Council said the decision confirms that Congress, not the president, mandates the right to apply for asylum.  
- ACLU attorney Lee Gelernt called the ruling “essential” for refugees denied a hearing under the executive order.  
- Nicolas Palazzo of Las Americas Immigrant Advocacy Center welcomed the decision as a victory for the rule of law over capricious presidential actions.

## Impact on Migrants
- The ruling marks another legal defeat for a central component of Trump’s immigration agenda, though advocates note it may not immediately change conditions for migrants on the ground.  
- Josue Martinez, a psychologist working in a Mexican migrant shelter, expressed cautious hope that the decision could lead to more concrete relief for stranded migrants.  
- Migrants from Haiti, Cuba, Venezuela and other countries continue to face severe hardships in Mexico’s overstretched asylum system.

## Next Steps
- The administration may ask the full D.C. Circuit to reconsider the panel’s decision or appeal to the Supreme Court.  
- The injunction does not take effect until any request for reconsideration is resolved.
