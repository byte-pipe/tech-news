# Anthony Albanese accused of ‘caving to gas companies’ as Labor set to reject new export tax

## Main decision
- The federal government is expected to reject a proposed 25 % tax on gas exports in the May budget.  
- The move is linked to the global oil crisis and Prime Minister Anthony Albanese’s diplomatic effort to secure reliable LNG supplies for Asian partners.

## Statements from government officials
- **Prime Minister Anthony Albanese** said gas corporations paid about $22 billion in taxes last year but did not confirm any new export levy.  
- **Trade Minister Don Farrell** stated the government is “not changing our policies in respect to gas” and emphasized the need to honour existing export contracts.  
- **Energy Minister Chris Bowen** highlighted the need to “balance” gas exports and taxes, stressing that Australian gas is vital both domestically and for the region.  
- **Greens leader Larissa Waters** criticised the decision, warning it would allow gas companies to make “obscene wartime profits”.

## Opposition and criticism
- **Senator David Pocock** (independent) accused the government of “caving to gas companies” and argued a flat 25 % export tax is essential to return resource wealth to all Australians.  
- Pocock pointed out that other sectors (builders, bakers, traders) pay for inputs and tax them, whereas gas is being “given away for free”.  
- Labor‑aligned trade unions and cross‑benchers have pushed for the tax; the Australia Institute estimates it could raise $17 billion a year.

## Alternative tax options
- Treasury has modelled reforms to the petroleum resources rent tax and a windfall‑profits tax, but no definitive decision has been announced.  
- These smaller adjustments are being considered as alternatives to a full export levy.

## Context and broader implications
- The debate occurs amid a fuel crisis and rising living costs for ordinary Australians.  
- The Independent Economic Inclusion Advisory Committee has recommended increasing JobSeeker and age‑pension payments; Pocock says revenue from a gas export tax could fund these proposals.  
- The government aims to maintain Australia’s reputation as a reliable gas supplier to overseas markets while managing domestic supply concerns.
