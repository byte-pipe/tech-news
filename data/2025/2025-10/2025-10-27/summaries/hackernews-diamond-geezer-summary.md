# Diamond Geezer: A Commemoration in Barking and the Lessons of Asbestos

### Monday, October 20, 2025

The long-forgotten monument of the Cape Asbestos Company's asbestos factory in Barking has finally surfaced, thanks to a renewed interest sparked by its unveiling on Workers' Memorial Day. This installation serves as a poignant reminder of one of the country's most pernicious environmental hazards, leaving many Barking residents and their families puzzled.

Despite being built over 105 years ago, the facility revealed little about the dangers posed by asbestos. The story begins in 1913 when Cape Asbestos Company established operations on River Roding, exploiting a lucrative source of fibre production in South Africa. Over the course of decades, hundreds of workers toiled without protective gear or safety measures, succumbing prematurely to chronic respiratory disease.

A long-overdue recognition that asbestos poses significant health risks led to its eventual condemnation as a hazardous material around 1985. Yet, for years after this turning point, many residents continued to live in areas contaminated by fine dust emissions emanating from the factory. Locally known colloquially as "the Barking Cough," decades of exposure left a lasting impact on their children and grandchildren.

The company's site eventually came under threat when Cape Asbestos announced its impending closure and replaced it with the Harts Lane council estate, including towering Colne House and Mersey House – now slated for demolition. Rather than remediate or repurpose these structures effectively, concern over potentially disturbed polluted land took precedence. In recent years, efforts to revitalize the site have been thwarted by financial constraints.

On April 2022, the actual day this monument was unveiled in Barking Town Square stands as a poignant reminder of the past. Designed by London Asbestos Support Awareness Group and workers union representatives, it emphasizes remembrance alongside advocacy for better working conditions. The intricate message at its core declares: "Remember the Dead and Fight for the Living." As such, Diamond Geezer has emerged not merely as a historical relic but an urgent call to action in commemoration of lives lost due to hazardous industrial practices.
