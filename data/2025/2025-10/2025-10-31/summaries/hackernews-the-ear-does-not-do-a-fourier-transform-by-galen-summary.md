# The ear does not do a Fourier transform

### Sensory coding 2: electric boogaloo

The cochlea is a complex organ responsible for converting sound waves into electrical signals that our brain can interpret as music and speech. While it may seem like the cochlea has some way to go in terms of its technical capabilities, researchers have made significant progress understanding how this function works – at least, not on the first pass.

## The Tympanic Membrane as Oscillators

- **The Inner Ear Amplifies Vibration**: When sound waves reach the eardrum, they cause vibrations that are transmitted through a fluid-filled structure called the cochlea. This process involves amplifying and transmitting these vibrations to stimulate hair cells in the inner ear.

## The Basilar Membrane and Frequency Separation

- **Vibrations Travel Down the Cochlea**: As sound waves progress down the cochlea, they cause the basilar membrane to vibrate at different frequencies. The difference in frequency resonates with specific parts of the membrane, enabling efficient transmission of sound energy.

## Hair Cells Transduce Vibrations to Electricity

- **Mechanoelectrical Transduction Process**: When a hair cell vibrates due to an incoming sound wave, it interacts with its neighboring cells, causing them to bend along the basilar membrane. This process opens ion channels and triggers neurotransmitter release, ultimately producing electrical signals that our brain interprets as sound.

## Filtering in the Cochlea

- **Filtering the Signal**: Unlike filters found in electronic devices, which can selectively pass or block specific frequencies, the cochlear filters work to separate frequency components from sound. These filters help stabilize signal transmission towards the brain.

## Why Don't We Have a Fourier Transform?

- The traditional Fourier transform relies on temporal precision and separates signals into sinusoidal waves. In contrast, human hearing allows for less temporal specificity, which makes it more adept at capturing complex sounds with varying rates.

## Realistic Models of Human Hearing

While we can simulate filters and sonic behavior in theoretical models, these findings emphasize the inherent flexibility and non-linearity found within the biological mechanisms making up the cochlea.
