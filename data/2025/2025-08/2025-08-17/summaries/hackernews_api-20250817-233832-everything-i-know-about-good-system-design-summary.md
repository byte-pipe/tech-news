# Everything I know about good system design

**System Design Analysis**

The article provides a unique perspective on system design by comparing it to software design. While both involve assembling lines of code, the author believes that system design encompasses the practical aspects of building services rather than just variables, functions, classes, and so forth.

For system designers, recognizing bad design is crucial, as even seemingly complex systems can lead to problems down the line. The author suggests that good design makes you "huh, this ended up being easier than I expected," while bad design often looks impressive at first but requires constant maintenance.

From a technical perspective, the article highlights the importance of distribution, consistency, and simplicity in system design. These factors can lead to elegant solutions, such as CQRS (Command Query Responsibility Segregation), where different responsibilities are handled by separate databases or queues.

**Market Indicators and Technical Feasibility**

The author notes that good system design often requires a significant amount of expertise and time investment. For solo developers, the challenge lies in striking the right balance between complexity and maintainability.

To address this issue, the article emphasizes the importance of:

1. **User adoption**: A complex system must reflect an absence of good design if it is to be used effectively.
2. **Revenue**: A complex system is more likely to generate revenue than a simple one.
3. **Willingness to pay**: Solo developers should consider the willingness of clients to pay for complex solutions.

**Business Viability Signals**

The article identifies several business viability signals for a solo developer business:

1. **Existing competition**: The authors suggest that solo development businesses should aim to outdo existing competitors by providing innovative solutions that meet complex demands.
2. **Distribution channels**: With the proliferation of popular services like GitHub, having an internal API or being able to integrate seamlessly with existing systems can be key to attracting clients.

**Actionable Insights and Specific Numbers**

To build a profitable solo development business, one can:

* Work on projects that are self-contained and have minimal external dependencies.
* Prioritize simplicity over complexity.
* Provide innovative solutions that meet complex demands quickly.
* Focus on integrating seamlessly with existing services.

By understanding the trade-offs involved in system design and being aware of market indicators, technical feasibility pressures, and business viability signals, solo developers can create profitable businesses.
