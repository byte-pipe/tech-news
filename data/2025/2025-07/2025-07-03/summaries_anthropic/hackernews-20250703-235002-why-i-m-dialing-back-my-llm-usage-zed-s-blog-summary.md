# Why I'm Dialing Back My LLM Usage — Zed's Blog

This article provides valuable insights for solo developers considering the use of large language models (LLMs) in their businesses. Here's a 3-4 paragraph analysis from a solo developer business perspective:

The key problem being discussed is the gap between the hype and reality of using LLMs in production software development. The author, Alberto Fortin, initially embraced LLMs with enthusiasm, hoping they would revolutionize his development workflow. However, he encountered significant challenges when rebuilding his infrastructure with Go and ClickHouse, leading him to re-evaluate his reliance on these AI tools.

The article highlights several market indicators that solo developers should consider. Fortin mentions the "productivity illusion" where developers get excited about the initial benefits of LLMs, but then struggle to maintain the quality and stability of the codebase long-term. He also notes that while LLMs have improved productivity by 10x, the expectations have also risen accordingly. This suggests that solo developers need to carefully manage their expectations and not overestimate the capabilities of current LLM technology.

From a technical feasibility perspective, the article suggests that senior developers with a deep understanding of their codebase may be better equipped to leverage LLMs effectively. Fortin emphasizes the importance of maintaining control and not blindly trusting the LLM's outputs, which can sometimes introduce new bugs or make things "more messed up." This requires a significant investment of time and skill to properly integrate and validate LLM-generated code.

In terms of business viability, the article highlights the willingness of developers to pay for tools that can improve their productivity, but also cautions against the hype and unrealistic expectations. Solo developers should carefully evaluate the specific pain points their customers are facing and determine if LLMs can provide a tangible, reliable solution that justifies the investment. The author's experience suggests that LLMs may be more suitable for small-scope tasks like refactoring, rather than large-scale feature development.
