# The Future is NOT Self-Hosted

The article "The Future is NOT Self-Hosted" discusses the problem of digital ownership and control, and presents self-hosting as a potential solution, but ultimately argues against it as the future we should be fighting for. Here's a 3-4 paragraph analysis from a solo developer business perspective:

The problem being discussed is the lack of true ownership and control over our digital assets and data, as companies like Amazon and Google increasingly position themselves as the gatekeepers to the content and information we "own." This represents a significant opportunity for solo developers, as there is a clear user need and pain point around regaining control and ownership of one's digital life.

The article cites several market indicators that point to this problem, including the shift in Kindle's terms to "purchasing licenses" rather than outright ownership of ebooks, as well as the difficulty in migrating data from cloud storage services like Dropbox and Google Drive. The author also mentions the growing "rebel" movement of people looking to take back control, suggesting a willingness to pay for solutions that address this need.

From a technical feasibility standpoint, the author acknowledges that self-hosting is a complex and time-intensive endeavor, requiring significant technical skills and investment. This presents a challenge for solo developers, who may not have the resources or expertise to build and maintain a full-fledged home server infrastructure. However, the article also highlights the existence of open-source alternatives to popular cloud-based apps, suggesting that there may be opportunities for solo developers to create more user-friendly and accessible self-hosting solutions.

In terms of business viability, the article suggests that while self-hosting may address the need for digital ownership and control, it is fundamentally limited by its focus on isolated, independent systems. This could present a challenge in terms of creating vibrant, interconnected communities and sharing resources, which may be important for building a successful solo developer business. The article's emphasis on the inefficiencies and inconveniences of self-hosting also raises questions about the willingness of users to adopt such solutions, particularly if they require significant technical expertise and maintenance.
