# 37 years ago this week, the Morris worm infected 10% of the Internet within 24 hours — worm slithered out and sparked a new era in cybersecurity | Tom...

**The Morris Worm: A Fateful Incident that Changed the Internet**

*   In 1988, Robert Tappan Morris, a Cornell graduate student, released a worm that infected an estimated 10% of the Internet within 24 hours.
*   The worm was designed to gauge the size of the internet but ultimately caused astronomical damage due to its malicious intent.
*   Despite its intended purpose, the Morris worm is often remembered for its unintended consequences.

**The Initial Release and Aftermath**

*   Morris released the worm from his Cornell terminal via an MIT computer in Ithaca, New York.
*   The FBI was eventually notified of the release and found a backdoor exploit and a vulnerability in a network mail system that contributed to its spread.

**Consequences and Cleanup Efforts**

*   The Internet suffered significant slowdowns and messaging delays during this time, with institutions like NASA and Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory affected.
*   Institutions ultimately unplugged their networks for weeks to rid themselves of the worm.
*   Morris received a fine, probation, and 400 hours of community service as an apology.

**The Legacy and Relevance**

*   The Morris worm has been described as one of the first cyberattacks, highlighting the rapid growth and vulnerabilities of the internet at that time.
*   It led to significant reforms in cybersecurity laws, particularly the enactment of the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) in 1986.
