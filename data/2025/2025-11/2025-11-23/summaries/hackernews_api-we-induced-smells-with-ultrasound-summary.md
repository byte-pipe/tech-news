# Inducing Smells with Ultrasound

## Introduction
This experiment aimed to stimulate olfactory processing in humans using focused ultrasound through the skull, effectively "inducing smells" on specific locations of the brain. The target region was identified through the olfactory bulb.

## Methods

### Anatomy
The primary obstacle in achieving effective stimulation was the location of the olfactory bulb within the nasal cavity. A transducer placed above the forehead and directed downward towards the bulb had limited stability, leading to inconsistent ultrasound delivery.

### Ultrasound Technology
A handheld probe initially used for this purpose became unstable with manual holding. Improvements included using a headset designed to improve stability and consistency, which eventually involved attaching a knife to stabilize the device relative to the brain.

## Calibration and Setup
To refine the placement of the transducer, an MRI scan was conducted with the help of Lev's skull image to determine the optimal position for stimulation.

* **Frequency**: 300 kHz, low enough to penetrate the skull well.
* **Angular Direction**: Focused below the forehead and directed downward towards the olfactory bulb.

### Results
The experiment successfully induced various sensations on different locations within the brain, replicating results from human trials conducted on two subjects.

#### Sensations Detected

1. A feeling of fresh air with a lot of oxygen (corresponding to a sense of scent).
2. The smell of garbage, like few-day-old fruit peels.
3. An ozone-like sensation, possibly related to being next to an air ionizer.
4. A campfire smell of burning wood.

#### Limitations

While this experience appears promising, there are certain challenges to consider, including potential interferances from the skull and surrounding tissues.

## Implications and Future Research Directions

Further development and refinement of the experiment could lead to better understandings of olfactory processing and improve upon techniques for more efficient stimulation.
