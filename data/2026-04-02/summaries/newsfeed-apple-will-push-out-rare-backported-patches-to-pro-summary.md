# Apple Will Push Out Rare ‘Backported’ Patches to Protect iOS 18 Users From DarkSword Hacking Tool | WIRED

# Summary of Apple’s Backported Patches for iOS 18 to Mitigate DarkSword

## Background
- Apple traditionally requires users to upgrade to the latest iOS version to receive security fixes.  
- Many iPhone owners remain on older versions for aesthetic or functional reasons, notably iOS 18.  

## DarkSword Threat
- DarkSword is a sophisticated hacking tool that silently compromises iPhones running iOS 18 when they visit a compromised website.  
- The exploit works on devices that could upgrade to iOS 26, which already includes protection.  
- DarkSword has been used in espionage, cryptocurrency theft, and phishing campaigns targeting users in Malaysia, Saudi Arabia, Turkey, Ukraine, and the United States.  
- The code was publicly posted on GitHub, increasing its accessibility to attackers.  

## Apple’s Response
- On Wednesday morning Apple will release an iOS 18 update that backports the DarkSword fix.  
- Devices with auto‑update enabled will receive the patched iOS 18 automatically; others can choose the patched iOS 18 or upgrade to iOS 26.  
- Apple’s statement emphasizes that the backported patch is for “more devices” while still encouraging migration to iOS 26 for the most advanced protections.  

## User Reaction
- Some iPhone users resist iOS 26 because of disliked features such as the “liquid glass” interface, storage constraints, or incompatibility with custom apps.  
- Reddit comments illustrate skepticism, accusing Apple of using the threat to force upgrades.  
- Others criticize Apple’s delay, noting that millions remained vulnerable for weeks.  

## Expert Commentary
- Security researchers (Google, iVerify, Lookout) initially disclosed DarkSword; Apple initially released patches only for older hardware.  
- Patrick Wardle (DoubleYou) called the backport “better‑late‑than‑never” but argued that backporting should be standard for critical fixes.  
- Rocky Cole (iVerify) highlighted practical reasons users stay on iOS 18, such as app compatibility and age‑verification features in iOS 26.  

## Historical Context – Coruna Backport
- Earlier in March, Apple backported patches for iOS 17 to address the Coruna exploitation kit, another advanced iOS attack linked to Russian espionage and profit‑driven criminals.  
- The Coruna incident demonstrated Apple’s willingness to patch older iOS versions when a widespread threat emerges.  

## Implications
- The dual backports (Coruna and DarkSword) suggest a shift in Apple’s patching policy in response to large‑scale, publicly known exploits.  
- The events expose the trade‑off between user preference for older UI/feature sets and the need for timely security updates.  
- Apple’s historical claim that iPhone hacks are rare is challenged by the rapid spread of these toolkits, prompting a reassessment of iOS security posture.
